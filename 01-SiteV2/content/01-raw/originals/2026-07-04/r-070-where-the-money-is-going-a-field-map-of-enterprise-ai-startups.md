# Where the Money Is Going: A Field Map of Enterprise AI Startups

## clean_text

First in a series on the enterprise AI startup ecosystem around SAP, Oracle, and the modern enterprise stack.
Why this map matters now
Two facts frame everything below.
Fact 1. In Q1 2026, foundational AI startups raised approximately $178 billion across just 24 deals. OpenAI alone has crossed $122 billion in cumulative fundraising. Anthropic raised $30 billion in a Series G at a $380 billion post-money valuation. xAI added another $20 billion (Crunchbase, Q1 2026 report; Angel Investors Network analysis).
Fact 2. In the same quarter, enterprise AI platform startups raised in the low hundreds of millions per round. Wonderful raised $150 million. Harvey raised $200 million. Sierra raised $350 million in September 2025.
The ratio is roughly 100 to 1 between model layer and application layer fundraising. Anyone reading those two facts side by side has to ask the obvious question: if the model layer is sucking up that much capital, what is left for everyone else, and where exactly is that capital being deployed?
The answer is the subject of this chapter. Eight categories of enterprise AI startup are absorbing the application-layer capital, each solving a specific operational problem inside large enterprises. Each category has emerging leaders, established pricing models, and a defined relationship to the SAP and Oracle installed base. Together they describe the working surface where founders are building, where VCs are deploying, and where SAP and Oracle Practice Heads should be paying attention.
This is the opening piece of an ongoing series. The goal is not to predict winners. The goal is to map the working surface clearly enough that founders can choose where to build, investors can choose where to deploy, and enterprise leaders can choose where to partner versus where to absorb.
How this series works
This piece opens an ongoing analytical series on the enterprise AI startup ecosystem. Each article publishes as a standalone analysis on LinkedIn and can be read independently. Future pieces in the series will cover founders, capital flow, obstacles, white space, and what makes founders successful. The full series will be compiled into a single downloadable report on completion.
The eight categories
Category 1: Customer Service and Support Agents
This is the most mature enterprise AI category. Two leaders, one clear duopoly pattern.
Sierra crossed $150 million in ARR by January 2026, raised $350 million at a $10 billion valuation in September 2025, and serves roughly 40% of the Fortune 50 (Sacra estimates; AI CERTs News). Resolution rates published by Sierra customers fall in the 64-94% range. In April 2026, Sierra became the first conversational AI platform with Level 1 PCI compliance, enabling end-to-end transactional commerce inside a single conversation.
Decagon sits at roughly $35 million in ARR (October 2025), valued at $1.5 billion, growing at the rate Silicon Valley calls a "war chest fundraise" (Sacra; Upstarts Media). Their pricing model is per-conversation or per-resolution, which lets enterprises forecast spend more predictably than Sierra's pure outcome-based model.
Crescendo rounds out the trio with managed CX operations at roughly $1.25 per resolution.
Pricing in this category has standardized around outcome-based models. The category-defining lesson: in customer service, vendors who charge per successful resolution displace vendors who charge per seat. Salesforce, Zendesk, and ServiceNow are all responding with their own outcome-priced tiers, but the AI-native players are setting the price points.
Relationship to SAP and Oracle: Mostly orthogonal. These platforms integrate with Salesforce, Zendesk, and Intercom via API. SAP CX and Oracle Service Cloud are not the integration targets. Enterprises running SAP for back-office and Sierra for customer service is now a common pattern.
Category 2: AI Agent Platform and Orchestration
This is where the most interesting capital is flowing in 2026, and where the bundle-versus-unbundle dynamic from the SAP-Oracle comparison plays out at the startup layer.
Wonderful raised $150 million Series B in March 2026, led by Insight Partners. The company has deployed production-grade AI agents in telecom, financial services, manufacturing, and healthcare across more than 30 countries within 8 months of launch (Angel Investors Network). The investor pattern here is significant: software investors (Insight Partners) outbidding AI specialists. Wonderful is being valued as a platform company, not a model company.
Orkes raised $60 million Series B for "reliable AI and agentic workflows" — orchestration plumbing for production AI deployments.
Agentin AI (Y Combinator) is explicitly building agents that "move data and take actions across enterprise systems like Salesforce, NetSuite and SAP" — the cross-platform layer that complicates SAP and Oracle's bundle strategies.
The category thesis is consolidating around services-led go-to-market. Wonderful's pitch is not "buy our agent platform." It is "we deploy production agents into your workflows in 8 weeks." That distinction is what enterprise buyers are now paying for.
Relationship to SAP and Oracle: Direct competitive friction. These platforms increasingly position themselves as alternatives to Joule and Fusion's embedded agents. The Indian GSI channel is particularly attentive here — a Practice Head can deploy Wonderful or similar inside a SAP customer engagement and bypass Joule's pricing entirely.
Category 3: Procurement and Source-to-Pay
A direct adjacency to SAP Ariba and Coupa, with a new generation of AI-native entrants.
A London-based procurement orchestration startup raised $50 million Series B in 2025 (Accel, Khosla Ventures, Insight Partners). Cavela raised $6.6 million seed for AI agents that negotiate directly with suppliers. The category is organized around two primitives: agentic supplier negotiation, and predictive sourcing analytics.
The historical pattern matters here. Coupa was the last category-defining startup to win this space, eventually going public in 2016 and being acquired by Thoma Bravo for $8 billion in 2022. The current AI-native entrants are betting that a similar 8-10 year arc plays out, with AI agents as the wedge that incumbents (SAP Ariba, Oracle Procurement Cloud) cannot fully replicate from their platform position.
Relationship to SAP and Oracle: Build-on-top. Most of these startups consume SAP Ariba and Oracle Procurement data via integration, then layer agentic workflows above. This is the classic ISV play, with consumption pricing risks if SAP or Oracle decides to bundle similar agents into their core platforms (which Oracle already has, with the AI Agent Marketplace).
Category 4: Finance and Back-Office Automation
This is the sleeper category, and the most internationally distributed.
LayerX (Bakuraku Suite) in Japan raised $100 million Series B from TCV, MUFG Bank, JAFCO, and others. The company serves 15,000+ customers, has hit $68 million ARR, and offers built-in AI agents for expense reporting, invoice processing, and compliance (Crescendo AI funding tracker).
Resistant AI in Prague raised $25 million Series B with 10x ARR growth since their previous round. Their positioning is AI-native fraud, AML, and document risk detection, increasingly deployed against generative-AI-driven fraud (which is itself a fast-growing problem).
The category competes directly with SAP Concur on expense, with SAP S/4HANA Finance on invoice processing, and with Oracle Fusion Finance on close automation. The wedge is that AI-native players started with autonomy as the design point, while the incumbents are retrofitting agents into seat-based products.
Relationship to SAP and Oracle: Mixed. Some of these companies (LayerX) are building parallel suites that displace SAP/Oracle modules entirely in mid-market. Others (Resistant AI) are building specialized capabilities that integrate with the incumbents. Both patterns work commercially.
Category 5: Master Data Management and Data Platforms
This category just had its defining moment. SAP announced its acquisition of Reltio in March 2026, with close expected in Q2/Q3 2026. The deal validates an entire category of cloud-native, AI-native master data platforms as strategic infrastructure.
For founders, the read is clear: this category has consolidated. The remaining independents are likely either acquisition targets within 24 months or niche players serving specific verticals. Census and Hightouch are adjacent (reverse ETL), but the pure MDM space is narrowing.
Relationship to SAP and Oracle: Acquisition targets. This is the cleanest signal of where SAP is willing to spend M&A dollars in 2026. Oracle has not made an equivalent move, which is itself signal.

Category 6: Software Supply Chain Security
This is the category nobody saw coming until AI coding agents started shipping in volume.
Cloudsmith raised $72 million Series C in April 2026 with the explicit thesis that "as AI coding agents generate more code, faster, the number of artifacts and dependencies explodes, making software supply chain control a bigger problem for engineering and security teams" (Tech Startups).
Socket raised $40 million Series B from a16z, focused on developer-first protection against software supply chain attacks.
This is a second-order AI demand category. The first-order demand is for AI coding tools (Cursor, Codeium, GitHub Copilot). The second-order demand is for the governance, security, and dependency management infrastructure that lets enterprises actually run AI-generated code in production.
Relationship to SAP and Oracle: Adjacent but not directly competitive. These platforms sell to engineering and security organizations, not to ERP buyers. But they are increasingly relevant to SAP customers running BTP extensions and to Oracle customers building on Fusion AI Agent Studio.
Category 7: Vertical AI (Legal, Healthcare, Financial Services)
The breakout category of 2026, with valuations that increasingly rival horizontal AI platforms.
Harvey (legal AI) raised $200 million in March 2026 at an $11 billion valuation, led by GIC and Sequoia Capital. Cambridge Mobile Telematics raised $350 million from The Rise Fund and Allianz X for AI applied to transportation safety.
The vertical AI thesis is straightforward: depth beats breadth in regulated industries. A legal AI platform that knows every Federal Circuit case is more valuable to a law firm than a horizontal AI platform with 80% of the same capability. Same logic applies to medical, financial, and compliance verticals.
Relationship to SAP and Oracle: Mostly parallel. Vertical AI platforms run on AWS, Azure, or Google Cloud, integrate with SAP/Oracle for financial workflows, but are not extensions of either ERP. The exception is healthcare, where Oracle's Cerner acquisition gives it an installed base that vertical healthcare AI startups have to navigate around.
Category 8: Enterprise Search and Knowledge
This category sits above both SAP and Oracle as a unified retrieval and reasoning layer over scattered enterprise data.
Imbue raised $200 million Series B in 2023 with a thesis built specifically around the enterprise knowledge problem: finding information scattered across disconnected systems and executing actions based on the discovered context. Glean is the better-known player in this space with a similar architecture.
The strategic question for these companies is whether the unified enterprise search and knowledge layer is a durable independent business or whether it gets absorbed by the foundation model platforms (OpenAI Enterprise Search, Anthropic, Google) that increasingly offer similar capabilities.
Relationship to SAP and Oracle: Layer above. These platforms ingest from SAP, Oracle, Salesforce, Workday, Slack, Confluence, and others. They sit at the level above where the ERP debate happens. For Practice Heads, this means even if a customer is "SAP-first," their employees may be querying enterprise data through Glean or Imbue rather than through SAP-native search.
What the map tells us
Five takeaways for the audiences this report serves.
For founders
The capital is going to platforms with services-led GTM and to vertical depth in regulated industries. Wonderful's pattern (production deployments in 8 months across 30+ countries) is the new bar for enterprise AI traction. Pure platform plays without deployment evidence are getting harder to fund at scale.
The white space worth examining: any function in finance, procurement, or HR where the SAP or Oracle product still requires multi-week implementation cycles. AI-native entrants are reaching production in weeks, which is a category-killing GTM advantage if execution is consistent.
For investors
The enterprise AI category is consolidating fast, but unevenly. Customer service and support is a clear duopoly (Sierra vs Decagon) with limited room for new entrants at the top. Procurement and back-office finance are still open. Vertical AI is the highest-velocity category for new positioning. Software supply chain security is the second-order demand most underestimated by first-order AI investors.
The investor pattern worth watching: software investors (Insight, Sequoia software practice, Accel) are now systematically outbidding AI-specialist firms on enterprise AI platform deals. This shifts the kind of company that wins funding — toward platforms with measurable enterprise traction, away from technology demonstrations without commercial proof.
For SAP Practice Heads and consultants
Customer service AI is no longer a SAP CX conversation. Sierra and Decagon have the best-in-class deployments, and your enterprise customers are deploying them regardless of their SAP relationship. Practice Heads should know how to integrate these platforms, not how to compete with them.
Finance back-office automation is the most contested adjacency. LayerX in Japan, Resistant AI in Europe, and US-based equivalents are deploying inside SAP Concur and S/4HANA Finance customer environments. Practice Heads should treat these as either partnership opportunities or competitive threats depending on engagement type.
Master data management is now a category SAP has explicitly bought (Reltio). Practice Heads should expect Reltio capabilities to be integrated into Business Data Cloud over the next 12-18 months and plan accordingly.
For Oracle Practice Heads
The 1,000+ Fusion AI agents bundled at no extra cost are a competitive answer to most of these categories. The strategic question is whether enterprises view bundled agents as "good enough" or as "starter kits" that get replaced by best-in-class independents.
The track record so far suggests both patterns are in play. Customers running Fusion CX use the bundled agents for routine tickets and Sierra for high-value resolutions. Customers running Fusion Finance use embedded agents for invoice processing and adjacent platforms for fraud detection.
For CIOs and enterprise leaders
The build-versus-buy-versus-bundle question now has three answers, not two. Build internally is rarely cost-effective for these categories. Buy from a startup gives best-in-class capability with integration cost. Bundle from SAP or Oracle gives lower capability with lower integration cost.
The right answer depends on category criticality. For customer service, where outcome quality directly affects revenue, the buy-from-startup answer is winning. For master data, where integration with the ERP backbone is more important than feature depth, the bundle answer is winning (which is why SAP just bought Reltio rather than letting customers buy independent MDM).

Resistant AI in Prague raised $25 million Series B with 10x ARR growth since their previous round. Their positioning is AI-native fraud, AML, and document risk detection, increasingly deployed against generative-AI-driven fraud (which is itself a fast-growing problem).
The category competes directly with SAP Concur on expense, with SAP S/4HANA Finance on invoice processing, and with Oracle Fusion Finance on close automation. The wedge is that AI-native players started with autonomy as the design point, while the incumbents are retrofitting agents into seat-based products.
Relationship to SAP and Oracle: Mixed. Some of these companies (LayerX) are building parallel suites that displace SAP/Oracle modules entirely in mid-market. Others (Resistant AI) are building specialized capabilities that integrate with the incumbents. Both patterns work commercially.
Category 5: Master Data Management and Data Platforms
This category just had its defining moment. SAP announced its acquisition of Reltio in March 2026, with close expected in Q2/Q3 2026. The deal validates an entire category of cloud-native, AI-native master data platforms as strategic infrastructure.
For founders, the read is clear: this category has consolidated. The remaining independents are likely either acquisition targets within 24 months or niche players serving specific verticals. Census and Hightouch are adjacent (reverse ETL), but the pure MDM space is narrowing.
Relationship to SAP and Oracle: Acquisition targets. This is the cleanest signal of where SAP is willing to spend M&A dollars in 2026. Oracle has not made an equivalent move, which is itself signal.

Category 6: Software Supply Chain Security
This is the category nobody saw coming until AI coding agents started shipping in volume.
Cloudsmith raised $72 million Series C in April 2026 with the explicit thesis that "as AI coding agents generate more code, faster, the number of artifacts and dependencies explodes, making software supply chain control a bigger problem for engineering and security teams" (Tech Startups).
Socket raised $40 million Series B from a16z, focused on developer-first protection against software supply chain attacks.
This is a second-order AI demand category. The first-order demand is for AI coding tools (Cursor, Codeium, GitHub Copilot). The second-order demand is for the governance, security, and dependency management infrastructure that lets enterprises actually run AI-generated code in production.
Relationship to SAP and Oracle: Adjacent but not directly competitive. These platforms sell to engineering and security organizations, not to ERP buyers. But they are increasingly relevant to SAP customers running BTP extensions and to Oracle customers building on Fusion AI Agent Studio.
Category 7: Vertical AI (Legal, Healthcare, Financial Services)
The breakout category of 2026, with valuations that increasingly rival horizontal AI platforms.
Harvey (legal AI) raised $200 million in March 2026 at an $11 billion valuation, led by GIC and Sequoia Capital. Cambridge Mobile Telematics raised $350 million from The Rise Fund and Allianz X for AI applied to transportation safety.
The vertical AI thesis is straightforward: depth beats breadth in regulated industries. A legal AI platform that knows every Federal Circuit case is more valuable to a law firm than a horizontal AI platform with 80% of the same capability. Same logic applies to medical, financial, and compliance verticals.
Relationship to SAP and Oracle: Mostly parallel. Vertical AI platforms run on AWS, Azure, or Google Cloud, integrate with SAP/Oracle for financial workflows, but are not extensions of either ERP. The exception is healthcare, where Oracle's Cerner acquisition gives it an installed base that vertical healthcare AI startups have to navigate around.
Category 8: Enterprise Search and Knowledge
This category sits above both SAP and Oracle as a unified retrieval and reasoning layer over scattered enterprise data.
Imbue raised $200 million Series B in 2023 with a thesis built specifically around the enterprise knowledge problem: finding information scattered across disconnected systems and executing actions based on the discovered context. Glean is the better-known player in this space with a similar architecture.
The strategic question for these companies is whether the unified enterprise search and knowledge layer is a durable independent business or whether it gets absorbed by the foundation model platforms (OpenAI Enterprise Search, Anthropic, Google) that increasingly offer similar capabilities.
Relationship to SAP and Oracle: Layer above. These platforms ingest from SAP, Oracle, Salesforce, Workday, Slack, Confluence, and others. They sit at the level above where the ERP debate happens. For Practice Heads, this means even if a customer is "SAP-first," their employees may be querying enterprise data through Glean or Imbue rather than through SAP-native search.
What the map tells us
Five takeaways for the audiences this report serves.
For founders
The capital is going to platforms with services-led GTM and to vertical depth in regulated industries. Wonderful's pattern (production deployments in 8 months across 30+ countries) is the new bar for enterprise AI traction. Pure platform plays without deployment evidence are getting harder to fund at scale.
The white space worth examining: any function in finance, procurement, or HR where the SAP or Oracle product still requires multi-week implementation cycles. AI-native entrants are reaching production in weeks, which is a category-killing GTM advantage if execution is consistent.
For investors
The enterprise AI category is consolidating fast, but unevenly. Customer service and support is a clear duopoly (Sierra vs Decagon) with limited room for new entrants at the top. Procurement and back-office finance are still open. Vertical AI is the highest-velocity category for new positioning. Software supply chain security is the second-order demand most underestimated by first-order AI investors.
The investor pattern worth watching: software investors (Insight, Sequoia software practice, Accel) are now systematically outbidding AI-specialist firms on enterprise AI platform deals. This shifts the kind of company that wins funding — toward platforms with measurable enterprise traction, away from technology demonstrations without commercial proof.
For SAP Practice Heads and consultants
Customer service AI is no longer a SAP CX conversation. Sierra and Decagon have the best-in-class deployments, and your enterprise customers are deploying them regardless of their SAP relationship. Practice Heads should know how to integrate these platforms, not how to compete with them.
Finance back-office automation is the most contested adjacency. LayerX in Japan, Resistant AI in Europe, and US-based equivalents are deploying inside SAP Concur and S/4HANA Finance customer environments. Practice Heads should treat these as either partnership opportunities or competitive threats depending on engagement type.
Master data management is now a category SAP has explicitly bought (Reltio). Practice Heads should expect Reltio capabilities to be integrated into Business Data Cloud over the next 12-18 months and plan accordingly.
For Oracle Practice Heads
The 1,000+ Fusion AI agents bundled at no extra cost are a competitive answer to most of these categories. The strategic question is whether enterprises view bundled agents as "good enough" or as "starter kits" that get replaced by best-in-class independents.
The track record so far suggests both patterns are in play. Customers running Fusion CX use the bundled agents for routine tickets and Sierra for high-value resolutions. Customers running Fusion Finance use embedded agents for invoice processing and adjacent platforms for fraud detection.
For CIOs and enterprise leaders
The build-versus-buy-versus-bundle question now has three answers, not two. Build internally is rarely cost-effective for these categories. Buy from a startup gives best-in-class capability with integration cost. Bundle from SAP or Oracle gives lower capability with lower integration cost.
The right answer depends on category criticality. For customer service, where outcome quality directly affects revenue, the buy-from-startup answer is winning. For master data, where integration with the ERP backbone is more important than feature depth, the bundle answer is winning (which is why SAP just bought Reltio rather than letting customers buy independent MDM).
