Forward Deployed Engineer is becoming one of the buzziest roles in AI. But what does it actually mean?
Many AI companies are now hiring for roles like AI Forward Deployed Engineer or Forward Deployed AI Engineer. At its core, this role refers to an engineer who is embedded close to the customer or business team to help translate real-world needs into practical, deployed solutions.
Think of it as a blend of:
Software engineer + solution architect + product manager + technical consultant
The best Forward Deployed Engineers are not just strong coders. They need a combination of technical depth, communication skills, business understanding, product mindset, and delivery discipline.
They need to work closely with clients or internal business teams to understand their needs, uncover the friction, define a practical solution, prioritize what will create the most value, communicate complex technology clearly, and challenge ideas that are unrealistic or not aligned with the broader strategy.
In many ways, this is not a new need.
Consulting organizations, enterprise technology teams, digital transformation groups, and non-tech companies have had people playing this role for years, even if they used different titles: solution engineer, implementation engineer, technical product lead, digital transformation lead, or business-facing engineering lead.
So why is the term becoming popular now?
Because agentic AI systems are not plug-and-play.
To build practical AI agents and agentic workflows, companies need engineers who can do more than connect an API. They need people who understand the business process, architect the solution, integrate the data, manage constraints, build trust, and deliver something that actually works in production.
That requires a hybrid skillset.
But as organizations mature, these hybrid roles often become more specialized. We have seen this before. The “software engineer” role eventually evolved into frontend, backend, mobile, data engineering, DevOps, MLOps, platform engineering, and many other specialized disciplines.
I expect something similar to happen with AI deployment.
Today, we may hire a few "AI" Forward Deployed Engineers to sit close to the business and accelerate adoption. But over time, mature organizations will likely build more specialized teams around:
AI solution architecture
Agentic workflow design
Data and integration engineering
AI product management
MLOps and deployment engineering
Governance, monitoring, and evaluation
Change management and adoption
The real question is not whether an organization needs Forward Deployed Engineers.
The real question is:
At what stage of maturity should this be one hybrid role, and when should it become a set of specialized capabilities embedded across the business and engineering organization?
For AI to create real enterprise value, we need both: people who can bridge the gap today, and operating models that can scale that capability tomorrow.

Everyone is asking whether AI will replace consultants, engineers, designers, or managers.
Maybe that’s the wrong question.
A better one is:
Are you using AI as an extension of your expertise, or slowly becoming an extension of it?
As a software engineer, I use AI every day.
ChatGPT helps me draft architecture diagrams.
Claude reviews pull requests.
Cursor autocompletes code.
GitHub Copilot saves me from typing repetitive boilerplate.
They’re genuinely useful.
But here’s what they don’t do.
They don’t join a client call and realize the client is uncertain even though they said “Looks good to me.”
They don’t know that a seemingly simple feature request will introduce months of technical debt.
They don’t know when a startup should ship an imperfect MVP instead of spending three more weeks polishing infrastructure.
They don’t wake up at 2 AM when production is down.
They don’t explain to stakeholders why a deadline slipped.
They don’t take responsibility when a decision turns out to be wrong.
We’ve seen this before.
Excel didn’t replace accountants.
IDEs didn’t replace developers.
AWS didn’t replace architects.
Docker didn’t replace DevOps engineers.
Terraform didn’t replace platform teams.
These tools made experienced professionals significantly more productive.
The same is happening with AI.
A junior developer can now build in days what previously took weeks.
A senior engineer can analyze thousands of lines of code in minutes.
A consultant can prepare strategy options faster.
But context still matters.
Judgment still matters.
Communication still matters.
Ownership still matters.
The best developers I know aren’t avoiding AI.
They’re using it aggressively.
They generate code.
They generate tests.
They summarize documents.
They create diagrams.
They prototype ideas.
And then they apply the one thing AI still lacks:
Experience.
The ability to read the room.
The ability to notice hesitation.
The ability to understand what wasn’t said.
The ability to make the final call and own the consequences.
AI may replace some tasks.
It will definitely reshape many jobs.
But I suspect the biggest winners won’t be the people who ignore AI.
They’ll be the people who combine deep domain expertise with AI leverage.
Use AI as leverage.
Keep ownership of judgment, context, and decisions.
What do you think?
Will AI replace experts, or simply make the best experts even better?

Enterprise AI is a system, not a prompt.
That is the part I think gets missed when companies move from AI experiments into real enterprise adoption.
A prompt can produce an answer. But enterprise teams need more than a one-time response. They need trusted context, repeatable outputs, measurable quality, controlled risk, and integration into the way work actually gets done.
That is where the engineering work begins.
Large Language Models are trained broadly. Enterprise systems are specific.
A model does not automatically understand your business rules, architecture constraints, customer edge cases, deployment history, or production issues. That context has to be captured, structured, and applied.
Some of that happens through in-context learning, where the model is guided at runtime with instructions, examples, constraints, and domain context inside the prompt.
Some of it happens through RAG, where current enterprise knowledge is retrieved from documents, runbooks, APIs, data schemas, tickets, architecture decisions, or knowledge bases before the answer is generated.
Some of it may happen through fine-tuning, especially when the task is narrow, repeatable, and domain-specific enough to justify training the model further.
But the AI pattern alone is not enough.
Senior engineers bring the judgment that makes these approaches useful. We know which context matters. We know where edge cases hide. We know when an answer sounds reasonable but ignores the actual system.
That experience also matters beyond code generation.
AI can help with project planning, phased delivery, Scrum mapping, backlog refinement, requirements analysis, test strategy, runbook generation, incident review, architecture tradeoffs, and modernization planning.
But only when the tool is tuned for the work.
I think of prompting less like asking questions and more like configuring a system.
Define the role.
Set the constraints.
Provide examples.
Specify the output.
Tell the model what not to do.
Tell it not to agree too quickly.
Tell it to challenge weak assumptions, ask clarifying questions, identify risks, and expose dependencies.
That is how AI becomes useful in the enterprise.
Not by replacing senior engineering experience.
By giving that experience a way to scale.

AI will not replace software engineers.
But software that only waits for humans to click buttons is already becoming outdated.
For the last two years, the market obsessed over bigger models, better prompts, and smarter chatbots.
That phase is ending.
The next wave will be built around systems that can observe, decide, act, verify, and improve—not merely generate an answer.
My prediction for the next 3–5 years:
1. Applications will become agentic
Users will stop navigating ten screens to complete one task. They will state an outcome, and coordinated AI agents will execute the workflow across APIs, databases, documents, and business tools.
2. Proprietary context will become more valuable than the model
The same models will be available to everyone. The real advantage will come from clean internal data, reliable retrieval, workflow history, evaluations, and the operational knowledge a company owns.
3. Software engineers will move from “code producers” to system governors
Generating code is getting cheaper. Understanding requirements, designing boundaries, protecting data, evaluating output, handling failure, and taking responsibility for production systems will become more valuable—not less.
4. AI will move from the cloud into the physical world
Edge AI, robotics, digital twins, autonomous inspection, and smart manufacturing will connect intelligence with real environments. The largest opportunities may not be another chatbot—they may be systems that operate factories, hospitals, logistics networks, vehicles, and energy infrastructure.
5. Trust will become a product feature
As agents gain access to money, customer data, infrastructure, and business decisions, security cannot be added later. Permissions, observability, human approval, audit trails, model evaluation, and post-quantum readiness will become part of the architecture.
Here is the uncomfortable truth:
Most companies do not have an AI problem.
They have a systems problem disguised as an AI ambition.
Adding a model to a broken workflow creates a faster broken workflow.
The winners will not be the companies using the most AI tools.
They will be the companies that combine:
AI + proprietary knowledge + reliable engineering + measurable business outcomes.
That is the future I am preparing for as a backend-focused AI engineer: building systems that do not just produce impressive demos, but survive real users, real data, real failures, and real business pressure.
The future of software is not “AI inside every product.”
It is intelligence becoming part of the operating system of every serious business.
What do you think will create the biggest shift: agentic systems, robotics, edge AI, or something the market is still underestimating?

People often say that AI "does not write good code" or that it "messes up the codebase."
That can be true when AI is used as a one-shot code generator.

For some time now, I have been using this kind of workflow with 100% success in the projects and changes where I applied it. The key difference is that the AI assistant is not working in a vacuum. The workflow combines Test-Driven Development with V-cycle traceability: every request is translated into requirements, every requirement is linked to tests, every change is mapped to affected files, and local validation is run iteratively against the tests impacted by those files.
In my current workflow template, the agent moves through explicit gates:
1. Intake: understand the request, inspect the project, map likely affected files.
2. Requirements: turn the request into small, testable behavior statements.
3. Checks: create deterministic tests first and prove they fail.
4. Implementation: change only what is needed and keep the affected-file map current.
5. Validation: use requirement/test/file traceability to run the relevant local tests iteratively, then run broader audits as needed.
6. Delivery: prepare the report and preflight checks before any commit, push, or PR.
The reason this matters is that larger applications need continuity. They need
project memory, requirement-to-test-to-file traceability, scoped changes, regression awareness, documentation updates, and clear human approvals.
That matters even more for legacy systems.
Most legacy applications cannot simply be "AI-generated again." They contain years of business rules, integrations, edge cases, implicit decisions, and production behavior. To make them AI-assisted, the first job is to give the agent a workflow that can read, respect, and gradually improve the existing system.
The important part is not that the AI is "trusted."
The important part is that the workflow does not rely on trust.
It relies on:
- human approval at key decision points;
- requirements before implementation;
- red-before-green tests;
- traceability between requirements, tests, affected files, and evidence;
- iterative local test runs for the files touched by the change;
- scoped write zones and guardrails;
- documentation updates when behavior or components change;
- delivery preflight before publishing.
This changes the role of AI in software development.
Instead of being a code slot machine, the assistant becomes an engineering partner operating inside a process that makes mistakes visible early and keeps the codebase understandable over time.

The question is not "Can AI produce flawed code?"
The question is: "Do we have a workflow that forces every change to earn its way into the codebase?"
