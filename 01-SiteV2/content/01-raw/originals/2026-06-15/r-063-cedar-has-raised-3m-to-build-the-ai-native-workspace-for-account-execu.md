Five operators. Three maturity tiers. One word nobody in the room could stop saying.
Wednesday night GTM+AI Masterclass #5 (NY Tech Week, hosted at Insight Partners ), was so rich with content, frameworks, ideas, and experts that I’m going to share this summary but post a deep dive on most of the speakers to share the full value they got into in this 2 hours or presentations and live demos.
The question on offer was the difference between single-player and the progression to multi-player GTM, what does the shift looks like both across systems and people?
→ Solo — 🦾Eric Nowoslawski (Growth Engine X): Reduced his team by +50% **and they sent more emails and generated more leads.” His recipe for a first AI employee: Context (build the company brain) → Connection (give it access to where work happens) → Creation (one approval loop it learns from).
→ Enterprise — Nikita Bokil (Optimizely / Opal): the same architecture at ~$400M ARR. Connectors are table stakes — everyone has a HubSpot connector — so the real unlock is org-level context. As she put it: “we wrote a skill to guide the LLM — when we say our golden accounts, this is what we mean.”
→ Internal — Kenneth T. (CoverForce): four agents — research, contact selection, sequence, routing — that take a 60,000-person account down to a ranked 12 worth a human’s time.
Different tiers. Identical skeleton: a context store, connectors, agents, and a human gate nobody removed.
The word for that skeleton — which host Nimo Shkedy flagged he “almost asked every speaker not to say, and every one of them said anyway” — is the harness.
That’s the real shift. A year ago we argued about prompts. Now the unit of construction is the harness: the thing that holds your context, your tools, your agents, and your humans together.
The map I used to make sense of it, if useful:
- MOVE ( Sangram Vajre ) — the GTM Operating System; fix the motion before you automate it
- Anthropic’s framing of agents + skills as composable units
- Optimizely’s agents-vs-skills cut — autonomy vs consistency; use both
Single-player was never the ceiling. It was the first rung.

Five operators. Three maturity tiers. One word nobody in the room could stop saying.
Wednesday night GTM+AI Masterclass #5 (NY Tech Week, hosted at Insight Partners ), was so rich with content, frameworks, ideas, and experts that I’m going to share this summary but post a deep dive on most of the speakers to share the full value they got into in this 2 hours or presentations and live demos.
The question on offer was the difference between single-player and the progression to multi-player GTM, what does the shift looks like both across systems and people?
→ Solo — 🦾Eric Nowoslawski (Growth Engine X): Reduced his team by +50% **and they sent more emails and generated more leads.” His recipe for a first AI employee: Context (build the company brain) → Connection (give it access to where work happens) → Creation (one approval loop it learns from).
→ Enterprise — Nikita Bokil (Optimizely / Opal): the same architecture at ~$400M ARR. Connectors are table stakes — everyone has a HubSpot connector — so the real unlock is org-level context. As she put it: “we wrote a skill to guide the LLM — when we say our golden accounts, this is what we mean.”
→ Internal — Kenneth T. (CoverForce): four agents — research, contact selection, sequence, routing — that take a 60,000-person account down to a ranked 12 worth a human’s time.
Different tiers. Identical skeleton: a context store, connectors, agents, and a human gate nobody removed.
The word for that skeleton — which host Nimo Shkedy flagged he “almost asked every speaker not to say, and every one of them said anyway” — is the harness.
That’s the real shift. A year ago we argued about prompts. Now the unit of construction is the harness: the thing that holds your context, your tools, your agents, and your humans together.
The map I used to make sense of it, if useful:
- MOVE ( Sangram Vajre ) — the GTM Operating System; fix the motion before you automate it
- Anthropic’s framing of agents + skills as composable units
- Optimizely’s agents-vs-skills cut — autonomy vs consistency; use both
Single-player was never the ceiling. It was the first rung.
