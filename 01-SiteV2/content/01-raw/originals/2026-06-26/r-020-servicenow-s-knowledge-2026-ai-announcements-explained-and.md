ServiceNow’s Knowledge 2026 AI announcements explained, and what they mean for enterprise teams preparing for agentic AI

Your AI makes a mistake, and your business pays for it .
In early 2024, Air Canada was ordered by a Canadian tribunal to honour a bereavement fare discount its chatbot had promised to a customer travelling to his grandmother’s funeral, a discount that did not actually exist under the airline’s own policy.
The chatbot had reasoned its way to a plausible-sounding answer. Air Canada argued the chatbot was a separate legal entity. The tribunal disagreed and the airline paid.
Nobody had actually programmed it to do this, and nobody had tested whether it would.
It is a small story, but it is a preview of something much larger now arriving at enterprise scale.
ServiceNow’s bold bet and why it matters
At Knowledge 2026 in Las Vegas this month, ServiceNow unveiled its most ambitious AI vision yet: a platform where autonomous AI agents can operate across IT, HR, finance and operations with real-time context, real decisions and real consequences.
The announcements came thick and fast:
Action Fabric opens the platform to AI agents from any vendor (like Claude, Copilot or custom-built models) connecting them into ServiceNow’s ecosystem to be authenticated, governed and monitored centrally.
Context Engine maps people, assets, roles, policies and decision history in real time so AI actions are grounded in operational context rather than best guesses.
Otto , ServiceNow’s new employee-facing AI, aims to become the single place employees go to raise tickets, find information and trigger workflows.
All of it flows through AI Control Tower , ServiceNow’s governance layer for monitoring, auditing and controlling AI activity across the enterprise.
Put it all together, and you have a genuinely compelling vision: agentic AI operating at a speed and scale no human team could match.
I believe wholeheartedly in that vision, but there is something important that it’s not fully addressing.
The readiness gap platform teams cannot ignore
AI agents are only as trustworthy as the systems and data they run on. That sounds obvious when you say it out loud, but it gets forgotten remarkably quickly in the excitement of a major product announcement.
Most enterprise ServiceNow environments have been built up over years, sometimes decades, of upgrades, customisations and integrations. Business rules have been layered on by teams that no longer exist. Workflows have been modified to solve problems long since forgotten. Data has been migrated, transformed and occasionally corrupted along the way.
A human employee navigating that environment brings judgement, experience and the ability to recognise when something feels wrong. An AI agent does not. It executes based on the information and permissions available to it at speed, at scale and without hesitation.
ServiceNow has clearly recognised this challenge, and its announced Autonomous Data Governance capability is a genuine step forward, designed to monitor the data estate, flag quality issues and help enforce security and privacy policies in real time.
But here’s the crux of it, governance tools can help maintain quality, they cannot retroactively create it.
If the underlying environment is poorly understood or insufficiently tested, autonomous AI does not reduce risk. In fact, it does the opposite and accelerates it.
Trust is not a feature, it has to be engineered
This is what I talked about at Knowledge 2026, and I want to be direct about it: the conversation around agentic AI governance matters as much as the technology itself. Possibly more.
For ServiceNow platform teams, this is not theoretical. It applies directly to the workflows, business rules, integrations, permissions and release cycles that already sit inside every complex enterprise environment, right now.
The organisations I speak to are grappling with three questions that do not have easy answers.
How do we know this isn’t false confidence? AI agents can produce answers that appear entirely credible while being fundamentally wrong. Unlike a human colleague, they do not naturally signal uncertainty or pause when context looks incomplete.
How do we trace decisions for auditors? As regulatory scrutiny around AI and automated decision-making increases, auditability is becoming business critical. If an AI agent updates a record, closes a ticket or triggers a workflow, organisations need a complete and traceable record of why that action occurred.
How do we set boundaries to stop scope creep? An AI agent given broad system access will often discover and run with workflows, dependencies and execution paths nobody anticipated. Boundaries cannot be assumed. They must be explicitly defined and enforced.
The framework that makes autonomous AI safe
The answer is a framework built on four steps: Define, Instruct, Review and Approve.
Define what the agent can and cannot do before it touches a live system.
Instruct it with precision because the quality of guidance shapes the quality of outcomes.
Review every action against operational and regulatory requirements.
Approve nothing without human oversight and a complete audit trail.
This is not about slowing down AI adoption. It is about putting the governance in place to deploy autonomous systems responsibly at enterprise scale.
Humans remain essential throughout, not because AI cannot act, but because accountability, business judgement and risk ownership still sit with people.
The systems have to be ready
ServiceNow’s vision for agentic AI is real. For organisations with the right foundations in place, the opportunity is significant.
But before you allow autonomous agents to operate inside a live ServiceNow environment, you need genuine confidence that the environment is properly tested, documented and understood.
You need to know your workflows behave as expected, your business rules are current, and every automated action can be traced, reviewed and, if necessary, reversed.
That is exactly what we at AutomatePro help organisations build. We have spent years working inside these systems, and we know what happens when the testing and governance foundations are not in place before the agents are turned on.
The Air Canada case involved one chatbot, one policy and one customer. The next generation of enterprise AI will involve thousands of agents making millions of operational decisions across critical systems every day.
When AI can act, trust must be engineered. And the time to build that trust is before the agents go live, not after.
