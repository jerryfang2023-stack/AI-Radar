Meet The New Y-Combinator Startups Poised To Change Tech
By Dasha Shunina ,
Contributor.
Forbes contributors publish independent expert analyses and insights.
Dasha Shunina is a San Francisco-based contributor who covers VC

Mar 16, 2026, 11:05am EDT Mar 18, 2026, 02:15pm EDT
Future unicorns
getty
Every few months, a new group of startups arrives in San Francisco to go through the intensive accelerator program run by Y Combinator. Each company receives $500,000 in funding and spends three months working closely with the accelerator’s partners before presenting to investors on Demo Day. The model has helped launch more than 5,000 companies over the past two decades.
Beyond the fundraising moment, each batch often reveals something deeper about where technology is heading. The latest cohort suggests a clear shift: founders are increasingly building products designed not only for human users but for AI agents. From systems that monitor autonomous software to platforms that train AI agents in simulated environments, this generation of startups is quietly constructing the infrastructure layer of an AI-native economy.
The Builders
Who are they — these ambitious founders disrupting the world of innovation?
The startups emerging from this Winter 2026 batch (W26) reflect that shift across multiple industries. Some are building the tools that allow AI agents to operate reliably inside companies, from monitoring autonomous systems to deploying "AI employees" that can run workflows across business software. Others are tackling complex infrastructure problems in sectors such as finance, cybersecurity and biotechnology, where automation has historically been difficult to implement. A number of founders are also venturing beyond software, building hardware and robotics systems designed to capture data from the physical world and automate real-world operations.
Together, these companies offer a glimpse into how founders are redefining the next generation of technology — not simply creating new apps but building the systems that enable machines to work alongside humans.
MORE FOR YOU
The Infrastructure For AI Agents
If SaaS defined the last generation of startups, agent infrastructure may define the next.
As AI agents begin to perform real work inside companies, a new layer of infrastructure is emerging to support them. Several startups in the latest batch are building the systems that allow agents to run workflows, access tools and operate reliably in production environments. Rather than creating new applications for humans, these founders are designing the operating systems that autonomous software will rely on.
The challenge is no longer whether agents can perform tasks. It's whether the systems around them are reliable enough to trust. Startups like Bubble Lab , which routes operational workflows through Slack, and Tensol , which deploys AI employees with their own email addresses, phone numbers and tool access, are attacking the deployment problem from opposite ends — one through the interfaces teams already use, the other through the organizational scaffolding companies need to treat agents like real hires. Together, they reflect a broader recognition that agents fail not because the models are weak, but because the infrastructure around them isn't ready.
That infrastructure gap extends deeper than deployment. Moda is building monitoring and debugging tools for agents — essentially Sentry for autonomous systems — while Compresr is solving the context problem, ensuring agents receive precisely the information they need without the bloat that confuses models and inflates costs. Meanwhile Polymath is taking an even more foundational approach, building simulated environments where agents can rehearse real-world workflows before operating in production. What unites these efforts is a shared conviction: that agents running in the real world need the same reliability guarantees we’ve always demanded from critical software.
AI Moves Into Regulated Industries
Productivity tools were the easy first act. The harder — and potentially larger — opportunity is in industries where automation has historically been too risky to attempt.
Founders in this batch are targeting banking, cybersecurity, compliance and fraud prevention, sectors where the cost of a mistake isn't a bad user experience but a regulatory penalty or a financial loss. Fenrock AI is automating the back-office workflows that keep banks buried in manual documentation, compressing loan processing from months to minutes while maintaining the audit trails regulators require. Corelayer is doing something similar for engineering operations, deploying AI on-call engineers that diagnose and resolve production incidents before human teams are even paged. What both companies understand is that trust, not capability, is the real product in regulated markets — and that building for institutional accountability is itself a competitive moat.
The same logic applies to the companies working on the threat side of the equation. Hex approaches cybersecurity the way attackers do, continuously probing systems for vulnerabilities rather than waiting for breaches to reveal them. Veriad AI monitors marketing content for compliance risks at the speed AI now generates it. And Beesafe AI is tackling a problem that AI itself created: the explosion of sophisticated, automated scams that impersonate trusted contacts at scale. Across all of them, the design challenge is identical — building systems that institutions can audit, explain and defend, not just systems that work.
Data Becomes the New Battleground
In the AI economy, the models often get the credit, but the data infrastructure underneath them is where the real leverage lives. Several companies in this batch are building the collection, organization and generation systems that modern AI applications depend on — and their work points to a structural gap that the industry has largely papered over.
The problem is that most AI systems are only as good as the data they can access, and most data is still siloed, unstructured or simply missing. VOYGR and LIBRAR Labs are attacking this from very different domains — one building real-time place data infrastructure so AI agents can reason about physical locations, the other organizing literary and knowledge datasets so they're actually usable by AI systems — but both are solving the same underlying problem: data that exists in the world isn't automatically data that machines can use.
Their efforts are more complementary than they might appear. VOYGR’s infrastructure enables AI to act on the physical world in real time; LIBRAR Labs enables AI to reason from accumulated human knowledge. One is about context in space, the other about context in meaning. Together they illustrate a broader pattern in this batch — that the next wave of AI capability won't come from better models alone, but from better-organized inputs. Shofo AI is building that layer for video, Axion for satellite imagery, and Strand AI for the biological measurements that pharmaceutical research depends on but rarely has. What connects them is a shared belief that generating or structuring the right data is often more valuable than building the model that consumes it.
Hardware Makes A Comeback
Software has dominated recent startup cycles, but a growing number of founders are returning to hardware to solve problems that code alone can't reach.
The companies in this batch building physical products share a common thesis: that as AI becomes more capable, the bottleneck shifts to the real world. Pocket is building a dedicated device for capturing conversations — not because the transcription problem is unsolved, but because smartphones are unreliable enough in real meetings that professionals still default to pen and paper. Fort is developing a wearable that tracks strength training automatically, filling a gap that fitness technology has ignored while obsessing over cardio. And RoboDock is tackling one of the quiet contradictions of the autonomous vehicle industry: fleets of self-driving cars that still require humans to walk the depot plugging in chargers. As AV fleets scale into the millions, that model breaks. RoboDock automates the physical infrastructure so the autonomy promise actually holds end to end.
Moonshots And Frontier Technologies
While many startups focus on practical AI applications, others are aiming far beyond Earth — literally.
GRU Space is developing the construction and habitation infrastructure needed for long-term human presence on the Moon, turning lunar materials into building resources rather than waiting for Earth to supply them. Constellation Space is building AI-powered traffic control for satellite networks, predicting link failures and rerouting data in seconds as low Earth orbit becomes increasingly crowded. And Aemon AI is attempting something more fundamental still — automating parts of the scientific discovery process itself, letting AI search solution spaces and test hypotheses faster than human research teams can. These are long bets. But they reflect something consistent across Y Combinator’s best batches: the willingness to treat the most important unsolved problems not as too hard to touch, but as too important to ignore.
The Stories Behind the Builders
GRU Space — Moon Infrastructure
"SpaceX is solving transportation. We're solving where people live once they get there," says founder Skyler Chan.
Chan, who has been fascinated with space since childhood, founded GRU to tackle one of the biggest barriers to becoming an interplanetary species: sustaining human life beyond Earth. The company is building infrastructure designed for long-term habitation on the Moon, starting with technologies that convert lunar materials into construction resources and deploy inflatable habitats.
GRU has also attracted leading talent in the field, including Dr. Kevin Cannon, a scientist known for pioneering lunar regolith simulant chemistry used by both NASA and SpaceX. Cannon left a professorship at the Colorado School of Mines and turned down an offer from Blue Origin to join GRU. While the company's first project is a Moon hotel, Chan sees it as only the beginning. "The real play is building the infrastructure layer for permanent human presence beyond Earth," he says. “The lunar economy doesn't exist yet. We want to build the first economic reason to be there.”
Aemon AI — AI Doing Scientific Research
"We came in building a tool. We're leaving understanding we're building the R&D engine for the next era of companies," says cofounder Richard Zhou, reflecting on the company's evolution during the Y Combinator program.
Aemon AI is developing systems designed to automate scientific discovery by exploring large solution spaces and testing ideas far faster than human teams alone. The turning point came when the founders ran their system on a notoriously difficult circle-packing problem.
"It broke the standing world record in under 15 iterations, at roughly $7 of API cost," Zhou says. "At that moment the question stopped being whether AI could keep up with human researchers and became something more interesting: when will AI become the default way companies conduct research?"
The company was founded by twins Richard and Ray Zhou, who deliberately pursued complementary paths before starting the company — Ray focusing on AI research at the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign and Richard studying applied engineering and entrepreneurship at the University of Waterloo.
Pocket — AI Hardware
"We saw that if you remove even one small friction point, like having to unlock your phone to start recording, behavior changes dramatically," says cofounder Gabriel Dymowski.
Pocket is building a dedicated AI device designed to capture conversations with a single button and turn them into summaries, action items and searchable records. In early testing, the founders discovered that eliminating the simple step of unlocking a phone increased recording activity roughly tenfold.
"Removing that one step made people record around 10 times more than they would on their phone," Dymowski says. "That convinced us that convenience beats intention and that the winning product would be a reliable dedicated device, not just another app."
The company was founded by Akshay Narisetti and Dymowski, who say persistence played a role in their journey to Y Combinator. "We got in on our third attempt, and honestly, I think that was part of the value," Narisetti says. "YC rejects you just enough to build the kind of resilience you'll need anyway, because the real world will say no far more often than any accelerator ever will."
Corelayer — AI On-Call Engineers
Before founding Corelayer, the team built petabyte-scale data infrastructure at Goldman Sachs, where they repeatedly encountered the same operational challenge: production systems breaking at inconvenient hours.
"Our lives were frequently disrupted by 5 a.m. weekend calls — whether we were traveling or meeting a significant other's parents for the first time — only to find that 90% of the time the fix was simple," the founders say. Those experiences led them to experiment with early large language models and eventually build Corelayer, a platform designed to automate production support and act as an AI on-call engineer for enterprise systems.
The company was founded by Shipra Jhahirani and Mitch Radhuber, who say the pace of the Y Combinator program reinforced their approach to building quickly. "YC forces your company to reach its top operating speed," Jhahirani says. "It isn't just about working long hours; it's about setting aggressive goals and ruthlessly prioritizing the tasks that actually move the needle for the business."
VOYGR — Maps For AI Agents
"Ask any map app about a restaurant and you'll get '4.2 stars, open till 10.' What it won't tell you is that the chef left, wait times doubled and locals stopped going," says cofounder Vlad Baskakov. "AI assistants will even recommend restaurants that closed a year ago." VOYGR is building a new generation of mapping infrastructure designed specifically for AI agents, providing richer and more reliable place data that autonomous systems can use to reason and take action in the real world.
The company was founded by Baskakov and Yarik Markov, who approached the problem from complementary backgrounds. Baskakov previously led product strategy for places and local advertising at Google Maps, while Markov spent more than a decade building AI and search systems that depended on accurate location data. As AI assistants began sending users to outdated or incorrect locations, the founders concluded the issue was not simply poor data quality but the absence of a dedicated infrastructure layer built for AI-driven applications.
Axion — Satellite AI
The company's path to Y Combinator was not straightforward. After publishing an early research breakthrough, cofounder Atharva Peshkar and his team applied to YC but were initially rejected because partners felt the model was not yet strong enough. The founders borrowed money from a YC alumnus, rebuilt the model from scratch using a new experimental approach and dramatically improved the results within a week. They contacted YC again over Thanksgiving, secured a second interview and were ultimately accepted into the program.
"So yes, technically it was our first attempt, but emotionally it felt like our second," the cofounders explain.
Fenrock AI — AI For Banks
"We help banks process loans in minutes instead of months," says cofounder Charu Sharma.
The company entered the Y Combinator program focused primarily on financial crime prevention but expanded its vision after working closely with banks and adjacent sectors such as fintech, private credit and money services businesses. Through rapid customer conversations and targeted experiments, the founders concluded that financial institutions were not looking for isolated tools but for a unified system capable of managing multiple regulated workflows.
"Banks don't want point solutions," Sharma says. "They want one AI platform that runs their back-office operations."
RoboDock — Robots For Autonomous Fleets
"Autonomous vehicles can drive themselves, but they still can't operate themselves. The vehicles are autonomous, but the infrastructure that supports them isn't," the founders explain.
The startup was founded by Celine Wang and Zinny Weli, two engineers with deep experience in robotics and autonomous systems. Reflecting on the pace of the Y Combinator program, the founders say the environment pushed them to move faster. "The batch has been amazing — everyone is moving ridiculously fast," they say. "Being surrounded by that level of ambition and speed is extremely motivating."
Inside the YC Machine
For many founders, the experience of going through Y Combinator remains a defining moment in building their companies. The three-month program brings startups to San Francisco, where they work closely with partners and peers before presenting to investors on Demo Day. “Getting into YC felt like fulfilling a childhood dream,” says Ivan Zakazov of Compresr. “You get mentorship from founders who have already built successful companies and spend time with batchmates — some of whom will statistically become unicorns. Being in San Francisco, the center of the AI revolution, makes the experience even more powerful.”
For other founders, YC has long represented the starting point for turning an idea into a company. “The first thought we had after coming up with the idea was to apply to YC,” says Dylan Ma. “In the back of our minds, we always saw it as the place to start a startup.” But the path to acceptance is rarely straightforward.

Constellation Space is building AI-powered traffic control for satellite networks, predicting link failures and rerouting data in seconds as low Earth orbit becomes increasingly crowded. And Aemon AI is attempting something more fundamental still — automating parts of the scientific discovery process itself, letting AI search solution spaces and test hypotheses faster than human research teams can. These are long bets. But they reflect something consistent across Y Combinator’s best batches: the willingness to treat the most important unsolved problems not as too hard to touch, but as too important to ignore.
The Stories Behind the Builders
GRU Space — Moon Infrastructure
"SpaceX is solving transportation. We're solving where people live once they get there," says founder Skyler Chan.
Chan, who has been fascinated with space since childhood, founded GRU to tackle one of the biggest barriers to becoming an interplanetary species: sustaining human life beyond Earth. The company is building infrastructure designed for long-term habitation on the Moon, starting with technologies that convert lunar materials into construction resources and deploy inflatable habitats.
GRU has also attracted leading talent in the field, including Dr. Kevin Cannon, a scientist known for pioneering lunar regolith simulant chemistry used by both NASA and SpaceX. Cannon left a professorship at the Colorado School of Mines and turned down an offer from Blue Origin to join GRU. While the company's first project is a Moon hotel, Chan sees it as only the beginning. "The real play is building the infrastructure layer for permanent human presence beyond Earth," he says. “The lunar economy doesn't exist yet. We want to build the first economic reason to be there.”
Aemon AI — AI Doing Scientific Research
"We came in building a tool. We're leaving understanding we're building the R&D engine for the next era of companies," says cofounder Richard Zhou, reflecting on the company's evolution during the Y Combinator program.
Aemon AI is developing systems designed to automate scientific discovery by exploring large solution spaces and testing ideas far faster than human teams alone. The turning point came when the founders ran their system on a notoriously difficult circle-packing problem.
"It broke the standing world record in under 15 iterations, at roughly $7 of API cost," Zhou says. "At that moment the question stopped being whether AI could keep up with human researchers and became something more interesting: when will AI become the default way companies conduct research?"
The company was founded by twins Richard and Ray Zhou, who deliberately pursued complementary paths before starting the company — Ray focusing on AI research at the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign and Richard studying applied engineering and entrepreneurship at the University of Waterloo.
Pocket — AI Hardware
"We saw that if you remove even one small friction point, like having to unlock your phone to start recording, behavior changes dramatically," says cofounder Gabriel Dymowski.
Pocket is building a dedicated AI device designed to capture conversations with a single button and turn them into summaries, action items and searchable records. In early testing, the founders discovered that eliminating the simple step of unlocking a phone increased recording activity roughly tenfold.
"Removing that one step made people record around 10 times more than they would on their phone," Dymowski says. "That convinced us that convenience beats intention and that the winning product would be a reliable dedicated device, not just another app."
The company was founded by Akshay Narisetti and Dymowski, who say persistence played a role in their journey to Y Combinator. "We got in on our third attempt, and honestly, I think that was part of the value," Narisetti says. "YC rejects you just enough to build the kind of resilience you'll need anyway, because the real world will say no far more often than any accelerator ever will."
Corelayer — AI On-Call Engineers
Before founding Corelayer, the team built petabyte-scale data infrastructure at Goldman Sachs, where they repeatedly encountered the same operational challenge: production systems breaking at inconvenient hours.
"Our lives were frequently disrupted by 5 a.m. weekend calls — whether we were traveling or meeting a significant other's parents for the first time — only to find that 90% of the time the fix was simple," the founders say. Those experiences led them to experiment with early large language models and eventually build Corelayer, a platform designed to automate production support and act as an AI on-call engineer for enterprise systems.
The company was founded by Shipra Jhahirani and Mitch Radhuber, who say the pace of the Y Combinator program reinforced their approach to building quickly. "YC forces your company to reach its top operating speed," Jhahirani says. "It isn't just about working long hours; it's about setting aggressive goals and ruthlessly prioritizing the tasks that actually move the needle for the business."
VOYGR — Maps For AI Agents
"Ask any map app about a restaurant and you'll get '4.2 stars, open till 10.' What it won't tell you is that the chef left, wait times doubled and locals stopped going," says cofounder Vlad Baskakov. "AI assistants will even recommend restaurants that closed a year ago." VOYGR is building a new generation of mapping infrastructure designed specifically for AI agents, providing richer and more reliable place data that autonomous systems can use to reason and take action in the real world.
The company was founded by Baskakov and Yarik Markov, who approached the problem from complementary backgrounds. Baskakov previously led product strategy for places and local advertising at Google Maps, while Markov spent more than a decade building AI and search systems that depended on accurate location data. As AI assistants began sending users to outdated or incorrect locations, the founders concluded the issue was not simply poor data quality but the absence of a dedicated infrastructure layer built for AI-driven applications.
Axion — Satellite AI
The company's path to Y Combinator was not straightforward. After publishing an early research breakthrough, cofounder Atharva Peshkar and his team applied to YC but were initially rejected because partners felt the model was not yet strong enough. The founders borrowed money from a YC alumnus, rebuilt the model from scratch using a new experimental approach and dramatically improved the results within a week. They contacted YC again over Thanksgiving, secured a second interview and were ultimately accepted into the program.
"So yes, technically it was our first attempt, but emotionally it felt like our second," the cofounders explain.
Fenrock AI — AI For Banks
"We help banks process loans in minutes instead of months," says cofounder Charu Sharma.
The company entered the Y Combinator program focused primarily on financial crime prevention but expanded its vision after working closely with banks and adjacent sectors such as fintech, private credit and money services businesses. Through rapid customer conversations and targeted experiments, the founders concluded that financial institutions were not looking for isolated tools but for a unified system capable of managing multiple regulated workflows.
"Banks don't want point solutions," Sharma says. "They want one AI platform that runs their back-office operations."
RoboDock — Robots For Autonomous Fleets
"Autonomous vehicles can drive themselves, but they still can't operate themselves. The vehicles are autonomous, but the infrastructure that supports them isn't," the founders explain.
The startup was founded by Celine Wang and Zinny Weli, two engineers with deep experience in robotics and autonomous systems. Reflecting on the pace of the Y Combinator program, the founders say the environment pushed them to move faster. "The batch has been amazing — everyone is moving ridiculously fast," they say. "Being surrounded by that level of ambition and speed is extremely motivating."
Inside the YC Machine
For many founders, the experience of going through Y Combinator remains a defining moment in building their companies. The three-month program brings startups to San Francisco, where they work closely with partners and peers before presenting to investors on Demo Day. “Getting into YC felt like fulfilling a childhood dream,” says Ivan Zakazov of Compresr. “You get mentorship from founders who have already built successful companies and spend time with batchmates — some of whom will statistically become unicorns. Being in San Francisco, the center of the AI revolution, makes the experience even more powerful.”
For other founders, YC has long represented the starting point for turning an idea into a company. “The first thought we had after coming up with the idea was to apply to YC,” says Dylan Ma. “In the back of our minds, we always saw it as the place to start a startup.” But the path to acceptance is rarely straightforward.
Selina Li says her team applied multiple times before being accepted. “Each rejection forced us to refine our thinking and build something stronger,” she says. “By the time we got in, we had much more conviction about the problem we were solving. YC rewards builders who keep going.”
The Takeaway
Taken together, the companies emerging from this batch illustrate how the startup landscape is evolving. Rather than building consumer apps or incremental productivity tools, many founders are focusing on deeper infrastructure — systems designed to support AI agents, power scientific discovery or automate complex real-world operations. If the last generation of startups built software for people, this one increasingly appears to be building the systems that machines themselves will rely on. And as with many previous cohorts from Y Combinator, the long-term impact of these companies may not become fully visible until years after Demo Day.
