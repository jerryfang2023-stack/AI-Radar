# InstaLILY raises $60 million to put AI agents inside construction and logistics firms - Startup Fortune

InstaLILY raises $60 million to put AI agents inside construction and logistics firms
InstaLILY closed a $60 million Series B led by Energize Capital, with Home Depot Ventures and United Rentals joining as strategic investors after seeing its AI agents inside their own operations. The round, which pushes total funding toward $100 million, comes with a launch of Lily, an AI 'Forward Deployed Engineer,' and a reported $200 million revenue result at one industrial distributor.

InstaLILY has raised $60 million because industrial companies are buying a very specific promise: AI agents that can find revenue and fix operations inside businesses built on parts, rentals and delivery routes.
A national industrial distributor added more than $200 million in new annual sales after handing sales prospecting to software built by InstaLILY. That's the number selling the pitch. No demo could do it better. It explains why Energize Capital led a $60 million Series B into the company behind it.
InstaLILY, founded in New York in 2023 by Amit Shah and Sumantro Das, announced the round alongside the launch of Lily, which the company calls the world's first AI "Forward Deployed Engineer." Insight Partners increased its existing stake, while Home Depot Ventures and United Rentals joined as strategic investors. Those names matter. They work in the physical economy InstaLILY is chasing: construction supplies, equipment rental, industrial distribution, logistics and field operations.
The company says the new capital brings its total funding to nearly $100 million and that revenue grew five times over the past year. Those are company-disclosed figures, so you should read them as claims from InstaLILY, not independent audited results. But they still tell you what investors are underwriting. This isn't a chatbot story. It's a wager that AI labor can sit inside old, complicated businesses and produce measurable sales or operational gains.
Here's what makes InstaLILY different from the wave of AI coding assistants and chatbots flooding the market. Lily doesn't just answer questions or write code on request. It embeds inside a business, learns how that operation works, writes the software the business needs, and goes live within days rather than the months a typical enterprise software rollout can take. Then it stays. Unlike a consultant or contractor who ships a project and leaves, Lily keeps adjusting the software as the business changes, according to the company's description of the product.
The industrial customer list is the real pitch
InstaLILY calls its agents "InstaWorkers," and it has deliberately aimed them at industries that rarely get AI headlines. Since its Series A, the company says it has gone live inside SRS Distribution, part of the Home Depot family, along with United Rentals, ShipStation Global, PartsTown, Radwell International, Henry Schein, PartsSource and Kedrion Biopharma.
That roster is more useful than another abstract claim about AI agents changing work. SRS Distribution sells roofing and building products. United Rentals runs a fleet of rental equipment. PartsTown sells replacement parts. These are not businesses where a neat software demo tells the whole story. The useful work is buried in customer histories, product catalogs, service calls, quotes, warehouse availability and delivery constraints.
Frankly, that's why the $200 million distributor result is worth sitting with. Sales teams at industrial distributors can only chase so many accounts by hand. Lily's software reportedly worked across the distributor's customer base for revenue opportunities a human sales force would not reach. That's a scale problem. AI is well suited to solve it when the task is pattern matching against known customers and a known catalog, not making judgment calls with context the system doesn't have.
Home Depot Ventures and United Rentals didn't just buy the product. They invested in the company that built it. When a corporate venture arm writes a check into a vendor already running inside its own world, that's a different signal from a strategic investment made from the sidelines. It says the technology has survived contact with a real supply chain.
AI agents need proof, not theater
The broader argument around AI agents replacing knowledge workers has mostly run on demos and vendor promises, propped up now and then by a benchmark score. InstaLILY's raise arrives with something more concrete: a named industrial use case and a disclosed revenue figure, backed by strategic investors tied to the customer base the company wants to serve. That's different. A chatbot transcript is easy to stage. A distributor finding hundreds of millions of dollars in annual sales is harder to dismiss, even if you still need to treat the figure as company-reported.
The money is also moving in a clear direction. Not every AI dollar is chasing a general-purpose model or a consumer app. Energize Capital, Insight Partners, Home Depot Ventures and United Rentals are betting on vertical AI labor for businesses where the work is quoting parts, planning delivery routes, diagnosing broken equipment in the field and finding customers who should have been called months ago.
That doesn't make the outcome guaranteed. Enterprise software history is full of tools that looked strong in early deployments and became harder to manage as customers multiplied. InstaLILY now has to prove Lily can adapt across messy operations without becoming another expensive implementation project with a better interface.
For founders watching this market, the point is plain. The AI agent companies that win won't be the ones with the broadest slogans. They will be the ones that can name the customer, name the workflow and show the number that changed after the software went live. InstaLILY has one of those numbers. Now it has $60 million to prove it can keep finding more.
