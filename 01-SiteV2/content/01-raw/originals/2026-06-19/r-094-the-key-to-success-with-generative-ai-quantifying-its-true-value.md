# The Key to Success With Generative AI? Quantifying Its True Value

## clean_text

By Malcolm deMayo, Global Vice President, Financial Services Industry, NVIDIA
Generative AI has propelled industries, and by extension the entire world, to a pivotal moment. This groundbreaking branch of artificial intelligence, made possible by accelerated computing, is now enabling enterprise leaders to drive innovation, improve decision-making, boost efficiency and gain competitive advantages at unprecedented scale and velocity.
A year after generative AI applications became readily available to the public, McKinsey projects generative AI could add $2.6 trillion to $4.4 trillion in economic value annually across industries including financial services, retail and technology. 1
The same report estimates doubling those projections if you include embedding generative AI in software that is already being used for other tasks.
Even so, some organizations are concerned about compounding current investments with generative AI. Financial services was one of the earliest industries to embrace generative AI. We’ll use it as an example for describing considerations for quantifying the true value of AI.
Strategic Experimentation
Pilot programs are a great way for organizations to assess and implement generative AI in a controlled manner. They provide room to make necessary adjustments before scaling up. Starting with a pilot program also provides a solid foundation to demonstrate preliminary results and real value to stakeholders.
NVIDIA’s annual State of AI in Financial Services report indicates that 60% of financial services professionals believe generative AI is the most important AI and machine learning workflow contributing to their company’s productivity. As the AI landscape has become increasingly complex and data-intensive, several financial institutions have launched pilots focused on generative AI-powered automation to enhance operational efficiency, accuracy and the customer experience.
Generative AI is now enabling enterprise leaders of the modern enterprise to drive innovation, improve decision-making, boost efficiency and gain competitive advantages.
Their thinking goes something like this: If the average customer service contact requiring human intervention costs approximately $3 to $6 , the goal of implementing an AI-powered automation pilot might be solely to reduce operational costs. This can lead to significant cost savings, enhanced productivity and faster service delivery. Automating the majority of standard customer contacts that require human intervention can lead to cost savings of 97%. This achievement alone can be considered a highly successful outcome, and a very good end to the story—if it ended here.
Re-quantifying the Value by Connecting the Dots
The same AI models that make automation possible can create a knock-on effect beyond customer service. It can reduce human error and improve turnaround times, productivity and customer satisfaction. It enables real-time data analysis as well as valuable insights for decision-making and risk management. Additionally, it enhances regulatory compliance and drives operational excellence, fostering growth at scale.
However, in the pilot example, implementing AI-powered automation within customer service requires enhanced security—utilizing optimized, pretrained AI models developed to identify fraudulent transactions or activity. When presenting the results of this pilot, someone might say they successfully reduced the cost per custom contact from an average of $3 to 20 cents , but they would fail to capture the effects of the critical and enhanced security measures that come with it.
Consider a real-life scenario of a leading credit card company.
Here is a snapshot of quarterly activity “before,” or prior to utilizing pretrained AI models to identify fraudulent activity:
After utilizing pretrained models here, the company saw a 10% reduction in fraud, which would reduce fraudulent payments by $1.36 million annually, and a 1,360% increase in return on investment.
This is just part of the story. The effects of a generative AI-powered fraud-detection pilot program like this would reverberate throughout other parts of an enterprise as well.
When piloting AI, it is common for individuals or departments to focus on the results of the single use case they’re solving for, overlooking potential benefits to other parts of the enterprise. It’s critical when quantifying the effectiveness of AI to have a holistic view and account for tangential and compounded value.
Realizing the Ripple Effect
Fraud has a significant impact on consumers, both financially and emotionally. When individuals fall victim to fraudulent activities, they can face devastating financial losses, which can take a considerable amount of time and effort to recover from.
Indeed, the effect an enterprise has on its customers, their families and communities may not be entirely quantifiable. But a single department’s AI initiatives may have positive consequences, both intentional and unintentional—like promoting economic security. This kind of impact counts.
It holds true that those with a comprehensive and defined AI strategy are primed to leverage the potential of generative AI. Regardless of where an organization is in its AI journey, it should look beyond the single use case being solved for and consider the broader impact on the overall enterprise and even the customers and communities being served.
The true value of an AI investment can be quite expansive. And this measure will help validate the addition of generative AI to strengthen and scale current business strategies—while paving the way for a more secure and innovative future.
