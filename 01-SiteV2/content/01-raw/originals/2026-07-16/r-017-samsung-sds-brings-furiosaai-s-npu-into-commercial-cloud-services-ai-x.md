# Samsung SDS Brings FuriosaAI’s NPU Into Commercial Cloud Services < AI·XR < K-Tech < K-World News < 기사본문 - KMJ

## clean_text

Samsung SDS is moving a homegrown artificial intelligence chip from the testing stage into real-world enterprise use.
Starting July 16, the company will offer NPU-as-a-Service, or NPUaaS, through the Samsung Cloud Platform. The service will run on Renegade, also known as RNGD, FuriosaAI&rsquo;s second-generation neural processing unit designed for AI inference.
FuriosaAI&rsquo;s second-generation AI inference accelerator, Renegade. Photo courtesy of FuriosaAI.
Samsung SDS will also use the chip to power two of its major enterprise AI products: FabriX, an AI agent platform, and Brity Works, an AI-powered collaboration suite.
The rollout marks a significant step for South Korea&rsquo;s AI semiconductor industry. Rather than remaining a benchmark project or pilot program, FuriosaAI&rsquo;s chip will now support cloud services used by public-sector agencies and corporate customers.
Choi Jung-jin, a group leader at Samsung SDS, announced the launch at the 2026 K-NPU Tech Wave event in Seoul on July 14.
&ldquo;We will launch Renegade-based NPUaaS on July 16,&rdquo; Choi said, adding that the company has already secured customers for the service.
Access to AI Chips Without Building Dedicated Servers
Samsung SDS has installed Renegade chips at its data centers in Sangam, Seoul, and Dongtan, Hwaseong. The company plans to expand capacity in stages through the end of the year.
Customers will be able to choose the amount of computing power they need, with configurations ranging from one NPU card to two, four or eight cards.
The service allows businesses to use South Korean AI chips without purchasing and operating their own NPU servers. Customers can select computing resources based on the type of workload, including AI model training, inference and serverless applications.
Samsung SDS is also developing a system that compares cost and performance before assigning workloads to either GPUs or NPUs.
Under this hybrid infrastructure model, GPUs could handle demanding AI training, while more energy-efficient NPUs would process repetitive inference tasks. The approach could help companies lower operating costs as generative AI and AI agents are used more widely.
Renegade is FuriosaAI&rsquo;s second-generation AI accelerator for inference. It supports large language models, multimodal systems and computer vision models, with a focus on reducing power consumption and data-center operating costs.
FabriX connects enterprise data with multiple AI models, allowing companies to build internal AI agents. Brity Works applies AI to tasks such as email, meetings and document search.
Samsung SDS has already used the two platforms in public-sector services, including civil complaint classification, government procurement regulation analysis and search tools for citizens.
A South Korean Alternative to GPU-Centered AI Infrastructure
The launch could give South Korea&rsquo;s AI chip industry something it urgently needs: real customers, real workloads and measurable service usage.
For domestic NPU developers, benchmark results alone are not enough. Commercial success will depend on stable cloud operations, software compatibility and proven customer cases.
Samsung SDS also stands to benefit by reducing its dependence on GPU-centered infrastructure. As companies deploy more generative AI services and AI agents, the cost of running models repeatedly is rising, along with the power demands placed on data centers.
The company is also considering using NPUs in its robot transformation, or RX, business.
One possible use case is smart factories, where NPUs could process video recognition and decision-making tasks for robots while reducing power consumption and response time. Samsung SDS has not yet selected a chip supplier or set a schedule for the project.
FuriosaAI, meanwhile, is developing a third-generation AI chip with substantially higher performance and processing capacity than Renegade.
The company plans to position the chip for large language models and AI agents in both data centers and on-premise systems. Mass production could begin as early as late 2027.
