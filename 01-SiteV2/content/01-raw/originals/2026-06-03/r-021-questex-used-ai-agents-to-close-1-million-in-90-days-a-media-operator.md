# Questex Used AI Agents to Close $1 Million in 90 Days - A Media Operator

Questex Used AI Agents to Close $1 Million in 90 Days
By
Lisa Plummer Savas
May 27, 2026
Sandwish – stock.adobe.com
When a prospective exhibitor submits a form expressing interest in a Questex trade show, an AI sales agent named Julian is on the phone within two minutes. No queue; no waiting for a sales rep to circle back. During a three-month pilot program, that speed translated into more than $1 million in closed revenue.
For the New York City-based B2B media and events company, “Julian” is one piece of a broader AI deployment that is reshaping how the company handles exhibitor and sponsorship sales and content production across its portfolio. The results so far demonstrate how event operators can use artificial intelligence not to replace human talent, but to amplify it.
“We hit a breaking point on sales bandwidth due to our rapid revenue growth,” said Rhiannon James, Questex group president of life sciences, health and wellness, entertainment and infrastructure. “Our marketing team was generating more inbound leads than our SDR team could handle, and at the same time we were expanding our events into new exhibitor and sponsor categories.”
SDR, or sales development representative, is the industry term for the front-line sales role responsible for prospecting, qualifying leads and booking meetings for senior account executives to close. At Questex, those reps were stretched thin, handling inbound qualification while also trying to run outbound campaigns that required rebuilding contact lists and messaging from scratch for each new event or industry vertical.
“We were iterating slowly, not because of strategy, but because of setup time,” James said. “So we started where the pain was sharpest and the process was most repeatable: outbound pipeline generation for our events business. That’s where we deployed ‘Alice,’ our outbound AI SDR.”
Two Agents, Two Jobs
Questex now runs two AI sales agents in parallel: Alice handles outbound prospecting, while Julian manages inbound leads.
Questex defines ideal customer profiles by event, industry and role. Operating as a fully autonomous outbound engine, Alice then sources and enriches contact lists automatically, builds personalized outreach based on public data and internal context, runs multi-touch sequences, handles replies and books qualified meetings directly onto sales rep calendars, syncing everything to the company’s Salesforce CRM. The process now runs continuously, no longer pausing between research, list-building and messaging the way it did when humans had to manage each step, James said.
Meanwhile, Julian works the inbound side, where speed is the primary variable. When a prospect submits a form, Julian places a call within two minutes, asks a focused set of qualifying questions, and routes the lead to the appropriate rep or event team. Leads that don’t immediately convert are placed into automated email and LinkedIn follow-up sequences through Gong Engage Flows, an enterprise-wide subscription Questex implemented with Gong’s support.
Before Julian, response times on inbound leads could run hours or even days, but closing that gap drove a meaningful lift in conversion rates. Inbound meeting conversion rose from 30% to 37%, and Julian was attributed with $1.056 million in revenue closed during the three-month pilot. Working the outbound side during the same period, Alice generated more than $1 million in new pipeline.
Questex tracks performance across each stage of the funnel, from the percentage of inbound leads contacted within their target response window, to conversion to meetings, meetings to opportunities and opportunities to close. Results are attributed through Salesforce and Gong, tagging any opportunity where the first meaningful touch came from an AI agent. “We measure this rigorously,” James said.
Where the Humans Come In
Alice and Julian may own the top of the Questex sales funnel, but once a meeting is booked or a lead is fully qualified, a human account executive steps in to handle discovery and closing. According to James, the company has also been deliberate about which prospects AI should and shouldn’t contact first.
“AI is not a universal first touch,” James said, adding that the company avoids AI-initiated outreach for strategic or high-value accounts, existing client relationships with history or nuance, late-stage deal conversations and categories where tone matters more than speed. In those situations, humans lead and AI supports with research, drafting and follow-up.
The early rollout also surfaced friction that required adjustment. A subset of prospects could tell when outreach messaging felt automated and disengaged, or asked outright whether they were talking to a bot. That pushed Questex to tighten Alice’s personalization prompts. Some early meetings were not as well-qualified as account executives expected, which led the team to sharpen qualification criteria and disqualification logic using Gong call data to better define what a good-fit prospect actually looks like.
Internally, SDRs initially worried about being replaced by the AI agents, so in response, Questex gave existing team members ownership over the AI program itself. Today, several SDRs manage the AI program as part of their role, handling quality control, monitoring campaign performance, and adjusting outreach sequences for efficiency. While the repetitive setup work — list building, enrichment, sequence construction — is now handled by Alice and Julian, the humans focus on what requires judgment.
“Once reps saw the results, adoption accelerated,” James said.
From Sales to Content Production
Beyond sales, Questex has also turned to AI to enhance its creative and video production capabilities.
For its Bar & Restaurant Expo, Questex’s creative team produced a promotional video using ChatGPT for scripting, project management and still imagery; Kling 3.0 for video generation; Suno for music; and Descript for editing, cuts and transitions. The video was completed in 24 hours at a cost of approximately $2,000, roughly one-tenth of what comparable production had cost in prior years.
“Historically, only our biggest events could afford this level of video production,” James said. “Now we can scale it across the entire portfolio.”
For its broader video portfolio, the company draws on a different mix of off-the-shelf tools, including Midjourney for imagery, Notion AI for scripting and structure, and other AI-assisted editing tools for cuts, transitions and variations, she added.
The company has also identified where AI-assisted production falls short. High-end brand cinematography, emotionally nuanced storytelling and fully AI-generated visuals for flagship events remain areas where human creative leadership is required. For those projects, James said, AI enhances the workflow but does not replace it.
Getting the Foundations Right
According to James, the same operating philosophy runs through both use cases at Questex: AI as a force multiplier for human teams, not a substitute for them. But the technology, James said, is only as good as the processes supporting it.
“Fix your process before layering AI on top,” James said. “AI amplifies whatever you plug it into. If your ICP definitions, CRM hygiene or follow-up processes are messy, AI will scale the mess.”
For event and media companies considering a similar path, James said the entry point matters: start with outbound sales for a specific event or segment, not the most complex part of the business, and bring the team along from the beginning by showing data and giving people ownership over the rollout.
“At Questex, AI didn’t replace reps — it removed friction and amplified performance,” James said. “If you get those foundations right, you can see ROI in weeks, not years.”
