I keep hearing „Shouldn’t we go all-in on this one big #AI use case?“ No - and here’s why.
I keep hearing „Shouldn’t we go all-in on this one big #AI use case?“ No - and here’s why.
Most companies don’t need one miracle use case.
They need one hundred small wins that quietly reshape the P&L.
To be clear — a single, massive AI use case certainly can move the needle. But in practice, those lighthouses come with huge trade-offs: they’re expensive, they need #data that often doesn’t exist yet, they require coordination across multiple business units, and they take a long time to deliver results.
Most boards don’t want to wait multiple years for #ROI. And most companies underestimate how much new data they need before a moonshot even becomes feasible.
That’s why - as of right now - the real economic value of AI often comes from the opposite direction:
a collection of smaller, faster wins that stack and compound — driving #productivity across hundreds of processes, not just one.
And if you want to consistently find these high-value opportunities, you need a repeatable way of spotting them. Over the years, I’ve developed a simple shortcut that yielded a broad variety of great projects:
1️⃣ Follow the big budgets.
If a process costs €100m a year, even a 5% improvement is meaningful value creation. And more often than not you don’t even need “AI magic” — proper data integration and combination can create spectacular results.
2️⃣ Look for one-size-fits-all logic.
Wherever everyone (or everything) gets the same marketing message, the same schedule, the same treatment despite that you have more granular data — AI can help to create tailored and personalised experiences that not only improve customer experience but also drive conversion rates. These use cases almost always print money.
3️⃣ Identify unnecessarily manual tasks.
Any process where someone reads something, interprets it, retypes it, routes it, or validates it is a candidate for #automation. LLMs understand the written and spoken word and hence can reduce friction, increase efficiency and reduce errors in manual processes.
So, from what I oberserve, the actual value from AI won‘t come from one single use case but from clever application across the entire value chains - and I‘d like to know if you see this differently.
Would you rather go for the one big use case or for the many smaller ones? Also, what‘s your favorite shortcut to identify high-value use cases in your organization?
