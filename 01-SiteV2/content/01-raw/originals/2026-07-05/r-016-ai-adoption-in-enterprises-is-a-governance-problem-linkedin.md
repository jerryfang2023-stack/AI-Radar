# AI Adoption in Enterprises is a Governance Problem - LinkedIn

## clean_text

Rolling out GitHub Copilot to 150+ developers taught me something nobody puts in the press releases.
Rolling out GitHub Copilot to 150+ developers taught me something nobody puts in the press releases.
The technology was the easy part.
We had the licences approved within weeks. The developers were excited. The tooling worked... and then we hit the real work. Legal needed to understand what data was leaving the organisation. Security needed to validate the model's access scope. Data Protection had questions about code trained on proprietary repositories. Procurement had a process. Compliance had a checklist...
Every single one of those conversations was legitimate, and none of them were in the (initial) rollout plan.
What I learned is that AI adoption in an enterprise is not a technology problem. It is a governance and change management problem wearing a technology hat.
The organisations that get stuck are not the ones with bad tools. They are the ones that treated adoption as a technical deployment rather than an organisational transformation.
The ones that succeed build the governance framework before they need it: they bring Legal, Security, and Data Protection into the conversation early, not as gatekeepers to negotiate around but as partners who need to understand what they are signing off on. They invest in the human side too: training, community building, showing developers what good looks like.
Eighteen months after go-live, 150 developers are active users. More telling: 70% of them are already using Copilot in agentic mode, not just chat. That shift from assisted coding to autonomous task execution is significant. It did not happen because the tool was good. It happened because we treated the rollout as a change programme, not a software installation.
That is the governance reality of AI adoption at scale. The technology is the smallest part of the problem.
So when I hear technology and delivery leads say they are not ready to start a new programme with an AI-first approach, I understand the hesitation. The tooling feels new, the governance feels unclear, and the risk feels easier to defer than to manage.
But here is what I have seen: the teams that wait until they feel ready rarely get there. The ones that commit to AI-first from day one, build the governance as they go, and treat the learning curve as part of the delivery, end up somewhere completely different eighteen months later. Not just in productivity. In how they think, how they work, and what they believe is possible.
Starting a new programme today without an AI-first mindset is a choice. Just make sure it is a conscious one.
