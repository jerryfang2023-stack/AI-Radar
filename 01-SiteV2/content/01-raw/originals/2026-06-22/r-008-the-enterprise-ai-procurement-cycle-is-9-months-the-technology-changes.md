# The enterprise AI procurement cycle is 9 months. The technology changes every 3.

## clean_text

Back to Blog A Fortune 500 CIO told me last month that they started evaluating enterprise AI search tools in June 2025. They selected a vendor in November. Legal and security review took until February 2026. The contract was signed in March. By the time deployment started, the vendor they'd evaluated nine months earlier had shipped three major architectural changes, deprecated two features from the original demo, and the competitive landscape included four new entrants that didn't exist when the evaluation began.
He wasn't upset with the vendor. He was upset with the process. "We're using a procurement system designed for buying ERP software on a five-year cycle to evaluate technology that reinvents itself quarterly," he said.
The anatomy of a 9-month procurement cycle
Enterprise AI procurement follows the same stages as any enterprise software purchase, but each stage has unique friction in the AI context.
Discovery and shortlisting (4-6 weeks). The team identifies the problem, surveys the market, and creates a shortlist of 3-5 vendors. For AI tools, this stage is complicated by the sheer number of entrants (the enterprise AI search category alone has 40+ vendors) and the difficulty of distinguishing real capability from demo-ware.
Technical evaluation and POC (6-10 weeks). Proof of concept with real data. For AI knowledge tools, this requires connecting to production systems (Slack, Confluence, Google Drive), which triggers its own approval cycle. Many companies take 3-4 weeks just to approve data access for a POC.
Security review (4-8 weeks). Standard at any enterprise. For AI tools, security review now includes: model provider assessment (where do prompts go?), data residency questions, AI-specific risk evaluation (hallucination, prompt injection), and often a net-new "AI governance" review that nobody has run before so nobody knows how long it takes.
Legal review (3-6 weeks). Contract negotiation, DPA, BAA if applicable, IP indemnification (an AI-specific clause that didn't exist two years ago), data processing terms, model training opt-out clauses.
Budget approval and procurement (2-4 weeks). Standard finance workflow. Often delayed by quarterly budget cycles.
Deployment and integration (4-8 weeks). Actual technical deployment, SSO integration, permission sync, initial indexing, user onboarding.
Total elapsed time: 7 to 11 months. The median across every enterprise customer I've talked to: approximately 9 months from first meeting to users in production.
Why the 3-month technology cycle matters
In mature enterprise software categories (CRM, ERP, HRIS), the product you evaluate in month 1 is substantively the same product you deploy in month 9. Features don't radically change. The architecture is stable. Your POC results are valid for the entire cycle.
AI is different. In the last 9 months:
Frontier model capabilities shifted significantly (GPT-4o, Claude 3.5, Gemini 2.0 all shipped during a typical procurement cycle)
RAG architectures evolved (agentic RAG, graph-augmented retrieval, late-interaction models became production-ready)
New categories emerged (coding agents went from demo to production use at multiple enterprises)
Pricing models changed (token costs dropped 50-70% across major providers)
The POC you ran in month 2 used a model that's now two generations old. The performance benchmarks from your evaluation are obsolete. The pricing comparison spreadsheet reflects costs that have since halved.
What this means for buyers
The "best" vendor at evaluation time might not be best at deployment time. If you selected a vendor partly because they used GPT-4 Turbo and a competitor used a weaker model, and by deployment time both vendors have access to frontier models, your selection criteria may no longer differentiate them.
Contractual commitments conflict with technological flexibility. A 3-year contract signed in March 2026 locks you into an architecture and approach that might be obsolete by March 2027. But vendors need multi-year commitments to justify enterprise sales effort.
POC results expire. A proof of concept run with 2025-era models and architectures does not predict performance with 2026-era technology. Teams that take 6 months between POC and deployment are deploying based on stale evidence.
What procurement needs to change
I don't have a complete answer here, but I see patterns in companies that handle this well:
Shorter evaluation cycles with faster decision rights. Companies that compress the evaluation to 4-6 weeks by pre-approving AI-specific security frameworks (rather than inventing a new review process per vendor) deploy 3-4 months earlier. The time savings comes from removing the "nobody knows how to evaluate AI security" bottleneck.
Architecture-first evaluation, model-agnostic. Instead of evaluating based on current model capability (which will change), evaluate based on architectural decisions that are durable: data isolation approach, retrieval methodology, integration depth, permission enforcement. These change slowly even as underlying models change rapidly.
Evergreen POCs over point-in-time evaluations. Some companies maintain a standing "AI sandbox" environment where new tools can be tested continuously against production data. This eliminates the 3-4 week data access approval cycle and allows re-evaluation when technology shifts.
Moonpool's architecture is model-agnostic by design. The retrieval layer, permission enforcement, and integration connectors are independent of the generation model. When a better model becomes available, we can swap it without architectural changes. This means what you evaluate in month 1 is architecturally identical to what you deploy in month 9, even if the underlying models have improved.
The honest tradeoff
Faster procurement means less thorough vetting. There's real value in a comprehensive security review, legal assessment, and multi-week POC. Skipping these stages to move faster introduces risk. Companies that deploy AI tools too quickly sometimes discover security gaps, compliance issues, or performance problems that a thorough evaluation would have caught.
The answer isn't "skip security review." It's "build reusable security evaluation frameworks for AI tools so the same review doesn't take 8 weeks every time." The first AI vendor through security review always takes longest. The fourth should be evaluated against an established framework in 2 weeks.
If you're in early stages of AI procurement now: focus your evaluation on architecture and integration depth, not model capability. Push your security team to create a reusable AI vendor assessment framework rather than a bespoke review per vendor. And set expectations internally that the product will improve between selection and deployment, so the POC represents a floor, not a ceiling.
