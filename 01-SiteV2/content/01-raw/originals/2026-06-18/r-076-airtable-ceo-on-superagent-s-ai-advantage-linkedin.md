The gap isn’t between people who use AI and people who don’t.
It’s between people who see AI as a tool vs. those who see it as a new layer of infrastructure.
Last week, a Thiel Fellow declared “Web 4.0” and launched an AI agent that earns its own compute, replicates itself, and operates without human permission.
The token spiked 3,600%. Vitalik Buterin called it reckless. Experts called it marketing.
They’re all missing the point.
The label doesn’t matter. The shift underneath it does.
We are watching the internet reorganize itself around agents that don’t need your login, your server, or your approval. They negotiate. They transact. They persist.
This isn’t theoretical. I run distributed AI agent networks across dedicated hardware. I’ve built what I call a Walled Garden: proprietary data and workflows that stay local while AI does the processing. I’m in active conversations with founders who are architecting secure, autonomous agent infrastructure right now.
Here is what I see coming, and how to position for it:
1/ Learn to DIRECT AI, not just prompt it.
Prompting is table stakes. The real leverage lies in orchestrating multi-agent systems, where AI handles 80% of a workflow end-to-end. The people who can architect these systems will operate at a scale that was previously reserved for entire departments.
2/ Build your irreplaceable edge NOW.
AI cannot replicate original thinking, lived experience, trust, relationships, or taste. These are not soft skills. They are the only durable competitive advantages left. Double down.
3/ Create assets, not output.
If AI can regenerate it in 10 seconds, it is not an asset. Systems that compound, proprietary data layers, and distribution you own are assets. Everything else is disposable.
4/ Get financially resilient.
Entire job categories will compress. The timeline is debatable. The direction is not. Multiple income streams and low fixed overhead are no longer optional.
5/ Study AI architecture like your career depends on it.
Because it does.
Not just prompting. Understand agent orchestration, tool use, API design, and the security implications of autonomous systems: 30 minutes a day, minimum.
6/ Own your distribution.
When AI can create anything, the person who controls attention and trust wins. Email lists. Communities. Direct audiences. These are the moats.
7/ Think in systems, not tasks.
Stop asking what AI can do for you today. Start asking what system you can build where AI runs the majority of it indefinitely. That question changes everything about how you allocate your time.
The people who treat this as a novelty will be managed by the people who treat it as infrastructure.
Prepare accordingly.

In the past few weeks the AI hype has shifted from context graphs (and yes, everyone thinks slightly differently about what they actually are and can deliver) to the AI tech stack lasagne.
Some big vendors, like OpenAI are making plays for various layers of the stack - stretching out from model layer to the orchestration layer. While this market continues to heat up - we will see more and more major vendors trying to own as much of the stack as they can. While this might seem convenient, this is usually not good news for enterprises (as history shows with mediocre monoliths), and the impact will quickly and acutely be felt in loss of agility and getting stuck in vendor bound logic.
The success of AI for enterprises hinges on excelling in both flexibility and control - which is best achieved by leveraging horizontal layers of best in class technology. Layers designed to work across vendor agnostic stack components, that evolve independently and that do not collapse into one roadmap.
At IndyKite, we believe control should increase as systems become more autonomous. But not coarse-grained, static, after-the-fact control. Control needs to be dynamic and intelligent, operating at speed at the moment data is retrieved and used.
That changes what enterprises can do.
They can experiment with different models and orchestration layers without rebuilding governance every time. They can introduce new agents without fragmenting data access and use policies. They can evolve parts of the stack while keeping a consistent layer of enforceable, traceable control across it.
Context is central to this. Not just to improve agent decisions, but to ensure that data carries its provenance, consent, and usage constraints wherever it moves. Context becomes the mechanism that makes governance enforceable at runtime, across the ecosystem.
The stack will evolve quickly. Enterprise architecture needs to remain free and flexible enough to evolve with it.
