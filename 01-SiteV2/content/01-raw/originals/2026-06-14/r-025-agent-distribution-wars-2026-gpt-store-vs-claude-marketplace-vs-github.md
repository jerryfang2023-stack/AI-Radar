# Agent Distribution Wars 2026: GPT Store vs Claude Marketplace vs GitHub MCP | AgentMarketCap

## clean_text

Three platforms. Three incompatible theories of how AI agents should be discovered, distributed, and monetized. Whoever wins this fight will effectively become the App Store of the agentic era — a position worth far more than the hundreds of millions of dollars currently flowing through these nascent marketplaces.
As of Q1 2026, the battle lines are clear: OpenAI's GPT Store is the incumbent consumer play, Anthropic's Claude Marketplace is the aggressive enterprise challenger with a zero-commission model, and GitHub's MCP Registry is the developer-native dark horse betting that open standards beat walled gardens every time. The agentic AI market is already projected to hit $10.8 billion in 2026 and expand to nearly $200 billion by 2034 — but the critical question isn't who builds the best agents. It's who owns the pipe through which those agents reach buyers.
The Incumbent: OpenAI's GPT Store and the Creator Economy Bet
OpenAI launched its GPT Store in January 2024 with a bold promise: custom AI agents would follow the trajectory of iPhone apps, minting a new class of AI-native entrepreneurs. By early 2026, the store hosts over three million custom GPTs — more applications than most enterprise software catalogues have seen in their entire lifetimes.
The reality of creator economics, however, has been more sobering than the headline number suggests. OpenAI's revenue-sharing program pays approximately $0.03 per conversation, meaning a creator needs more than 33,000 quality interactions per month to clear $1,000 in earnings. The eligibility bar is steep: US-based account, public GPT, and a sustained 25+ conversations per week. Most builders never qualify. Those who do typically earn $100–500 per month, with only a tiny fraction reaching the mid-four-figures range.
What salvaged the GPT Store thesis was a product shift, not a monetization fix. The release of GPT-5.2 in December 2025 fundamentally transformed custom GPTs from "enhanced chatbots" into what developers are now calling "agentic mini-apps" — systems that can call external tools, maintain state across sessions, and execute multi-step workflows autonomously. Savvy builders responded by abandoning viral consumer GPTs entirely and pivoting to B2B consulting tools and enterprise internal deployments, where value is easier to demonstrate and willingness to pay is measurably higher.
This pivot has meaningful implications for the Store's distribution model. OpenAI built its marketplace assuming a consumer discovery metaphor — users browse, try, subscribe. What's emerging instead is closer to an enterprise sales motion dressed in a storefront UI. The GPT Store's browse-and-discover interface was optimized for the wrong buyer persona, and OpenAI hasn't yet rebuilt it for the B2B reality that its most valuable creators have already moved toward.
The Challenger: Anthropic's Zero-Commission Gambit
Anthropic's entry into marketplace distribution was, by the standards of the industry, unusually transparent about its strategic intent. In March 2026, the company launched the Claude Marketplace — and announced it would take zero commission on transactions.
To understand why this is remarkable, consider what "zero cut" means against the competitive backdrop. AWS Marketplace and Azure Marketplace typically extract between 3% and 30% of each transaction, depending on product category. Apple takes 15–30% from App Store developers. Anthropic is explicitly forgoing this revenue stream at launch, a decision that signals one thing loudly: deepening enterprise lock-in is currently worth more to Anthropic than transaction revenue.
The launch catalog was deliberately curated rather than open. Six partners anchored the initial marketplace: GitLab, Harvey AI, Lovable, Replit, Rogo, and Snowflake. Each represents a different enterprise vertical — developer tooling, legal AI, low-code creation, collaborative coding, financial intelligence, and data cloud. The selection isn't random; it's a statement about which enterprise software categories Anthropic believes agents will disrupt first.
The procurement mechanic is cleverly engineered. Enterprise customers with committed annual API spending can apply a portion of that commitment toward partner tools — and Anthropic handles all invoicing. This makes Anthropic the central clearinghouse for enterprise AI procurement, not just an infrastructure provider. Partners gain immediate access to an installed base of enterprise customers who have already completed security reviews, signed contracts, and allocated budget. Anthropic gains stickiness that goes well beyond API calls.
Simultaneously, Anthropic's Claude Cowork platform shipped 13 new MCP connectors in February 2026, covering Google Workspace (Drive, Calendar, Gmail), DocuSign, Apollo, Clay, Outreach, SimilarWeb, MSCI, LegalZoom, FactSet, WordPress, and Harvey. Enterprises can now build private internal marketplaces — curated catalogs of specialized agents distributed across their organizations without exposing those agents to the public marketplace. This two-tier approach (public marketplace for ecosystem, private marketplace for enterprise customization) is structurally similar to Salesforce AppExchange's public/private distribution model.
The Dark Horse: GitHub's MCP Registry and the Open Standard Play
While OpenAI and Anthropic fight to own proprietary distribution channels, GitHub is betting on a different theory entirely: that the agent layer will be governed by an open standard the way the web was governed by HTTP, and that the platform controlling the canonical registry for that standard will own discovery by default.
The Model Context Protocol (MCP) — originally developed by Anthropic but donated to the Linux Foundation and now adopted by Anthropic, OpenAI, Microsoft, and Google — has become what developers are calling "the USB-C for AI." It provides a standardized interface for agents to connect to external tools, data sources, and services without bespoke integrations.
GitHub launched its MCP Registry in September 2025 as the central discovery hub for MCP servers. By early 2026, the registry hosts 923+ MCP servers covering everything from Playwright-based browser automation to Notion knowledge access to GitHub's own MCP server with over a hundred tools. This isn't a curated enterprise catalog — it's a developer-native ecosystem index, more similar to npm or PyPI than to an app store.
The GitHub angle is reinforced by raw repository growth. GitHub's Octoverse 2025 report revealed 4.3 million AI-related repositories on the platform — a 178% year-over-year increase in LLM-focused projects. GitHub Copilot Agent Mode, which rolled out to all VS Code users in April 2025 with full MCP support, gives every developer a direct pathway from MCP discovery to MCP deployment without leaving their IDE.
The limitation of this approach is commercialization. GitHub's MCP Registry, like npm, is a discovery mechanism rather than a transaction layer. There's no built-in monetization, no procurement integration, and no enterprise contract consolidation. Developers love it. CFOs have never heard of it.
Three Strategies, Three Different Markets
The divergence in approach maps directly onto the three distinct buyer personas these platforms are optimizing for:
Platform Primary Buyer Commission Model Distribution Logic
OpenAI GPT Store Consumers & SMBs $0.03/conversation Browse-and-discover
Anthropic Claude Marketplace Enterprise procurement teams 0% (no cut) Spend consolidation
GitHub MCP Registry Individual developers Free (no monetization) Open standard index
None of these is wrong in isolation. The problem is that the market hasn't decided which buyer persona matters most at the current stage of agentic AI adoption — and all three platforms are making different bets on that answer.
Salesforce's trajectory with Agentforce offers a data point worth noting: it hit $540 million in annual recurring revenue and 18,500 enterprise customers by early 2026 through pure enterprise sales, with no consumer marketplace at all. If the enterprise bet is correct, Anthropic's zero-commission model looks prescient. If consumer adoption drives enterprise pull-through (as happened with Slack, Zoom, and Dropbox), OpenAI's creator ecosystem looks like the right foundation. If developers standardize on MCP before the distribution wars are decided, GitHub's registry position becomes defensible in ways that proprietary marketplaces cannot easily replicate.
The Consolidation Question
A single analogy structures most predictions about this market: the early smartphone ecosystem had dozens of app stores before network effects collapsed the market to two dominant platforms. Will the same happen with agent distribution?
The conditions are partially analogous. Discovery is a genuine problem — with 10,000+ agent applications and growing, buyers increasingly need curated, searchable, trustworthy catalogues. Trust and security review are enterprise-critical, which creates a natural moat for platforms that have already completed procurement compliance work. And the distribution layer is sticky: agents that are already deployed inside enterprise workflows, already credentialed against enterprise data sources, and already invoiced through a consolidated procurement channel, are very hard to move.
But MCP complicates the smartphone analogy in a meaningful way. The App Store's moat depended on closed APIs and exclusive distribution. MCP, by design, is open and interoperable. An agent built for the Claude Marketplace can, in principle, be served through the GitHub MCP Registry with minimal modification. The distribution wars are happening in a world where the underlying connector protocol is already standardized — which may limit how durable any single platform's marketplace moat can actually be.
The fragmentation we're witnessing in early 2026 may be less "winner-take-all consolidation incoming" and more "platform differentiation by vertical" — where OpenAI owns consumer and SMB discovery, Anthropic owns enterprise procurement consolidation, and GitHub owns developer-native tooling distribution. All three could sustain meaningful positions without any one of them winning outright.
What This Means for Agent Builders
For teams building AI agents today, the distribution question has immediate strategic implications:
If you're targeting enterprise procurement cycles , the Claude Marketplace's zero-commission model and spend consolidation mechanics represent a structurally superior go-to-market path right now. Being in front of buyers who have already committed budget and completed security reviews is more valuable than organic discovery traffic.
If you're targeting developer adoption , MCP compliance and a GitHub MCP Registry listing is effectively table stakes. The 923+ servers already indexed represent a competitive set, not a validation of success — but absence from the registry is a meaningful trust signal deficiency.
If you're targeting consumer or SMB buyers , the GPT Store's 910 million weekly active users on ChatGPT remain an unmatched distribution surface, despite the thin creator economics. The challenge is the B2B pivot the most successful builders have already made: the consumer UI doesn't serve B2B discovery well, and OpenAI hasn't fixed it yet.
The uncomfortable reality for most agent builders is that distribution strategy can't be deferred until after product-market fit. The three platforms have different technical requirements, different compliance expectations, and different buyer relationships. Building for one often means not being optimized for another.
The $500M Question
Current estimates put the agent marketplace economy at roughly $500 million in 2026 — a number that will look trivially small against the $199 billion projected agentic AI market by 2034. The platform that wins the distribution layer won't just earn marketplace fees; it will set the pricing norms, security standards, compliance requirements, and integration expectations for an entire category of software.
OpenAI has the audience. Anthropic has the enterprise relationships and the boldest commercial positioning. GitHub has the developer trust and the open standard. The distribution wars of 2026 are still early enough that none of these advantages is decisive — and late enough that the patterns being established now will be very hard to reverse.
