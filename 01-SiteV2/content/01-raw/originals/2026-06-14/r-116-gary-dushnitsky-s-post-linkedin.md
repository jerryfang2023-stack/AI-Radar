## clean_text

Techcrunch's Marina Temkin, CFA reports that this cohort of YC companies is raising less money, partially because they can now leverage AI tools where previously they needed to hire expensive talent.
Techcrunch's Marina Temkin, CFA reports that this cohort of YC companies is raising less money, partially because they can now leverage AI tools where previously they needed to hire expensive talent.
The 'vibe shift' echoes recent chatter about the "BIG small team" - namely, teams with a headcount of a dozen or so that reach dramatic ARR targets in a matter of months.
What will the future hold?
1. Will we see an increase in 'midsized startups' as Benjamin Hallen and Ed Hallen recently observed? (Source: https://lnkd.in/eyHS_ARq). Could it be that lower fundraise amounts imply that founders can now target sectors with moderate TAM, potentially giving rise to mid-size vertical-specific solutions?
or
2. Will we see excess entry and ensuing competition? As individuals become (over-) confident in their ability to build a unicorn on the cheap, there may be more and more startups going after the coveted unicorn status. Seminal insights from Colin Camerer and Dan Lovallo suggest that overconfidence can lead to excess entry and result in hyper competition and an increase in failure rates (source: https://lnkd.in/ef8zRXEY).
Curious to hear your predictions- and any early signals of what the future holds!
BTW, the impact of digital tools on scale-up trajectory is not new. We observed a similar pattern in the context of e-commerce. Startups using low-code tools exhibit lower headcounts and engage less in fundraising. Notabely, the exit profile shift from 'go big or go bust' and the value-per-FTE is on par with other startups (source: https://lnkd.in/dmGt3Y5x).
