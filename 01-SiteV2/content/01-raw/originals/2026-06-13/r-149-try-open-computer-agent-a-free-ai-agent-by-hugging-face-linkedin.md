If you zoom out on the last 30 years of AI, it’s not just a story of faster models, it’s a story of evolution in intelligence.
We started with rule-based systems machines that could only do what we told them to.
Then came classical machine learning algorithms that started finding patterns on their own.
The deep learning era taught computers to see, speak, and understand giving rise to everything from voice assistants to computer vision.
But the real shift happened with Transformers and LLMs.
For the first time, machines could understand and generate natural language but they still had limits.
Static models. Hallucinations. No real-time grounding.
Then came RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) a bridge between LLMs and the outside world. Models could now pull verified, contextual data to make their answers smarter.
That’s when Tool Use & Function Calling entered enabling LLMs to act, not just answer.
Calling APIs, executing code, retrieving databases suddenly, AI wasn’t just conversational, it was operational.
Now, we’re entering the Agentic Era, where AI systems plan, reason, and collaborate with other agents. They’re not just tools anymore; they’re teammates.
Multi-agent systems where dozens of AI agents coordinate, share memory, and execute complex workflows together.
Every leap in this journey has one thing in common, we’re moving from intelligence in isolation to intelligence in collaboration.
That’s where the real potential (and responsibility) lies.
What excites me most isn’t what AI can do today .
It’s what happens when these systems start working together, seamlessly, like teams do.
Because the future of AI won’t be about one model being smarter.
It’ll be about many systems thinking together.

(Almost) Everything You’ve Heard About AI Agents Is a Lie
An AI agent in 2025 is not an autonomous worker. It is a large language model wrapped with a planner, some APIs, memory, and error handling. A probabilistic system pretending to be deterministic.
The math is ruthless. Ten steps at 90% accuracy yield only a 35% chance of success. Benchmarks confirm it: most state-of-the-art agents complete fewer than half of real workflows.
Still, there is value in current AI Agents!
In narrow domains, with clear constraints and human oversight, agents are already useful. They draft SQL queries, triage tickets, handle exceptions, and cut cycle times. This is where they work today.
What enterprises cannot afford is vendor hype.
Deployment requires shadow mode testing, full observability, validation layers, and strict permission boundaries. Without this, you are funding expensive failures.
Agents are fragile orchestrators. Not lies, not magic. Just engineering. This will change in the future as research continues to progress at a rapid pace.

Debugging your AI agent? You're probably looking at the wrong thing. 🧐
Most of us instinctively focus on the final, flawed output. But that's like treating the symptom and ignoring the disease.
The real failure almost always happens earlier, in the agent's internal Thought process or during a Tool Call. If the reasoning is flawed from the start, the output is guaranteed to be garbage.
🔍 The Fix: Stop staring at the end result. Dive into the Trace.
Your debug process should be:
1️⃣ Find the bad Agent Response.
2️⃣ Scroll up to the immediate Thought or Tool Input that preceded it.
3️⃣ Target your prompt fix or adjustment there.
That's where you'll find the lightning-speed solution.
Debugging an agent means debugging the timeline of its decisions.
