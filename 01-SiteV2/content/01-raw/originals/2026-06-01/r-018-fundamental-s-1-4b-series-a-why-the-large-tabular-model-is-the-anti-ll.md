# Fundamental's $1.4B Series A: Why the Large Tabular Model Is the Anti-LLM Enterprise Bet | AgentMarketCap

## clean_text

Most of the money in enterprise AI is chasing the wrong data.
Roughly 80–90% of what an enterprise generates is unstructured — emails, PDFs, videos, Slack transcripts, call recordings. It is also the stuff that LLMs have already conquered. But a disproportionate share of the decisions a company actually cares about — pricing, risk, fraud, churn, hospital readmissions, demand planning, energy load — still lives in the other 10–20%: rows, columns, foreign keys, timestamps. Structured data. The boring kind. The kind GPT-5 still cannot reliably reason over.
On February 5, 2026, a stealth-mode AI lab called Fundamental emerged with a $255M raise, a $1.4B post-money valuation, and a foundation model purpose-built for that boring 10–20%. The round — $30M Seed plus a $225M Series A led by Oak HC/FT — minted a unicorn before Series B and announced a new product category: the Large Tabular Model, or LTM. The flagship model, NEXUS, was trained on billions of tables on Amazon SageMaker HyperPod and is being sold through the AWS Marketplace alongside compute and storage. Fortune 100 customers are already paying seven figures.
This is a consequential round for three reasons. The company is compressing the pre-unicorn timeline for a vertical foundation-model lab. The investor syndicate — Oak HC/FT, Valor Equity Partners, Battery, Salesforce Ventures, Hetz — is a health-and-fintech-heavy signal, not the usual frontier-lab cast. And the underlying thesis — that tables, not text, are the unclaimed foundation-model frontier — directly contradicts the LLM-everywhere procurement default that most enterprise buyers have spent 2024–2026 absorbing.
The Anti-LLM Thesis: Tables Are the Last Unclaimed Frontier
LLMs are a triumph of sequence modeling. They were built to predict the next token in a stream of text. That works beautifully for prose, and — with enough post-training — for code. It does not work for a 40-column customer table where the 37th column is a foreign key and the 12th is a denormalized rolling sum.
The industry's standard workaround has been to stringify structured data and hope the LLM figures it out. It rarely does. Anyone who has asked Claude or GPT-5 to reason over a 10,000-row CSV has watched the model hallucinate aggregates, miscount rows, or skip columns that did not fit in its context window. Retrieval-augmented patterns, text-to-SQL, and "chart mode" tool calls paper over the problem without solving it. The underlying model has no native sense of tabular structure.
Fundamental's bet, articulated most cleanly by Oak HC/FT managing partner Annie Lamont, is that "structured, relational data has yet to see the benefits of the deep learning revolution." NEXUS is the lab's answer. Rather than a text transformer coerced into reading tables, it is trained from the ground up on billions of tabular datasets, learning non-linear relationships across rows and columns the way GPT-5 learned relationships across tokens. Integration takes "a single line of code," ingesting raw tables without manual feature engineering, and the model outputs predictions — demand forecasts, price elasticities, churn probabilities, fraud scores — directly into the enterprise data stack.
The category already has a name: Large Tabular Model. Citi Ventures flagged LTMs as a distinct 2026 enterprise AI category, arguing the analytics platform market alone ($77B+ by 2028, 16% CAGR) plus the foundation model market ($66B by 2029, 64%+ CAGR) creates a pull strong enough to sustain a parallel foundation-model lineage optimized for rows and columns.
A Unicorn Before Series B: The Timeline Compression
Fundamental was founded in October 2024. Sixteen months later it is a $1.4B company. That cadence is worth pausing on.
The traditional vertical foundation-model path ran something like: seed in year one, Series A in year two to build the model, Series B in year three once pilot revenue materialized, then unicorn pricing somewhere between Series B and Series C on the back of production ARR. Fundamental skipped three rungs of that ladder. It raised a $30M seed, folded it into a $225M Series A, emerged from stealth with the model already shipping, and priced the round at unicorn levels before any investor could see a second year of telemetry.
The comparables tell the story of how unusual this looks across vertical categories:
Company Vertical Round at unicorn Notes
Fundamental Tabular foundation model Series A ($1.4B) Pre-Series-B unicorn, 16 months post-founding
Cursor Coding agents Series C ($29B, later $50B) Required ~$500M ARR for markup
Cognition (Devin) Autonomous engineering Series B Required Windsurf-scale distribution deal
Sierra CX agents Series C ($10B / ~$150M ARR) Two-year stealth-plus-ramp
Legora Legal agents Series B ($5.55B) Vertical-native GTM through law firms
9fin Credit intelligence Series C ($170M raise) Data-moat compounding required
None of the other vertical-agent unicorns in the 2024–2026 cohort crossed $1B at Series A. Fundamental did, and the investors paying that price seem to be making a very specific argument: that structured-data foundation models are a category-defining rather than vertical-slice opportunity, and that anchoring the category requires pre-committing frontier-lab-style capital before a second mover can even announce.
The knock-on effect for H2 2026 vertical-agent pricing is real. Every Series A lead pricing a tabular-model or structured-data-adjacent startup now has a $1.4B Series A comp they cannot ignore. That pulls the pricing floor up across any vertical that can credibly claim "foundation model for X" — and pulls it back down, brutally, for vertical companies that cannot.
The Syndicate Signal: Health, Fintech, and a Roster of Operator Angels
Oak HC/FT is not a frontier-lab investor. It is a healthcare-and-fintech specialist. Its presence as lead — rather than Sequoia, Founders Fund, or a16z — tells you something about where Fundamental plans to land the model first.
The named investor list reinforces the read:
Oak HC/FT — lead; portfolio skews to healthcare payment, pharma data, and financial infrastructure.
Valor Equity Partners — operational-value growth fund; heavy in supply chain, logistics, and industrial software.
Battery Ventures — deep enterprise software index.
Salesforce Ventures — strategic pull for CRM churn and pipeline prediction.
Hetz Ventures — Israeli-rooted early-stage fund with a deep-tech bias.
Then the angel list, which reads less like "we like the team" and more like a directed distribution plan:
Aravind Srinivas (Perplexity CEO) — search / retrieval pull.
Assaf Rappaport (Wiz CEO) — security and cloud-native data-flow routing.
Henrique Dubugras (Brex co-founder) — fintech structured-data pipelines.
Olivier Pomel (Datadog CEO) — observability and telemetry-table scale.
The subtext of this syndicate is that NEXUS will be sold into financial services, healthcare revenue-cycle management, e-commerce pricing, and operational telemetry — not into "enterprise search" or "general GenAI." Those are all domains where the ROI of a 2–3% accuracy lift on a structured prediction compounds into seven-figure ACV, because a 2% lift on a fraud model or a demand forecast is worth orders of magnitude more than a 2% lift on a document-summarization task.
The AWS Partnership: LTM as a Marketplace SKU
The AWS distribution deal is the least-discussed but arguably most strategic piece of the announcement.
Fundamental trained NEXUS on Amazon SageMaker HyperPod and is selling it through the AWS Marketplace "the same way [customers] buy compute or storage." That framing is deliberate. It positions NEXUS as infrastructure — a ratable line item in an AWS bill — rather than as an enterprise software SaaS subscription with separate procurement, security review, and VPC peering.
Three things fall out of that:
Procurement friction collapses. Enterprise buyers already on AWS can spin up NEXUS against their existing Redshift, RDS, and S3 data without a new vendor review.
Pricing becomes usage-based. Tabular predictions are a compute-like primitive; billing NEXUS against row-scans or inference calls maps cleanly to the AWS consumption model.
AWS gets a differentiated LTM SKU that Azure and GCP do not yet counter. Databricks has a TabPFN integration, Google has nothing specifically branded, and Azure's structured-data AI story still leans on SQL-adjacent copilots.
This is the same playbook Anthropic ran through Bedrock and OpenAI ran through Azure — but transposed onto a foundation-model lineage that does not yet have a dominant frontier lab.
The Competitive Field: Prior Labs, Neuralk, and the Open Research Lineage
Fundamental is not inventing the tabular foundation model concept from scratch. A small research community has been building toward LTMs for two years:
Prior Labs' TabPFN-2.5 shipped in late 2025, scaling to 50K samples and 2K features, with an enterprise variant extending to 10 million rows via Databricks. TabPFN is open-source-friendly and research-heavy.
Neuralk-AI has been publishing a five-part series on tabular foundation models targeting enterprise workflows, with the fourth installment landing February 24, 2026.
Academic benchmarks — the LAMDA-Tabular survey, TabPFN zero-shot leaderboards, and the growing tabular-deep-learning track at NeurIPS — suggest the research frontier is real.
Fundamental's differentiation relative to Prior Labs is scale and go-to-market. TabPFN is a research-led brand with a 100K-row operational ceiling (10M in the enterprise tier). NEXUS is being pitched as "the billions-of-tables foundation model" and bundled with an AWS distribution deal and Fortune 100 anchor customers. That is a different commercial posture: Prior Labs looks like Hugging Face, Fundamental looks like OpenAI.
Whether both can coexist — one as the open-source research lineage and one as the closed frontier lab — is the open question of the LTM category through 2027.
What to Watch Next
The round raises a set of specific empirical questions that 2026 will answer:
Does NEXUS actually beat gradient-boosted trees at production scale? XGBoost, LightGBM, and CatBoost remain the operational workhorses of tabular prediction, and they are cheap. An LTM has to be materially more accurate or materially easier to deploy — ideally both — to unseat a twenty-year incumbent stack. No independent benchmarks have landed yet.
Do the Fortune 100 pilots convert to multi-year ACV? Seven-figure pilot contracts are a strong signal but not a renewal. The churn-prediction and demand-forecasting use cases also happen to be the ones enterprises are most instrumented to measure, which cuts both ways.
Does a second LTM lab raise a matching round? If Prior Labs or a new entrant lands a $200M+ round in H2 2026, the LTM category becomes real in the same way the coding-agent category became real when Cursor, Cognition, and Codeium all crossed $1B within a quarter.
Does AWS grant NEXUS the same co-sell motion it gave Anthropic? The Marketplace listing is table stakes; a dedicated Bedrock-equivalent console placement would be the indicator that AWS is committing to LTM as a platform category.
How does the syndicate's healthcare and fintech lean show up in product roadmap? If the next major NEXUS release ships with a verticalized HCC-coding or fraud-scoring pack, the syndicate thesis becomes self-evident.
The Underwrite
The $1.4B Series A makes sense only if you believe two things simultaneously: that structured data is a foundation-model-scale opportunity rather than a feature inside the LLM stack, and that a DeepMind-alum team with an AWS distribution deal is the right one to anchor the category. Neither is self-evidently true. Tabular ML has been "the next big thing" for two decades, and the gradient-boosted-tree incumbents are stubbornly good. The counterargument — that LLMs generalized beyond anyone's 2019 expectations once scale hit a threshold, and tabular models may have a similar phase transition — is plausible but unproven.
What is already true is this: a foundation-model category that did not have a named frontier lab in January 2026 has one now, priced like a frontier lab, with Fortune 100 revenue and a hyperscaler distribution deal. Every enterprise AI stack decision made in H2 2026 will have to answer whether an LTM sits in the architecture. That is the real consequence of the round.
