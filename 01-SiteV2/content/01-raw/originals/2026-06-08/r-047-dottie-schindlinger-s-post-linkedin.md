For decades, boards have relied on management, advisors, and fellow directors to challenge assumptions and bring outside perspectives into the room.
AI introduces a new possibility: a governance-aware system that can analyze board materials, identify blind spots, and pressure-test decisions before they're made.
The question isn't whether AI can replace directors. It can't.
The more interesting question is whether directors will eventually be expected to use AI to augment their judgment—just as they rely on financial reports, legal advice, and other decision-support tools today.
If that happens, the conversation shifts from AI oversight to AI-enabled governance.
Do you think AI-assisted inquiry will become a standard part of good governance practice? 💬

AI governance is getting crowded.
That is not bad. It means the problem is real.
But I think the market is splitting into two questions:
1. Was the AI agent allowed to act?
2. Can the organization prove what happened after it did?
The first question is runtime governance.
The second question is post-workflow evidence.
That is the lane I’m building Orthos around:
human authority receipts,
preserved unknowns,
evidence readiness,
and tamper-evident records for AI-assisted work.
Because when an AI-supported decision, claim, report, or workflow is challenged later, “we had a policy” will not be enough.
The record has to survive.

So many AI conversations still start with the tool.
For organizations, I think the better starting point is the workflow.
Where does the work start? Who reviews it? What information travels with it? Where do decisions happen? What record exists afterward?
A model can produce a good answer and still be useless if it sits outside that path.
New legal AI systems will be less about standalone outputs and more about AI-enhanced workflows: processes that help work move from intake to analysis to review to decision without losing context, evidence, or accountability.

Most AI policies in higher ed govern nothing.
They sit in a shared drive.
Nobody reads them. Nobody enforces them. Nobody owns them.
I advise presidents and provosts on this. The pattern repeats everywhere.
Five myths keep AI governance stuck. Here they are.
𝗠𝘆𝘁𝗵 𝟭: You need a policy first. Truth: You need a decision owner first. Policy without an owner is decoration.
𝗠𝘆𝘁𝗵 𝟮: Faculty consensus comes before action. Truth: Consensus is the reward, not the requirement. Waiting for it guarantees drift.
𝗠𝘆𝘁𝗵 𝟯: Detection software solves integrity. Truth: Tools catch symptoms. They do not assign accountability.
𝗠𝘆𝘁𝗵 𝟰: A committee equals governance. Truth: Committees recommend. They rarely decide. Governance needs a single accountable name.
𝗠𝘆𝘁𝗵 𝟱: You must wait for clarity. Truth: Clarity arrives after you choose. Not before.
Each myth shares one root. It hands the hard call to no one.
That is the real cost. Not the AI. The avoidance.
Here is the takeaway.
Governance is not a document. It is a decision someone owns.
If this reframed the problem, repost it. Follow me for more.
