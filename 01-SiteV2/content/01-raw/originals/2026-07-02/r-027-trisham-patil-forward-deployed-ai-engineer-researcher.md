Everyone talks about Prompt Engineering.
But after deploying AI agents into a production legal drafting workflow, I realized the real bottleneck is something else entirely:
Context Engineering.
At small scale (10–20 files), our system worked beautifully:
• accurate legal references
• stable reasoning
• strong cross-document understanding
But when we scaled toward ~200 files, things broke:
• forgotten context
• hallucinated citations
• repeated analysis
• inconsistent drafts
At first, it felt random.
It wasn’t.
It was context degradation.
The biggest lesson I learned:
More context ≠ better reasoning.
Transformers have a finite attention budget. Once context becomes noisy, overlapping, or poorly timed, reasoning quality starts collapsing even if everything technically fits inside the context window.
The solution was not “better prompting.”
It was redesigning the system around Context Engineering:
• multi-agent partitioning instead of one giant agent
• just-in-time retrieval instead of preloading everything
• context compaction + summarization pipelines
• MCP tools returning only high-signal outputs
• structured prompts with clear sections
• eliminating duplicated semantic context
The mindset shift that changed everything:
“Give the model only what it needs right now for this reasoning step.”
Anthropic published an excellent article on this topic, and it perfectly matched many of the production failure modes we were seeing:
Anthropic – Effective Context Engineering for AI Agents
I also wrote a deeper technical breakdown of how we applied these ideas in a production legal draft generation workflow:
My blog post
I genuinely believe Context Engineering will become one of the defining disciplines of production AI engineering over the next few years.
Not just prompting models —
but designing how intelligence receives, filters, compresses, and reasons over information.

In 2010, the US Air Force built a supercomputer out of PlayStation 3s. And it ranked 33rd fastest in the world.
This isn't a joke.
The Air Force Research Laboratory (AFRL) assembled the Condor Cluster — 1,716 PS3 consoles running in parallel at Rome Laboratory, New York. Total cost: ~$2 million.
Purpose: processing aerial surveillance imagery and AI research.
So why PS3s?
The PS3's Cell processor was uniquely powerful for parallel computing — roughly 200 GFLOPS per unit. At $250 each, that worked out to ~$1.25 per GFLOP. Comparable research hardware at the time (like the NVIDIA Tesla C1060) cost $19 per GFLOP. The PS3 was 4–10x cheaper than anything else available.
An equivalent cluster using traditional server hardware would have cost $10–50 million. The military saved 80–95% by shopping at GameStop.
There was one more critical factor: PS3s officially supported Linux via a feature called OtherOS. That meant real kernel access, MPI support, and the ability to build a proper computing cluster. Sony later removed this in a firmware update — which is partly why this trick can never be repeated.
Could you do the same with PS5s today?
No. OtherOS is gone. The PS5's GPU is a standard AMD RDNA2 chip with no open compute stack. And even if you could unlock it, you'd get roughly 10 TFLOPS per unit.
Meanwhile, 9 NVIDIA H100 GPUs (~$350k total) match or exceed the entire Condor Cluster's 500 TFLOPS — with tensor cores, NVLink interconnects, and a mature AI software stack built around them.
The PS3 moment was a perfect storm: a uniquely powerful consumer chip, an open software layer, and a massive price gap versus research hardware.
All three conditions are gone.
What made it genius in 2010 would be pointless in 2025. The best hacks have an expiration date — and that's what makes them worth studying.

We have been thinking about Transformers wrong.
Not wrong in a way that breaks your models. Wrong in a way that hides a deeper structure — one that connects two fields most people treat as completely separate.
A Transformer is a Graph Neural Network.
Specifically, a GNN operating on a complete graph — every token is a node, every node is connected to every other. No missing edges. Fully dense by construction.
Once you see this, self-attention looks completely different.
Self-attention is message passing.
In a standard GNN, each node aggregates information from its neighbors: collect messages, weight them, update your representation.
Self-attention does exactly this — on a graph where every node is a neighbor of every other.
The Query, Key, and Value projections are the message-passing functions. Query asks what a node needs. Key declares what it offers. Value carries the content being passed.
The critical difference: edge weights are not fixed.
Traditional GNNs aggregate with a sum or mean — static operations.
Transformers compute attention scores dynamically: softmax(QKᵀ / √d).
These are data-dependent edge weights, recomputed from scratch for every input.
The graph stays complete. But the effective connectivity is learned at inference time, not baked in.
Multi-head attention is multiple parallel message-passing channels.
Each head runs its own independent aggregation — its own queries, keys, values, its own edge weights. One head might learn syntactic structure. Another, coreference. A third, positional proximity.
Same graph. Different learned aggregation functions running in parallel.
Without positional encodings, a Transformer is permutation-equivariant.
Shuffle the input sequence — you get a shuffled but otherwise identical output. Self-attention on a complete graph has no notion of token order.
Positional encodings fix this by injecting node-level features that encode position — exactly the technique used in GNNs when node identity matters.
The architecture stays the same. You are simply giving each node a feature that says where it sits.
Why does this reframing matter?
It collapses a false boundary.
Over-smoothing in deep GNNs has an analogue in attention collapse. Graph positional encodings map directly to sequence positional encodings. Sparse attention is graph sparsification.
These are not two communities working on different problems. They independently converged on the same primitive and built different vocabularies around it.
If you work with Transformers, you are already doing graph learning.

• accurate legal references
• stable reasoning
• strong cross-document understanding
But when we scaled toward ~200 files, things broke:
• forgotten context
• hallucinated citations
• repeated analysis
• inconsistent drafts

It wasn’t.
It was context degradation.

In 2010, the US Air Force built a supercomputer out of PlayStation 3s. And it ranked 33rd fastest in the world.
This isn't a joke.
The Air Force Research Laboratory (AFRL) assembled the Condor Cluster — 1,716 PS3 consoles running in parallel at Rome Laboratory, New York. Total cost: ~$2 million.
Purpose: processing aerial surveillance imagery and AI research.
So why PS3s?
The PS3's Cell processor was uniquely powerful for parallel computing — roughly 200 GFLOPS per unit. At $250 each, that worked out to ~$1.25 per GFLOP. Comparable research hardware at the time (like the NVIDIA Tesla C1060) cost $19 per GFLOP. The PS3 was 4–10x cheaper than anything else available.
An equivalent cluster using traditional server hardware would have cost $10–50 million. The military saved 80–95% by shopping at GameStop.
There was one more critical factor: PS3s officially supported Linux via a feature called OtherOS. That meant real kernel access, MPI support, and the ability to build a proper computing cluster. Sony later removed this in a firmware update — which is partly why this trick can never be repeated.
Could you do the same with PS5s today?
No. OtherOS is gone. The PS5's GPU is a standard AMD RDNA2 chip with no open compute stack. And even if you could unlock it, you'd get roughly 10 TFLOPS per unit.
Meanwhile, 9 NVIDIA H100 GPUs (~$350k total) match or exceed the entire Condor Cluster's 500 TFLOPS — with tensor cores, NVLink interconnects, and a mature AI software stack built around them.
The PS3 moment was a perfect storm: a uniquely powerful consumer chip, an open software layer, and a massive price gap versus research hardware.
All three conditions are gone.
What made it genius in 2010 would be pointless in 2025. The best hacks have an expiration date — and that's what makes them worth studying.

We have been thinking about Transformers wrong.
Not wrong in a way that breaks your models. Wrong in a way that hides a deeper structure — one that connects two fields most people treat as completely separate.
A Transformer is a Graph Neural Network.
Specifically, a GNN operating on a complete graph — every token is a node, every node is connected to every other. No missing edges. Fully dense by construction.
Once you see this, self-attention looks completely different.
Self-attention is message passing.
In a standard GNN, each node aggregates information from its neighbors: collect messages, weight them, update your representation.
Self-attention does exactly this — on a graph where every node is a neighbor of every other.
The Query, Key, and Value projections are the message-passing functions. Query asks what a node needs. Key declares what it offers. Value carries the content being passed.
The critical difference: edge weights are not fixed.
Traditional GNNs aggregate with a sum or mean — static operations.
Transformers compute attention scores dynamically: softmax(QKᵀ / √d).
These are data-dependent edge weights, recomputed from scratch for every input.
The graph stays complete. But the effective connectivity is learned at inference time, not baked in.
Multi-head attention is multiple parallel message-passing channels.
Each head runs its own independent aggregation — its own queries, keys, values, its own edge weights. One head might learn syntactic structure. Another, coreference. A third, positional proximity.
Same graph. Different learned aggregation functions running in parallel.
Without positional encodings, a Transformer is permutation-equivariant.
Shuffle the input sequence — you get a shuffled but otherwise identical output. Self-attention on a complete graph has no notion of token order.
Positional encodings fix this by injecting node-level features that encode position — exactly the technique used in GNNs when node identity matters.
The architecture stays the same. You are simply giving each node a feature that says where it sits.
Why does this reframing matter?
It collapses a false boundary.
Over-smoothing in deep GNNs has an analogue in attention collapse. Graph positional encodings map directly to sequence positional encodings. Sparse attention is graph sparsification.
These are not two communities working on different problems. They independently converged on the same primitive and built different vocabularies around it.
If you work with Transformers, you are already doing graph learning.

What if the database was already inside the model?
Not a metaphor. A literal question about where knowledge actually lives in a trained transformer.
The standard ML stack splits two things that shouldn't be split:
A database stores facts. A model reasons over them. They talk at query time — stitched together by retrieval layers, embedding lookups, and synchronization logic. That seam is where latency hides, schemas go stale, and pipelines break.
But the seam may be architecturally unnecessary.
A trained LLM is not just a text generator. It is a compressed knowledge graph encoded across billions of weights. When it completes "The capital of France is …", that fact lives as a distributed activation pattern across MLP layers — alongside millions of others. The knowledge is already there. It just has no query interface.
So I built one.
LARQL hooks into every FFN layer during inference, captures neuron activations, and builds a knowledge graph from what actually fires. From there:
INFER "The capital of France is"
DESCRIBE France
NEAREST "France" LIMIT 5
INSERT INTO edges VALUES (Atlantis, capital, Poseidon)
Not a chatbot wrapper. A mechanistic interpretability tool with a database interface — Dockerized, SQL-style REPL, 33 passing unit tests (no GPU required).
Why this matters beyond the demo:
Reasoning and storage in one pass. No round-trip. No cache warming. The forward pass is the retrieval.
Semantic querying without a schema. "FR", "France", "the French Republic" — the model encodes the concept, not the string. Queries work by meaning.
No upfront schema design. "France borders Germany" and "Paris is the capital of France" are both encoded from raw text. No foreign keys. No junction tables.
The honest caveats: generation is probabilistic. Graph-level INSERT does not edit model weights — it overlays the graph, not the parameters. Nearest-neighbour is linear past ~1,000 entities. Known limits, not hidden ones.
This isn't replacing databases. It's recognising that a new layer exists in the stack — one where knowledge, retrieval, and reasoning converge into a single forward pass — and building tooling to work with it explicitly.
The model was always a knowledge system. We just never queried it as one.
