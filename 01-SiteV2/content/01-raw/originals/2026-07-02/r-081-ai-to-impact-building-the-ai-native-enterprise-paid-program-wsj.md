# AI to impact: Building the AI-Native Enterprise - Paid Program - WSJ

## clean_text

AI to impact:
Building the AI-Native Enterprise
Turning AI into durable business value requires a model built on accountability, architecture and enterprise-scale discipline.
As AI systems begin shaping operational outcomes, the design of the enterprise itself becomes the next frontier of transformation that organizations can no longer afford to overlook. According to Yutaka Sasaki, President and CEO of NTT DATA Group , and Bratin Saha, CEO of NTT DATA AIVista , organizations that plan to scale AI effectively must treat intelligence as a core part of their operating model, supported by systems and governance that allow it to operate at speed while maintaining clear accountability. In this conversation, they discuss what it takes to translate AI capability into measurable enterprise impact.
What does it take for a global organization to move toward an AI-native operating model?
Most enterprises have yet to realize AI’s full potential. NTT DATA’s 2026 Global AI Report 1 finds that only 15% qualify as AI leaders with a clear vision and execution strategy. AI is not just a technology; it requires the latest data, integrated business processes and continuous monitoring to build, adopt and scale. Clients must retrain AI systems continuously with the latest data they generate every day.
Companies will introduce AI systems into existing IT environments, and most will need continued support as they integrate and operate across hybrid landscapes. Many will also need to modernize their infrastructure and adopt new technologies to support AI at scale. Enterprises cannot become AI-native through software alone—they require secure infrastructure, scalable compute and disciplined integration.
Last December, we established NTT DATA AIVista in Silicon Valley in response to the rapid evolution of AI technologies and the need to stay close to the most advanced emerging use cases. Starting this year, we are positioning our AI strategy as an execution program focused on measurable business impact. We are confident that AI is moving from isolated productivity tools to something that reshapes how enterprises operate. Although the impact may initially be seen in IT companies, it will soon spread to every enterprise.
1. 2026 Global AI Report: A Playbook for AI Leaders | NTT DATA, sourced from 2,567 C-suite and other senior leaders across 35 markets and 15 industries.
Yutaka Sasaki
President and CEO,
NTT DATA Group
President and CEO,
NTT DATA Group
In enterprises today, the biggest bottleneck is moving from proof-of-concept (PoC) to production at scale. Clients find it is not difficult to build exciting demos, but converting those demos into production workflows that generate real business value is far more challenging. It’s not just a technology problem; clients tell us that operationalizing AI is the real bottleneck, and that is what they need to master to get to an AI-native model.
There are a few things organizations can do to operationalize AI more effectively. One of them is to use platforms for repeatable deployments, rather than developing bespoke approaches for every team and use case. Another is to focus on the right use case to start with and ensure end-to-end deployment in production—realizing value before scaling further. Organizations should also consider change management upfront, since introducing AI will change how people work.
Bratin Saha
CEO,
NTT DATA AIVista

Once an organization commits to becoming AI-native, what does it take to translate capability into sustained business impact?
The promise of AI is that it can emulate how humans work, and we humans learn continuously—especially at work, where we adapt to data every day. That is why every company conducts new employee orientation and regular training, even when hiring from within the same domain. AI must go through that same “last mile specialization”, ingesting all the knowledge in the company’s systems, and continuously evolving. Governance must be built in from the start, and AI needs to be deeply integrated into existing workflows.
When product and platform capabilities are combined with deep domain and workflow expertise, organizations are far more likely to operationalize AI successfully and drive sustained business impact.
B. Saha
In an AI-native enterprise, how must governance evolve to manage risk, performance and accountability?
We should recognize that AI operates as a black box, producing outputs without clearly showing how it arrived at those decisions, which makes disciplined governance essential from the outset. Organizations must know what AI does, why it does it and on whose behalf it acts. To manage that system, three human roles are required.
First, we must qualify the input: clarifying the right questions, context and requirements. Second, we must validate the output to ensure the AI’s results lead to the correct business outcomes. Third, we must govern the total AI system so that it operates in an ethical, reliable and transparent manner, including oversight of its performance, response time, throughput, security and other non-functional qualities.
AI can operate autonomously within defined frameworks, but human oversight remains essential. Humans must oversee input, validate output and manage the system as a whole, including risks such as hallucinations. Without that structure, enterprises will struggle to scale AI beyond pilot use cases and may generate additional risk rather than sustained outcomes.
Y. Sasaki
What makes AI powerful is precisely what makes strong governance important. AI’s strength lies in its ability to learn and adapt dynamically rather than being statically pre-programmed, which also means its outputs can be unpredictable. Execution must be governed and traceable from the outset: can actions taken by the AI system be observed and monitored? Can ownership be clearly ascribed to them? Is there transparency into the AI system’s reasoning, and how can you confirm it is operating in alignment with the company’s vision? If it deviates, how can you install appropriate human oversight?
In addressing these questions, organizations should consider the governance primitives—the built-in rules you want ingrained in every AI system. Those primitives must be invoked across every AI operation in the enterprise, which means governance needs to be structured and systematic. We believe a platform approach is key to enabling governance out of the box, which is essential for operationalizing AI.
B. Saha

How would you describe your organization’s current AI maturity and business impact?
A. AI Leader: achieving significant business value with defined AI capabilities
B. AI Laggard: achieving little business value with early-stage AI capabilities
C. Somewhere in between: AI strategy yet to yield strong business value
Does becoming AI-native require a total redesign of today’s enterprise workflow? How does the relationship between humans and technology change in this context?
To realize the full benefit of AI, organizations need to reimagine workflows rather than simply retrofit them with AI. That said, reimagining can be disruptive and may not be feasible right away. In many situations, you will likely start by automating existing workflows with AI, as a first step toward deeper transformation. Either way, organizations must decide which outcomes they are comfortable delegating to AI and where humans retain oversight. Organizations and employees will also have to reconsider human-technology interaction.
Today, humans handle most information gathering and analysis, but much of that work can and will be delegated to machines. That shifts the human role toward more creative tasks: connecting the dots and filling in missing context. We will also have to redesign the human-machine interface to suit teams with both humans and agents. How will those teams function when we’re used to working only with other humans? How will humans provide the right input, control the outputs and maintain effective oversight?
B. Saha
Many business processes today are still inefficient due to manual coordination across teams. AI has the potential to dramatically improve those processes, but only if we redesign workflows from the ground up rather than applying ad hoc automation.
AI is adept at analyzing and collecting large amounts of information and solving problems when goals are clearly defined. It is far less capable in negotiation or compromise. In the future, multiple AI agents will work together as integrated teams. Unlike human teams, these agents can operate around the clock and autonomously plan and execute end-to-end processes, fundamentally changing the economics of service delivery. Humans must remain in control of the overall system, define the requirements and verify the combined outcomes.
That is why strong architectural discipline becomes essential—not only for business processes, but for the broader systems that support them. Client organizations will need to operate effectively within these more flexible AI systems—including adaptive and probabilistic systems that learn from data and generate context-dependent outputs—as opposed to the rule-based responses of traditional systems. This shift will redefine how IT services create value, moving from scale driven by headcount to scale driven by productivity and outcomes.
Y. Sasaki
