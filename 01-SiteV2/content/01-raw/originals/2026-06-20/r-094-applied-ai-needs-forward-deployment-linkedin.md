# Applied AI Needs Forward Deployment - LinkedIn

## clean_text

Everyone in #AI is suddenly rediscovering the Forward-Deployed Engineer (FDE).
Palantir Technologies
made the model famous, and now every AI company seems to be asking the same question: Is forward deployment the future of AI software or just professional services with a better title?
The answer depends entirely on the architecture behind it.
Old enterprise SaaS often took 6 to 18 months to implement because customers had to translate their messy business reality into the software's rigid parameters. Requirements were gathered, workflows were mapped, integrations were scoped, change requests were filed, and eventually, the system went live months later.
Applied AI should change this, but only if forward deployment is more than humans filling product gaps.
The models from
OpenAI
Anthropic
, and the broader AI ecosystem have made intelligence dramatically more available. But intelligence alone does not turn into enterprise workflow automation. The hard part is still getting AI to work inside messy customer operations: systems, rules, permissions, integrations, exceptions, audit trails, and human review.
If an FDE is just covering for missing product features, the model collapses into services. If an FDE is just a sales engineer with a new title, it becomes pre-sales. If an FDE is only configuring workflows by hand, it becomes implementation labor.
The real leverage comes when the FDE operates an AI-native platform that learns from every deployment. The FDE discovers the customer’s operational reality, models it into structured workflow primitives, uses agents to execute the work, and feeds those learnings back into the platform.
That is when forward deployment becomes the product loop.
The Product Is the Deployment Loop
The last decade of SaaS was built around reducing human involvement in deployment. The best companies were expected to be low-touch, product-led, and scalable without needing large implementation teams. That model worked because many SaaS products helped customers manage workflows they already understood.
Applied AI changes the deployment model because the product is often trying to automate work that has never been fully captured in software. In operational categories, the hard part is more than just handing the customer a tool. It requires a deep understanding of how the work actually happens: which rules matter, which exceptions are safe, which systems are trusted, which handoffs break, and which human workarounds quietly keep the operation running.
That reality rarely lives in one clean system. It lives across SOPs, spreadsheets, Slack threads, emails, vendor portals, compliance rules, manager memory, and thousands of small decisions that were never formally documented. Before AI can automate that work, the system has to discover the operational reality behind it.
This is why forward deployment matters. Real-world operations must be understood before they can be modeled, executed, and improved. In applied AI, deployment is more than a path to go live. It is where the product learns.
Why FirstWork (YC S24) Engineers Think Like FDEs
At Firstwork, forward deployment is an intrinsic component of our product development, not a post-sale add-on.
In our early days, almost every engineer at Firstwork had to think like a forward-deployed engineer. We spent time close to customer workflows, understanding how frontline operations actually happen: where workers drop off, which compliance rules matter, which vendor handoffs break, which exceptions need human judgment, and which parts of the process are only understood by the operators running it every day.
That customer closeness shaped our core architecture. It drove our deep investments in the Object Graph, workflow primitives, rules, integrations, permissions, audit trails, and AI agent infrastructure. We built these layers specifically to allow forward deployment to compound over time.
The goal is to maximize the leverage of every FDE rather than removing them from the customer context. A strong FDE motion should turn repeated customer patterns into reusable product primitives instead of creating bespoke, repetitive work. This ensures that the next deployment starts from a stronger base.
That is the difference between services and software. Services solve a customer’s problem once. A forward-deployed product company turns that solution into infrastructure for the next customer.
Our Technical Thesis: Discover, Model, Execute, Learn
This is the foundation of our technical thesis at Firstwork: Discover, Model, Execute, Learn.
Our Technical Thesis: Discover, Model, Execute, Learn
Discover means FDEs embed with the customer to map the operational ground truth behind worker readiness. In frontline labor, this includes compliance checks, credentialing rules, approval chains, exceptions, vendor dependencies, cross-system handoffs, and the human workarounds that keep the process running.
Model means Firstwork converts that unstructured reality into a structured workflow ontology. The customer’s operating model becomes objects, relationships, rules, readiness states, permissions, integrations, and audit trails. This is where messy frontline operations become software the platform can reason over.
Execute means AI agents run work through those structured primitives. They review documents, trigger recollection, operate portals, update systems, route exceptions, and move workers toward readiness with human-equivalent throughput, while staying inside permissions, rules, tools, and audit boundaries.
Learn means every workflow, exception, integration, and deployment feeds intelligence back into the platform. The system surfaces gaps, recommends improvements, strengthens platform skills, and ensures the next deployment starts from a stronger base.
This is why forward deployment matters for applied AI. It is the core operating loop that lets the product discover reality, model it, execute against it, and learn from it.
The Frontline Test Case
Frontline labor is one of the clearest examples of why this deployment model matters.
Getting a worker from application to their first shift is not a single workflow but a chain of dependent steps across documents, identity, work authorization, background checks, training, scheduling, payroll readiness, compliance rules, vendor systems, and human approvals.
A worker cannot move forward until the right documents are collected. A document cannot pass unless it satisfies the right rule. A rule may depend on location, client, role, or jurisdiction. A background check may depend on information collected earlier. A worker may be ready for one role but blocked for another.
Most software records pieces of this process. But frontline companies need more than a historical record of events. They need to know who can work tomorrow, why someone is blocked, what action will unblock them, what can be automated, and what needs human review.
That is the operational problem Firstwork is built around.
We are not trying to replace every system on day one. We are building the applied AI operating layer that sits across the stack, understands the live worker state, executes the workflow, and makes disparate existing systems work together.
The Only Test That Matters: Does Every Deployment Improve the Platform?
The best applied AI companies will lean into the operational mess to translate it into a scalable product.
The mistake is executing a forward-deployment strategy without a product loop. If every deployment remains custom, you are a services company. If every deployment improves the platform, you are building an applied AI company.
That is the lesson we are learning at Firstwork.
Applied AI needs forward deployment because operational reality cannot be automated from a distance. It has to be discovered, modeled, executed, and learned from.
The best applied AI companies will embed themselves in the field to build the product alongside the customer until every deployment makes the next one faster.
Firstwork brings applied AI & AI agents to frontline enterprises. Our forward-deployed teams map the processes, systems, rules, and exceptions that slow workforce activation, then deploy AI agents to automate the work across them. Starting with worker readiness, Firstwork turns fragmented manual workflows into faster, more visible operating systems. Firstwork is backed by
