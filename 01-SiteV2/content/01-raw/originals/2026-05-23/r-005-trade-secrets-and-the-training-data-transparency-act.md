# Trade secrets and the Training Data Transparency Act

## clean_text

May 18, 2026 - California's AB 2013, also known as the Generative Artificial Intelligence: Training Data Transparency Act (TDTA) was signed into law on September 28, 2024, and took effect on January 1, 2026. No other state has adopted the TDTA. TDTA requires developers of generative AI systems offered to the public in California to publish a "high-level summary" of the datasets used to train ​their models and to update that disclosure as the models change. Developers must publicly post a written "high-level summary" of the training datasets used for each covered generative AI system or service provided to the public since January 2022. The disclosure must be posted on the developer's website before the system is made publicly available and must be updated whenever there is a substantial modification. The categories of information that must be disclosed are: A challenge raised in recent litigation is how to comply with the TDTA without destroying trade secrets. A trade secret is any information that can be used in the operation of a business or other enterprise and that is sufficiently valuable and secret to afford an actual or potential economic advantage over others. On December 29, 2025, X.AI LLC (X.AI or Plaintiff) filed a complaint against the State of California seeking a preliminary injunction to protect the secrecy of its datasets and processes which are closely guarded trade secrets including the datasets used to train AI models, their contents, origins, size, and cleaning methods. X.AI ​LLC v. Bonta, No. CV 25-12295 JGB (SSCx), 2026 LX 151126 (C.D. Cal. Mar. 4, 2026). The district court, applying the requirements for a preliminary injunction, concluded that Plaintiff did not have a likelihood of success on merits because the Plaintiff had failed to prove the existence of trade secrets: "Before the Court can evaluate whether Plaintiff has likely alleged a successful Takings Clause claim, the Court must determine the likelihood of Plaintiff proving that the sources, sizes, and cleaning methods of its datasets qualify as trade secrets." The trial court concluded that the trade secret allegations were too general: Plaintiff offered a variety ​of general allegations about the importance of datasets in developing AI ​models and why they are kept secret. The plaintiff pointed ⁠out that if one company does not use a dataset used by another, the AI model may not be as well trained. If xAI reveals all the datasets that it uses, its competitors would immediately move to acquire those resources to ensure their models were equally effective. It is the differences between the datasets each company uses that gives xAI a competitive advantage. If competitors could see the ​sources of all of xAI's datasets or even the size of its dataset, competitors could evaluate both what data xAI has and how much they lack. Despite this showing, the district court concluded: "Plaintiff ​has made general arguments about datasets, ⁠dataset sizes, cleaning methods, and configurations of data, but has not identified any dataset or approach to cleaning and using datasets that is distinct from its competitors in a manner warranting trade secret protection." The district court concluded: "Plaintiff has failed at this stage to sufficiently allege that trade secrets are ⁠implicated.
