When I built the ingestion pipeline for my support automation system, I went with fixed-size chunks: 300 characters, with a 50-character overlap between consecutive chunks.
When I built the ingestion pipeline for my support automation system, I went with fixed-size chunks: 300 characters, with a 50-character overlap between consecutive chunks.
Why overlap matters: if you split documents into clean, non-overlapping blocks, you risk cutting a sentence — or the answer to a question — right at the boundary. The information that would've answered the query ends up split across two chunks, and neither one scores well enough to get retrieved. Overlap means the same sentence often appears in two chunks, so it has two chances to be retrieved.
Why 300 characters: small enough that each chunk stays focused on one idea (better embedding quality, more precise retrieval), but large enough to retain context — too small and you retrieve fragments with no meaning on their own.
The tradeoff I'm aware of: fixed-size chunking is simple and predictable, but it doesn't respect semantic boundaries — it can still split a paragraph mid-thought if you're unlucky with where 300 characters lands. Semantic chunking (splitting by meaning, not character count) solves this but adds complexity and cost.
For a knowledge base of short, structured docs like support articles, fixed-size with overlap was the simpler, "good enough" choice. For longer, denser documents, I'd lean toward semantic chunking.
Small decision, but it's the foundation everything else in RAG sits on — bad chunks mean bad retrieval, no matter how good your LLM is.
