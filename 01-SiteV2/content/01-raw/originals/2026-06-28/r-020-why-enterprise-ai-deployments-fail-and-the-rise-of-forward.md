# Why Enterprise AI Deployments Fail and the Rise of Forward ...

## clean_text

Why did OpenAI launch a consulting firm?
Why did OpenAI launch a consulting firm? Why is Anthropic partnering with megafunds to launch an Enterprise AI services firm?
We've moved from AI bottleneck to AI bottleneck in a frenzied pace over the last three years:
First, the models. Can they reason, code, and stop hallucinating.
Then compute. Can we get enough GPUs.
Then semiconductors. Can Nvidia & TSMC keep up.
Now power and data centers. Can the grid, land, cooling, permitting, and capex support AI infrastructure (still lots to do there)
What's the next frontier?
Deployment.
Engineers build, salespeople sell, but who owns and implements?
Who's helping companies rewire entire workflows?
Who's helping employees rethink how they work?
Kiss goodbye to all those theoretical EBITDA improvements if change management fails. And failure modes are plenty:
- Passive resistance to change (or active undermining) by employees fearing they're being replaced by AI
- Internal politics over who owns the AI transformation
- Budget fights over who pays for implementation and who gets credit for the savings
- Middle managers resisting because AI threatens headcount, control, or status
- Broken workflows that companies try to “AI-enable” instead of redesigning
- Messy data, fragmented systems, and tribal knowledge buried in emails and spreadsheets
- Legal, compliance, and security teams that slow deployment because the risks are real
- Pilot purgatory (dozens of demos, very few production workflows)
- No clear owner for end-to-end process change
- No credible way to measure whether AI actually improved revenue, margins, speed, or quality
What kinds of historical parallels do we have of company-wide transformations? I can think of two:
1. Multi-year SAP/Oracle rollouts of the 2000s and 2010s.
2. Major M&A deals.
55% to 75% of ERP implementations have historically failed to meet their intended objectives (Gartner).
70% to 90% of mergers and acquisitions fail (HBR).
Why would enterprise-wide AI deployments be any easier? More importantly, how can it succeed?
That's probably why Forward Deployed Engineers are becoming one of the most important talent categories in AI. The FDE profile is still being defined, but we often see:
A) Former PMs
B) Management consultants with CS degrees
C) Software engineers who are commercial/consultative enough to sell, scope, and sit with customers.
If I had to guess, consulting firms will end up building massive AI implementation practices. This will take time and require AI-native talent.
If I were in college again and debating between engineering and business careers, I’d likely get a CS minor at a minimum, and pursue AI advisory roles at consulting firms or major labs’ consulting/implementation arms.
The future belongs to AI-native orthogonal thinkers.
Meanwhile, many of the world's best investment firms are hiring junior FDEs for themselves, and those roles are becoming some of the best-paid jobs in the market. David C. has many such openings.
