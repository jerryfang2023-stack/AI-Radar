🚀 Blue Origin 's launch pad exploded last week. Most people assumed New Glenn wouldn’t fly again until 2027.
Blue Origin says they’ll be back before the end of 2026.
Whether they hit that target or not almost misses the bigger lesson.
🛠️ The most interesting part of this story isn’t the explosion.
It’s the response.
After a highly visible failure, the team immediately shifted into assessment mode:
• What infrastructure survived?
• What hardware can be reused?
• What needs redesign?
• How quickly can we safely return to operation?
That’s not just aerospace.
That’s leadership.
📉 We often celebrate successful launches.
But organizations reveal their true operating model after something goes wrong.
Failure exposes:
• Decision-making speed
• Organizational resilience
• Technical discipline
• Leadership confidence
Anyone can manage success.
Recovery is harder.
🎯 There’s also a lesson here for AI initiatives.
Many AI pilots, agent deployments, and transformation efforts will fail their first test.
Some will produce bad outputs.
Some will break workflows.
Some will create more work than they eliminate.
The organizations that win won’t be the ones that avoid failure.
They’ll be the ones that learn, adapt, and redeploy faster than everyone else.
🚀 Space companies call this return-to-flight.
Maybe enterprises need a similar concept for AI.
Because the question isn’t whether something will fail.
The question is how quickly your organization can learn from it and get back into operation.
That’s increasingly becoming a competitive advantage.
What do you think is harder: launching successfully the first time, or recovering quickly after a public failure?

🚀 A rocket exploded. The internet got its headlines.
But what caught my attention wasn't the failure itself.
On May 28, Blue Origin's New Glenn rocket — backed by Jeff Bezos — suffered a catastrophic explosion during a hot-fire test in Florida. Fortunately, no injuries were reported, but years of engineering effort went up in flames within seconds.
As software engineers, product builders, and tech professionals, it's easy to celebrate successful launches.
The production deployment that goes perfectly.
The feature that scales to millions.
The startup that suddenly becomes a unicorn.
What we rarely see are the thousands of failed tests behind those moments.
Space companies don't test because they expect failure.
They test because they expect reality.
And reality always finds the assumptions you missed.
Whether it's:
• A rocket engine on a launch pad
• A distributed system under peak traffic
• A new feature released to production
The goal isn't to avoid every failure.
The goal is to fail in an environment where you can learn, recover, and come back stronger.
SpaceX exploded rockets.
Blue Origin exploded rockets.
Even NASA's history is filled with setbacks before breakthroughs.
Innovation has never been a straight line.
Sometimes progress looks like a successful launch.
Sometimes progress looks like a giant fireball that teaches you something your simulations couldn't.
The teams that win are usually not the teams that never fail.
They're the teams that keep building after failure.

🚀 Blue Origin 's launch pad exploded last week. Most people assumed New Glenn wouldn’t fly again until 2027.
Blue Origin says they’ll be back before the end of 2026.
Whether they hit that target or not almost misses the bigger lesson.
🛠️ The most interesting part of this story isn’t the explosion.
It’s the response.
After a highly visible failure, the team immediately shifted into assessment mode:
• What infrastructure survived?
• What hardware can be reused?
• What needs redesign?
• How quickly can we safely return to operation?
That’s not just aerospace.
That’s leadership.
📉 We often celebrate successful launches.
But organizations reveal their true operating model after something goes wrong.
Failure exposes:
• Decision-making speed
• Organizational resilience
• Technical discipline
• Leadership confidence
Anyone can manage success.
Recovery is harder.
🎯 There’s also a lesson here for AI initiatives.
Many AI pilots, agent deployments, and transformation efforts will fail their first test.
Some will produce bad outputs.
Some will break workflows.
Some will create more work than they eliminate.
The organizations that win won’t be the ones that avoid failure.
They’ll be the ones that learn, adapt, and redeploy faster than everyone else.
🚀 Space companies call this return-to-flight.
Maybe enterprises need a similar concept for AI.
Because the question isn’t whether something will fail.
The question is how quickly your organization can learn from it and get back into operation.
That’s increasingly becoming a competitive advantage.
What do you think is harder: launching successfully the first time, or recovering quickly after a public failure?
