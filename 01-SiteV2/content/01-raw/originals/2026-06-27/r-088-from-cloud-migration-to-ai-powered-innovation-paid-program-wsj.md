# From Cloud Migration to AI-Powered Innovation - Paid Program - WSJ

## clean_text

Quick Summary

Organizations often treat cloud migration as a logistical infrastructure task rather than a strategic opportunity to modernize workflows and eliminate on-premises constraints.
Adopting cloud-native architectures enables AI to deliver real-time insights, automate decisions, and help teams act faster at scale.
Through a strategic partnership with AWS, Atlassian enables enterprises to centralize fragmented systems and automate complex workflows to reduce costs and accelerate decision-making.
An artificial-intelligence tool created this summary, which was based on the text of the article and checked by a Custom Content from WSJ editor.
Bill Hustad, Head of GTM Ecosystems, Atlassian
Chris Casey, Managing Director ANZ, AWS
Enterprise buyers now evaluate technology through an AI-first lens. Cloud is no longer just infrastructure. It’s the prerequisite for delivering the intelligent, automated experiences they expect. The partnerships that win will be those that combine platform depth with AI-native integrations that drive productivity from day one.
The 15-year alliance between Atlassian and AWS was built on moving businesses to the cloud. Now, with AI reshaping enterprise operations, that migration isn’t optional. It’s existential. Bill Hustad, Atlassian’s head of GTM ecosystems, and Chris Casey, AWS’s managing director ANZ, explain what’s changed.
Q: Cloud migration can feel like a massive undertaking for the enterprise. What’s the case for why it’s worth it?
Bill Hustad: For me, migration implies a lift-and-shift move of what you have today into the cloud. But what we’re doing is a transformation. We’re giving customers the opportunity to fundamentally reimagine how they work with AI-powered collaboration, deeper integrations, and capabilities that simply can’t exist on-prem.
We surveyed 500 executives in our customer base, and the signal is clear: Organizations are seeing 55% greater scalability in the cloud, plus measurable gains in reliability, agility, and cost efficiency. But what’s really accelerating the conversation is AI. More than half of these executives say AI is now a leading reason to migrate.
Cloud also mitigates infrastructure constraints. Just imagine: As we see with AI models and how they scale, having that kind of infinite capacity versus having to procure, build, configure, and manage internally allows companies to focus on what they do best.
We saw this with Delivery Hero, a food-delivery company based in Germany. We worked with them closely alongside AWS, and after moving to cloud, we centralized 40,000 employees on a single system of work. That resulted in a 69% cost reduction in the first three years, and over 1,000 annual maintenance hours redirected to higher-value work.
Q: Why is cloud migration the right starting point when it comes to making the most of AI?
Chris Casey: Cloud migration is the foundation for AI adoption because the technology requires three things that on-premises infrastructure struggles to provide: massive computational power, access to the latest AI models, and the ability to scale dynamically. On-premises, you’re still building infrastructure. In AI, speed matters. And the scale of this shift is still very early, as 85% of global IT spend is still on-premises. The organizations that move now will have an AI-ready foundation. To put it into perspective, we’re seeing AI moving up to 10 times faster than electricity did when it reorganized every factory, home, and industry.
A concrete example of our AI story coming together for customers is the integration of Rovo, Atlassian’s generative AI, with Amazon Quick, our business and intelligence automation platform. When knowledge is available right where people work, they make decisions faster and collaborate more easily. It improves what we’d call “decision-making velocity.”
Q: After an organization migrates, what does that transformation actually look like for teams on a day-to-day basis?
Hustad: The big shift is moving from reactive models to proactive ones. Real-time insights give teams continuous visibility: live dashboards showing project status, velocity, blockers, and dependencies across the entire organization. But more than that, you can start to look into the future. You can see bottlenecks forming across different projects before they become critical.
The other side is risk identification: automatically surfacing things like a missed deadline that will have follow-on effects to other projects, or resource constraints being managed separately from the project itself. Tying those things together gives executives much better visibility to make decisions before they become critical issues.
Ultimately, there are fewer assumptions, more facts, and better visibility into where you’re going and why. That’s what allows teams to plan more effectively and deliver much faster.
Q: With both AWS and Atlassian making significant investments in AI, how is this partnership specifically accelerating AI adoption and delivering tangible value for enterprise customers?
Hustad: A major problem we see holding up AI adoption is something we call the “coordination tax.” Many organizations will have deployed AI tools and seen subsequent productivity gains on a person-by-person basis. But a lot of the time, that individual uplift never translates to business-wide ROI.
It’s a structural problem; not a lack of effort, organization, or vision. It stems from AI being deployed on the wrong infrastructure or a disconnected stack, which forces workers to spend more time on coordination than execution. Hence, “the coordination tax.”
Our partnership creates tangible value by eliminating this “tax.” Atlassian’s Rovo AI is powered by the Teamwork Graph, our unified data intelligence layer that connects across an organization’s entire toolset. When run on AWS’s secure and scalable enterprise-grade infrastructure, organizations can utilize agentic, connected intelligence that helps them do more and collaborate with less friction.
That means less time spent on coordination, searching, aligning, and context switching, and more time on the high-impact work that drives business outcomes. From software delivery to IT service management and strategic planning, Atlassian and AWS are making AI-native teamwork the new standard for the enterprise.
Casey: From our perspective, the growth and breadth of innovation we are seeing from AWS customers is unprecedented. We have the trust of millions of active customers, including the most security-sensitive organizations, as AWS delivers the functionality, security, and operational performance enterprises need. That’s the foundation Atlassian leverages when it builds AI apps with Amazon Bedrock: Enterprise customers get the security and scale they need without compromise.
Q: Looking ahead, how will AWS and Atlassian’s partnership evolve to handle the increasing complexity of software development and service management?
Casey: The future centers on three areas. First, AI-native workflows: We’re moving beyond AI as a feature to AI as the foundation, with intelligent agents that can automate complex workflows, predict project outcomes, and provide recommendations across the entire development lifecycle.
Second, deeper platform integration, making it seamless to move from planning to deployment to operations without context switching. And third, enterprise-scale automation: As software development grows more complex, we’re investing in automation that can handle multiteam coordination, dependency management, and risk mitigation across organizations of different sizes and operating in diverse industries.
Our goal is to ensure that as complexity increases, the tools teams use become simpler, more intelligent, and more powerful. That’s why we’re investing $200 billion in infrastructure in 2026, which is already backed by real customer commitments. The investment also ensures our partners like Atlassian can build on a strong foundation that scales ahead of demand.
Start your AI transformation today with AWS and Atlassian.
