Lovable ’s deal with Google Cloud may look like just another headline about a fast-growing AI startup, but it says a lot about where the AI product market is moving.
At the early stage, an impressive demo was enough: an agent writes code, builds an interface, and speeds up development. The product is easy to explain, easy to sell to early users, and easy to turn into a viral case. But once it enters the enterprise segment, the evaluation logic changes. A large company immediately looks at whether the product can run reliably inside its infrastructure, pass security review, fit into existing processes, and scale with predictable economics.
This deal shows how AI products are moving from demonstrating capabilities to building a full operating model. When a system relies on multiple LLMs, the team has to manage model selection for each task, response latency, inference cost, usage limits, fallback scenarios, and output quality across different use cases. In practice, this becomes an operational architecture where every product decision has an engineering cost, and every engineering constraint directly affects adoption.
In this context, the partnership with Google Cloud looks like more than an infrastructure deal. It gives Lovable a clearer path into enterprise: compute, distribution, procurement routes, integrations with the corporate stack, and a more familiar security perimeter for large customers. For an AI startup, this primarily reduces implementation risk in the buyer’s eyes.
There is also an important shift happening in the vibe-coding category. Tools that started as a fast way to build prototypes are gradually moving into production workflows. That brings a different level of requirements: code quality, change control, agent auditability, security, and decision transparency become part of the product’s value, rather than additional “enterprise features.”
I think the next phase of the market will be shaped by teams that know how to turn models into reliable products. Demos still matter because they open the door. But long-term value comes from products that can pass security review, fit into unit economics, and withstand real production workflows. That intersection of LLM engineering, infrastructure economics, and understanding the enterprise buyer is where AI leadership will be defined.

I keep seeing the same pattern. A company spends 18 months deploying a major SaaS AI platform. Then the vendor changes their pricing model.
This isn't hypothetical. OpenAI raised enterprise API pricing twice in 2024. Anthropic repriced with each major Claude release. When your entire AI stack is built on a single vendor's API, every pricing update is your problem — with no leverage and nowhere to go.
The sales pitch calls it "partnership." What it actually means: you build workflows on their infrastructure, your operations become dependencies, and when terms change, you migrate or pay.
Forrester found that 67% of enterprise software migrations take 18+ months and cost 3–5× the original contract value. The cost of switching AI vendors isn't just the new contract. It's rebuilding every integration, retraining every user, and explaining to the board why the platform you presented as a competitive advantage is now a liability.
The most expensive AI decision an enterprise can make is optimizing for speed-to-deploy over total cost of ownership.
Infrastructure you own doesn't renegotiate. It doesn't issue a "pricing update effective next quarter." It doesn't discontinue the model your workflows depend on.
The enterprises building durable AI advantage in 2026 are the ones who treated the infrastructure question as a strategic one — not a procurement one.

What happens when software buyers stop searching… and start delegating?
That question came up repeatedly across conversations at the Sydney summits this last week.
So I’m sharing 3 observations from the week that I think matter for software vendors, partners, and marketplaces.
Learning #1: the future is agentic.
Shared the stage with Jamie Kim at AWS Partner Summit Sydney discussing how AI agents are changing software discovery, evaluation, and procurement.
The signals are getting hard to ignore.
Recent G2 research shows:
• 51% of B2B software buyers now start software research with AI chatbots more often than Google
• 69% chose a different vendor than they originally planned based on AI guidance
• 33% purchased from a vendor they had never heard of before
• 83% said AI made them more confident in their final decision
• AI chatbots are now the #1 influence on vendor shortlists
That changes the rules.
Winning becomes less about who has the loudest brand… and more about who is machine-readable, transactable, and easy to govern.
Why marketplaces are well positioned:
✅ Structured metadata wins
AI agents can interpret integrations, compatibility, deployment models, pricing structures, and procurement paths better than static websites.
✅ Procurement speed becomes critical
Discovery means little if the transaction is slow. AWS Marketplace reduces friction through existing cloud procurement and billing workflows.
✅ Ecosystems outperform standalone tools
Agentic buying increasingly favors integrated multi-vendor outcomes over isolated products.
✅ Governance matters
Enterprises will want autonomous procurement happening inside trusted, policy-controlled environments.
And this gets even more interesting.
AWS selecting Coinbase wallet infrastructure + x402 protocol for Amazon Bedrock AgentCore hints at where this could go next: sub-second, sub-cent machine-to-machine transactions at enterprise scale.
That future may arrive faster than most expect.
Agent Mode + Express Private Offers + Multi-Product Solutions are part of building for that world.
Learning #2 tomorrow.

Agentic AI deployments are quietly building toward the same problem many companies ran into during the SaaS boom.
Back then, teams adopted best-in-class tools and wired them together. The switching costs became painful years later with pricing changes, acquisitions, and workflows nobody fully understood anymore.
The same pattern is emerging in AI orchestration at a faster rate. If your agents rely on a proprietary orchestration layer, the dependency compounds over time. State management, memory, retries, escalation logic, approvals, tool integrations, and observability accumulate around one provider's model of how workflows should run.
A Zapier survey published in April found 3 in 4 of enterprises are concerned about their dependency on AI vendors. While many companies are confident they can quickly switch AI vendors, nearly 60% who have tried found it much more difficult to switch than anticipated.
Open standards help. MCP is a meaningful step forward at the model interface layer.
But orchestration lock-in is different. Your workflow logic still must live somewhere. The longer running and more operationally critical the process becomes, the more that decision matters.
Teams should consider orchestration as infrastructure they control, not a SaaS feature sitting behind an API.
Where you run your workflows matters as much as how you design them.

Agentic AI deployments are quietly building toward the same problem many companies ran into during the SaaS boom.
Back then, teams adopted best-in-class tools and wired them together. The switching costs became painful years later with pricing changes, acquisitions, and workflows nobody fully understood anymore.
The same pattern is emerging in AI orchestration at a faster rate. If your agents rely on a proprietary orchestration layer, the dependency compounds over time. State management, memory, retries, escalation logic, approvals, tool integrations, and observability accumulate around one provider's model of how workflows should run.
A Zapier survey published in April found 3 in 4 of enterprises are concerned about their dependency on AI vendors. While many companies are confident they can quickly switch AI vendors, nearly 60% who have tried found it much more difficult to switch than anticipated.
Open standards help. MCP is a meaningful step forward at the model interface layer.
But orchestration lock-in is different. Your workflow logic still must live somewhere. The longer running and more operationally critical the process becomes, the more that decision matters.
Teams should consider orchestration as infrastructure they control, not a SaaS feature sitting behind an API.
Where you run your workflows matters as much as how you design them.
