# Brijesh Deb - Is AI Profitable Yet? - LinkedIn

## clean_text

For sometime now, I have been talking about narratives and for the past two years, one of the dominant narratives surrounding AI has been that it will reduce costs.
For sometime now, I have been talking about narratives and for the past two years, one of the dominant narratives surrounding AI has been that it will reduce costs. Automate enough work, increase productivity, and the financial benefits will naturally follow.
Recent reports suggest the reality may be different to the perception and perhaps more complicated. Companies such as Microsoft and Uber are reportedly finding that large scale AI adoption comes with significant costs of its own. While Microsoft is cancelling Claude licences, Uber saw their 2026 AI budget consumed in just 4 months. A key Nvidia executive recently made a similar observation, stating that AI is not reducing labor costs today and is, in many cases, more expensive than the human workers companies already have.
What makes this interesting is that these are not examples of failed adoption. They are supposedly the examples of successful adoption. Employees are seemingly using the tools, teams are seemingly integrating AI into daily work, and agentic workflows are apparently becoming more common. The challenge is that every prompt, inference, retrieval operation, and agent interaction consumes compute, infrastructure, governance, and operational resources.
That creates a paradox many organizations may not have anticipated. Success in AI adoption can increase costs just as easily as it can increase productivity.
While reading about this, I came across a website called Is AI Profitable Yet? (isaiprofitable.com) thanks to Maaike Brinkhof. Whether one agrees with every assumption behind its calculations is almost beside the point. The value of the site is that it encourages a question many organizations are only beginning to ask.
Are we measuring AI value, or are we measuring AI activity?
Prompts, agents, tokens, and adoption rates tell us AI is being used. They do not necessarily tell us that AI is creating meaningful business outcomes. Businesses ultimately care about revenue, customer trust, operational efficiency, risk reduction, decision quality, and sustainable growth.
The first phase of the AI conversation was about capability. The next phase may be about accountability. Was the outcome worth the cost? Can the value be demonstrated? Can the investment be justified?
Those are not anti AI questions. They are the same questions every serious business eventually asks of every serious investment.
