# Why the AI PC Is the Front Line of Enterprise Security - Paid Program

## clean_text

Why the AI PC Is the Front Line of Enterprise Security
As AI moves onto enterprise devices, security shifts from centralized defenses to distributed trust rooted at the endpoint.
AI’s growth is taking it from centralized systems into everyday workflows, and the enterprise endpoint is taking on a new role—and new risks. AI PCs are increasingly where sensitive data is accessed, processed and acted on in real time. That shift is forcing security leaders to rethink long-standing assumptions about trust, data protection and resilience at the device level.
In this interview, Meghana Patwardhan, corporate vice president of product management, client business unit at AMD, discusses how enterprises are rethinking security as an architectural foundation rather than a compliance afterthought, and how hardware-rooted trust, confidential computing and on-device AI are enabling safer, faster AI deployment.
Q:
How does the rise of AI at the endpoint change the way enterprises should think about security, compared with more centralized AI deployments?
Meghana Patwardhan: For much of the history of personal computing, the endpoint was seen as the easiest target to go after. It used to be the leading cause of breaches. While that’s still somewhat true, hardware security has evolved significantly. We’ve seen a massive shift in enforcing trust, alongside the emergence of use cases where customers can run models and perform inference locally. This changes the game. Traditionally, security is located where protection is needed and along the channels where data passes.
When AI runs in a data center or the cloud, protections are built around those hubs. But when AI moves to the endpoint, security becomes distributed. Enterprises must treat these devices as a natural extension of their zero-trust policies. They need to understand the value of security that is designed in via hardware-enforced trust, rather than software layered on as an afterthought. Leaders must move away from the centralized “moat” concept and embrace distributed security because, quite frankly, there are no more moats.
The upside? AI at the endpoint also offers an opportunity to detect threats locally and respond instantly.
Q:
On-device AI is often positioned as a way to keep sensitive data local. In practical terms, how does local inference change the balance between productivity, privacy and risk?
Patwardhan: The main drivers for on-device AI are increasing inference speed while keeping data local and costs down.
From a privacy standpoint, sensitive data is processed where it’s created. This reduces exposure because less data is moving back and forth, effectively creating a smaller “blast radius.” On the productivity side, you get fast responses and less dependence on connectivity; in many cases, you don’t even need to be online.
However, there’s a balance. On-device models generally aren’t as capable as cloud-hosted AI. If you’re running heavy AI workloads that require billions of tokens, the cloud is still the better option. Local AI doesn’t eliminate risk; it shifts it. Instead of managing concentrated risk in one cloud environment, organizations must now manage distributed risk across every endpoint, ensuring device integrity and secure model updates.
Q:
When evaluating AI-ready PCs, what should IT leaders look for to ensure security is built into the platform rather than added later?
Patwardhan: I look for four key elements.
First, security must be foundational, anchored in silicon—not an afterthought. It has to be a continuous chain of trust anchored in hardware. This means utilizing secure boot and device attestation to verify firmware and drivers before AI workloads even run. If the platform can’t prove its integrity at startup and during runtime, everything running on top of it is built on assumptions rather than deep trust.
Second, leaders need to evaluate how AI workloads are isolated and governed. Models, data and inference outputs should run in protected environments, isolated from the operating system layer. This ensures that if one area is compromised, it doesn’t cascade across the whole system. This is where technologies like confidential compute become essential to create secure enclaves for AI.
Third is continuous security. A truly secure platform will have built-in mechanisms—like Shadow Stack or Memory Guard—to protect against memory-based attacks and secure data while in use.
Finally, visibility and observability are key. PCs must be able to report their security and AI state to your existing enterprise tools, allowing security teams to enforce policies effectively.
Q:
AMD often talks about “security for AI” and “AI for security.” How do AMD AI PC technologies support both protecting AI workloads and using AI to strengthen endpoint security?
Patwardhan: We view these as two sides of the same strategy.
“Security for AI” is about the defensive infrastructure. This is where confidential computing is critical—it creates a secure enclave where models and data are isolated and encrypted in memory. Even if the OS is compromised, that IP should remain inaccessible. It essentially extends zero-trust principles right down to the silicon.
“AI for security” is about using technology to improve outcomes. With the neural processing unit, the PC can continuously monitor system behavior, detect anomalies and respond in real time—without relying on the cloud.
You have hardware protecting the workloads, and the AI helping to protect the device. They reinforce each other.
