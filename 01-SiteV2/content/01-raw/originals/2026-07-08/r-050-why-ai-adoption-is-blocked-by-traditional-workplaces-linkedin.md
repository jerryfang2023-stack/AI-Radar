Today we announce that AIX Ventures has led the Series A of Workhelix .
Every enterprise must have an AI transformation war room.
Deploying AI will lead to pricing power. When productivity improves through AI, gross- and operating- margins go up. This means price can decrease, bringing more demand and putting the competition on notice.
This isn't an optional transformation. It’s a race. Every enterprise AI transformation war room needs to be staffed with a team that understands the intricacies of the particular enterprise, the data behind the enterprise, economics, and the ability to lead the enterprise through the transformation. And the team needs to have an AI transformation software suite custom to their enterprise, to maximize transformation velocity.
Enter James Milin , Daniel Rock , Andrew McAfee , Erik Brynjolfsson , and Workhelix team. The Workhelix team deploys engineers trained on the Workhelix task-based approach to understand the tasks being completed, specific to a particular enterprise. The engineers partner with the enterprise’s leaders to assess these tasks, and to map bundles of tasks to GenAI solutions.
The war room team members can then partner with various organizations within the enterprise to implement, monitor solution efficacy, and reassess. An effective war room should have every organization engaged in this process, iterating to the best version of that organization within the context of the bleeding edge Gen AI solutions today.
Today AIX Ventures is leading the Workhelix $15 million Series A, along with Andrew Ng 's AI Fund , Accenture Technology Ventures BV, Reid Hoffman , Jeff Dean , Yann LeCun , Sebastian Thrun , Paul Daugherty , Mira Murati, and Jeff Wilke.

Zain Kahn 's Superhuman did a nice summary of a very informing AI study by Andreessen Horowitz on Startup spends. Worth the read.
Venture Capital firm Andreessen Horowitz’s first AI Application Spending Report reveals where early-stage companies are putting their dollars. Using transaction data from Mercury, the report lists the top 50 AI tools startups can’t live without — and some surprise products made the list.
Experimentation is everywhere. Startups are testing multiple note-takers, coding assistants, and productivity apps at once, picking “their own flavor” of tools. New apps rise and fall at breakneck speed. “There’s a proliferation of tools,” says a16z partner Seema Amble. “It hasn’t just coalesced around one or two in each category.”
Copilots are leading the way. Despite the hype around autonomous AI agents, startups are still spending on AI tools that assist employees, helping them work faster and make smarter decisions rather than replacing them. Amble expects a gradual shift toward end-to-end AI agents as workflows mature.
The current frontrunners: OpenAI tops the list, followed by Anthropic. Vibe-coding tools like Replit and Cursor rank high while creative tools such as CapCut, Canva, Midjourney, Freepik, and ElevenLabs are also making waves — showing how quickly the line between consumer and enterprise in AI is blurring.

Show Me the Money in AI: Where the Real Value Lies in the AI Gold Rush
Beyond the Hype: Following the Innovation Rails
As the world races to build smarter AI models, billions are being poured into infrastructure—from chips to data centers—while questions mount over where the true economic value lies. According to venture capitalist Rohini Chakravarthy of NewBuild Venture Capital, the answer may not be in the models themselves, but in the “innovation rails”—the infrastructure and services that enable scalable, profitable AI deployment.
Key Insights
• The AI Value Chain Defined:
The AI ecosystem spans raw compute and data, model training, application development, and end-user delivery. Each layer transforms technical capability into business utility, but not all layers capture equal value.
• Infrastructure Is the Foundation:
Companies such as Nvidia, Oracle, and AWS dominate the hardware and cloud layer, providing the “rails” for AI deployment. Their execution capabilities and consumption-based models make them the early winners in monetization.
• Middle Layers Hold the Promise:
Between foundational models and end-user apps lies a lucrative middle ground—platforms that abstract complexity, offering APIs, orchestration tools, and plug-and-play AI functions for enterprises.
• Applications Face Pressure:
Many GenAI startups focused solely on chatbots or image tools are struggling with differentiation and cost efficiency. Their success increasingly depends on how well they leverage infrastructure partners and manage compute economics.
• Value Creation Shifts to Enablement:
As Chakravarthy notes, sustainable returns will come from “companies that make AI practical”—those that bridge innovation to execution, not just generate headlines.
Why It Matters
The next AI boom will favor builders of rails, not just riders. Investors and enterprises seeking lasting value must look beyond flashy applications to the infrastructure and integration layers powering them. In the evolving AI economy, profitability belongs to those who control the flow of intelligence—not just the output.
