Backed by Anthropic, OpenAI, and Meta insiders, Bespoke Labs raises $40M: can better environments beat bigger models?
by Abhinaya Prabhu
July 7, 2026
3 minute read
Image credits: Bespoke Labs
Total
Shares
Bespoke Labs has raised $40 million across its seed and Series A rounds, with the Series A led by Wing VC and participation from Mayfield, The House Fund, 8VC, and AI leaders from Anthropic, OpenAI and Meta.
The company develops realistic enterprise environments where agents learn long-horizon tasks before deployment.
As enterprises race to deploy autonomous AI, investors are increasingly backing the infrastructure that trains and evaluates AI agents.
The engineers building some of the industry’s most closely watched AI models are personally backing a 40-person startup that does something almost the opposite of their day jobs: making agents fail, safely, over and over again.
That startup is Bespoke Labs , and it announced on July 6, 2026, that it had raised $40 million across a seed and Series A round. The Series A was led by Wing VC, with participation from Mayfield, The House Fund, Tristan Handy, dbt Labs’ chief executive, and individual angel investors who work at Anthropic, OpenAI, and Meta.
The earlier seed was led by 8VC, with Google DeepMind’s Jeff Dean, Dheeraj Pandey, DevRev’s chief executive, and Spiros Xanthos, Resolve AI’s chief executive, among the backers.
“Frontier labs, enterprises, and all organisations relying on reliable agents need access to high-quality environments. This is the critical piece needed to optimise and develop agents,” says Mahesh Sathiamoorthy , co-founder and CEO of Bespoke Labs.
Why models alone aren’t enough
Founded in 2024 by Sathiamoorthy and Alex Dimakis , Bespoke Labs is based in Mountain View, California, and employs roughly 40-48 people. It builds simulated enterprise worlds that agents train in, such as large codebases, Slack threads, tickets, emails, and operational logs, designed to look and behave like a real company.
The idea is that an agent that only ever sees clean benchmark tasks never learns to survive a messy, multi-day workflow. For example, an agent tasked with resolving a customer complaint that spans three internal systems and a week of Slack history needs a realistic mess to practice on, not a tidy prompt.
That distinction matters because of what independent research is showing. METR has found that the length of tasks AI agents can complete has been doubling roughly every 7 months for 6 years, and more recent analysis suggests that pace has accelerated to every 4 months. Bigger models alone are not producing that gain; training environments are increasingly the bottleneck.
A crowded infrastructure race
Competitors are circling the same problem from different angles. Scale AI, whose $14.3 billion investment from Meta valued it near $29 billion, supplies labelled data rather than interactive training environments. Poolside raised $500 million at a $3 billion valuation to build coding-focused foundation models. Reflection AI raised $130 million in seed funding for autonomous software engineering agents.
While those companies focus on models or datasets, Bespoke Labs stands out by selling the practice grounds where agents train, positioning itself as infrastructure rather than a rival model maker.
Wing VC’s portfolio includes Cohesity , Gong , and Snowflake , giving the firm a track record investors will recognise in enterprise infrastructure deals.
“As frontier labs and AI-native enterprises push the boundaries of long-horizon agentic capabilities, a new generation of data and training infrastructure is required,” says Peter Wagner, founding partner of Wing VC.
The company frames itself as much a research lab as a startup: its team is a core contributor to Terminal-Bench, a widely cited agent benchmark, and helped launch OpenThoughts, an open reasoning dataset collaboration involving Stanford, UC Berkeley, and other academic partners.
The bigger picture
The market backing this idea is not small: the global AI agents market is projected to grow from $10.9 billion in 2026 to $182.9 billion by 2033 , a 49.6% compound annual growth rate, according to Grand View Research. Meta’s own scramble to secure AI infrastructure, including its record-setting financing package for AI data centres, shows how much capital is chasing every layer of the agent stack right now.
Whether “better environments” genuinely out-competes “bigger models” is still an open question, not a settled argument, and the answer will likely decide which infrastructure companies survive the next funding cycle.
