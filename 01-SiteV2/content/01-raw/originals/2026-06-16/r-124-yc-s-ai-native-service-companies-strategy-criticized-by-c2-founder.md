YCombinator is wrong with one of their latest Requests for Startups. AI-Native Service Companies.
We're doing exactly this at C2. 160 people, profitable, high-growth, 4 continents. Turning it into an AI-native business.
Our moat isn't pure tech but rather tech + domain expertise + years of relationships with tier-1 clients. You can't speed run that with an 8 figure round.
All we needed was product leadership, process redesign and board-level commitment to AI. VC funding doesn't accelerate that. Operational muscle does.
YC wants founders to build new AI service companies from zero. We did it inside an existing one. Yes, that's a private equity play. No, this isn't VC-backable.

The AI startup landscape is seeing a concerning trend where 'Annual Recurring Revenue' (ARR) is being inflated, often by substituting 'Committed ARR' (CARR) for actual revenue.
This practice, sometimes supported by major venture capital firms, misrepresents growth and can mislead investors and the public. CARR includes revenue from contracts not yet fully onboarded or even initiated, and can be significantly higher than true ARR.
Startups are pressured to adopt these metrics to appear competitive, potentially masking underlying issues with product adoption or implementation delays. This trend raises critical questions about transparency and sustainable growth models in the fast-paced AI sector, impacting technology, software, and venture capital valuation strategies.

A startup that answers customer support tickets just raised $950 million. Your "AI for X" seed deck is now competing with that. Good luck.
Sierra's round pushed it past a $15B valuation. It hit roughly $150M ARR in about a quarter. Founders Fund raised a record $6B for late-stage AI. VCs poured $18.8B into AI startups founded since 2025.
Sounds like easy money. It's the opposite.
The capital didn't get more generous. It got more concentrated. The money is flooding into a handful of teams with real traction and brutal technical moats. Everyone else is getting "less patience for sloppy startup logic," as one fund put it. Translation: your narrative had better be tight.
I keep seeing founders read these mega-rounds as proof the AI gold rush is wide open. It's not. It's proof the bar moved to a place most decks can't reach. "We wrap an LLM and add a UI" was a seed story in 2024. In 2026 it's a feature — and probably someone else's.
The opportunity didn't close. It got specific. Vertical depth, regulated industries, real distribution, defensibility you can explain in one sentence.
If you're raising right now: what's the one sentence that makes you not a feature?
