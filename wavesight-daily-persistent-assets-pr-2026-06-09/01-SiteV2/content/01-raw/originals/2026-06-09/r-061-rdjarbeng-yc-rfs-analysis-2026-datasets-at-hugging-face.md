🛒 YC's Shopping List: What the World's Top Accelerator Is Begging Founders to Build (Summer 2026)
A founder's field guide to Y Combinator's Request for Startups — decoded, ranked, mapped to real opportunities, and tracked across 17 years of evolution.
Primary Source: ycombinator.com/rfs — Summer 2026 Edition | Analysis compiled April 2026
TL;DR
Y Combinator's Request for Startups (RFS) is their public wishlist — the ideas they actively want founders to pitch. Think of it as a cheat code: you don't have to guess what YC wants to fund. They tell you.
The Summer 2026 edition marks a decisive shift. Gone are the broad sector labels ("Healthcare," "Defense," "Energy"). In their place: 16 hyper-specific bets — each written by a named YC partner, each reading like a mini-thesis on exactly why now is the moment for this company to exist.
The message: AI has stopped being a feature and started being the foundation. Now wire it into the physical world — silicon, agriculture, space, defense, and the operating system of every company on Earth.

Deep Dive: Every Category Explained
1. 🌾 AI for Low-Pesticide Agriculture
Partner: Garry Tan (CEO) | Domain: AgTech × AI × Biotech × Robotics
The thesis: Modern agriculture is trapped in a doom loop — spray more chemicals → pests adapt → spray even more → margins collapse. AI vision, cheap sensors, precision robotics, and biological alternatives (microbes, peptides, RNA-based solutions) have converged to break this cycle. The company that cuts pesticide use by 90% and increases yields isn't just a good business — it's a generational company.
Key signals Garry highlights:
AI can now identify individual weeds and pests in real time
Cameras and sensors are cheap enough to blanket fields
Robots can treat one plant instead of spraying entire fields
Biology is catching up: engineered plants, microbial solutions, RNA interference
Agriculture is one of the biggest markets in the world
Why now: The convergence of computer vision, cheap hardware, and biological engineering makes precision agriculture viable at scale for the first time.
2. 💼 AI-Native Service Companies
Partner: Gustaf Alströmer | Domain: Enterprise Services × AI
The thesis: The evolution went: Services → SaaS → AI Copilots → AI does the work . Most 2023–2025 startups built copilots (tools that help people). YC now wants companies that skip the tool and just deliver the outcome . Total spend on services dwarfs software spend, and outsourced services are the easiest to replace.
Target verticals YC calls out:
Insurance brokerage
Accounting, tax, and audit
Compliance
Healthcare administration
Why now: AI capabilities crossed the threshold where "replace the service entirely" is more practical than "make the human 20% faster."
3. 🧬 AI Personalized Medicine
Partner: Ankit Gupta | Domain: Healthcare × Genomics × AI Agents
The thesis: Three revolutions are colliding: (1) AI agents can now analyze personalized health data with clinical-grade accuracy, (2) the cost of diagnostics (genome sequencing, biomarkers) is plummeting faster than Moore's Law, and (3) the cost of n-of-1 therapies (mRNA, gene therapies) is dropping while the FDA is becoming more open to letting patients try them.
The result: Truly personalized care delivery — not "here's what works on average" but "here's what works for you , based on your genome, your wearables data, and your medical history."
Why now: Agent harnesses like Claude Code can now analyze multi-modal health data. Genome sequencing costs have dropped below $200. FDA is signaling openness to personalized therapy pathways.
4. 🧠 Company Brain
Partner: Tom Blomfield | Domain: Enterprise Knowledge × AI Agents
The thesis: The biggest blocker to AI automation isn't the models — they're good enough. It's the domain knowledge . Every company's critical know-how is scattered across people's heads, Slack threads, old emails, support tickets, and databases. Humans vaguely remember where it is. AI agents can't operate like that.
What YC wants: A "company brain" — a living map of how a company works (how refunds are handled, how pricing exceptions are decided, how engineers respond to incidents) that AI agents can use as an "executable skills file."
This is NOT: A company-wide search tool or a chatbot over documents.
This IS: The missing layer between raw company data and reliable AI automation.
Why now: AI agents are ready to do real work, but they need structured knowledge to do it safely and consistently. Tom says every company in the world will need this.
5. 🛡️ Counter-Swarm Defense
Partner: Tyler Bosmeny | Domain: Defense × Hardware × AI × Real-time Systems
The thesis: "Last month, a swarm of cheap Iranian drones took out an AWS data center. Nobody stopped them." A Patriot missile costs $3M. An FPV drone costs $500. The cost advantage lies entirely with attackers, and current counter-drone defenses (radars, cameras, jammers, people with binoculars) won't work against coordinated swarms of hundreds.
What YC wants — the "counter-swarm stack":
High-capacity interceptors (one platform neutralizes 50 drones, not one)
Sensor fusion software (single real-time picture from all sensors)
Non-kinetic defenses (aerosols that foul rotors, streamers that entangle swarms)
Attacks on the autonomy stack itself (beyond radio jamming)
The killer quote: "Drone defense is looking less like operating a weapon and more like running a real-time distributed system. The winning companies will look more like Cloudflare than Raytheon."
Why now: Drone swarms have gone from theoretical to battlefield-proven. The Ukraine conflict, Houthi attacks, and the AWS incident have made this existential.
6. 💻 Dynamic Software Interfaces
Partner: Ankit Gupta | Domain: Developer Tools × AI × UX Design
The thesis: Before AI, all users got the same interface with minor customizations. But the way you use email is fundamentally different from how a college student uses email — yet every email client looks the same. Coding agents are now good enough that users can become their own "forward-deployed engineers," radically customizing the software they consume.
What YC envisions:
Software companies ship shared primitives, not finished UIs
Users' coding agents customize the final interface
Your email might look like a task list; a student's looks like an events calendar
The entire software delivery stack gets rethought: source code vs. binaries, frontend vs. middleware modification
Why now: AI coding agents (Cursor, Claude Code, etc.) have made it possible for non-developers to meaningfully modify software interfaces.
7. 🚀 Electronics in Space
Partner: Philip Johnston | Domain: Aerospace × Semiconductor Design
The thesis: Reusable rockets (SpaceX, Stoke Space) are about to massively increase humanity's capacity to put things in space. That means enormous demand for space-grade compute — specifically inference chips optimized for mass, thermal management, and radiation hardness.
Target founder profile: "If you are working at SpaceX or NVIDIA and you've been spending time doing chip design, then Y Combinator would love to hear from you."
Why now: Launch costs are dropping exponentially. The bottleneck shifts from "getting to space" to "running workloads in space."
8. ⚙️ Hardware Supply Chain
Partner: Nicolas Dessaigne | Domain: Manufacturing × Logistics × Hardware
The thesis: Building hardware in the US is still far too slow compared to China. In Shenzhen, a team goes from design to new physical part in a day . In the US, that same loop takes weeks . That gap compounds with every iteration.
What YC wants:
Startups that produce parts dramatically faster
Tools enabling rapid hardware iteration
Tight integration of design, manufacturing, and logistics
YC companies already in this space: Hlabs (W26, building actuators for robots), Prototyping.io (P26, mechanical parts in days).
Why now: YC is funding more hardware companies than ever (robots, medical devices, space). Those companies all need a faster physical supply chain.
9. 🌙 Industrial Capabilities in Space
Partner: Adi Oltean | Domain: Aerospace × Manufacturing × Materials Science
The thesis: Develop industrial capabilities on the moon and in space — extracting raw materials (silicon, aluminum, iron, titanium) through electrolysis and 3D printing complex structures from molten regolith. Lunar manufacturing has structural advantages: no atmosphere means no support structures needed, lower gravity reduces energy costs.
Why now: Artemis program, commercial lunar landers (Intuitive Machines, Astrobotic), and reusable rockets are making lunar operations increasingly feasible.
10. 🔧 Inference Chips for Agent Workflows
Partner: Diana Hu | Domain: Semiconductor Design × AI Infrastructure
The thesis: Current AI chips are designed for "prompt in, response out." But agents don't work that way — they loop, call tools, branch, backtrack, and hold context across dozens of steps. Current GPUs hit only 30–40% utilization on agentic workloads because the work is bursty.
What's needed:
Fast context switching between models
Native speculative decoding
Memory built for KV caches that persist across entire execution graphs
Compilers that understand the agent loop (Groq's real insight was the compiler, not the chip)
Context: NVIDIA acquired Groq for $20B. Google built TPU v7 for inference. But nobody's designing for the agent loop itself .
Why now: Agentic AI has moved from research demo to production workload. The hardware isn't keeping up.
11. ⚔️ SaaS Challengers
Partner: Jared Friedman | Domain: Enterprise Software × AI
The thesis: "Everyone's talking about how AI coding means the end of SaaS. Investors have wiped trillions off software market caps. Well, that might be bad news for incumbents, but it's good news for startups ."
AI has collapsed the cost of producing software by 10–100×. The moat that protected legacy SaaS (millions of lines of code built over decades) is gone.
Attack vectors Jared suggests:
Clone an existing product, sell it for 1/10th the price
Build AI-native from the ground up (not a chatbot bolted onto a 2010 UI)
Bundle 10 point solutions into one suite
Open-source a $50K/seat product, monetize through services
His challenge: "Don't go after project management tools. Go after the products that seem invulnerable: chip design software, ERPs, industrial control systems, supply chain management. The giant, 10-million-line codebases that have been untouchable for decades."
Why now: AI coding has made the "million lines of code" moat obsolete. The incumbents are vulnerable for the first time in decades.
12. 🤖 Software for Agents
Partner: Aaron Epstein | Domain: Developer Tools × AI Infrastructure
The thesis: "The next trillion users on the internet won't be people — they'll be AI agents." Right now, agents interact with software designed for humans (clicking buttons in browsers), which is slow, inconsistent, and brittle.
What agents actually need:
Machine-readable interfaces (APIs, MCPs, CLIs) instead of visual ones (forms, buttons)
Thorough documentation for programmatic discovery
The ability to sign up for and use tools without human-in-the-loop
The framing: "While everyone else is building agents, the biggest opportunity might be building the software those agents depend on."
Why now: Agent usage has crossed the threshold where human-designed UIs are the bottleneck, not the models.
13. 🏢 Startups Selling to Huge Companies
Partners: Harshita Arora & Brad Flora | Domain: Enterprise Sales × AI
The thesis: PG's classic advice was "sell to other startups." But AI has unlocked a new playbook: sell directly to Fortune 100 companies from Day 1.
Three things changed:
Buyers are awake. F100 leaders are actively looking for AI teams. YC companies are landing multi-million dollar deals during the batch .
Small teams ship fast. A 2–3 person team can now ship something a Fortune 10 finds useful before the ink is dry on incorporation docs.
Leaders understand urgency. They know what to build internally, what to outsource, and what happens if they don't adapt.
Why now: Enterprise buyers are proactively seeking startups for the first time. The "stealth for 3 years until feature parity" meme is dead.
14. 🔗 Supply Chain 2.0 for Semiconductors
Partner: Diana Hu | Domain: Semiconductors × Supply Chain × Software
The thesis: A single advanced AI chip crosses ~1,400 process steps, a dozen countries, and takes five months to build. This supply chain is managed with spreadsheets, SAP, and phone calls.
The pain:
In 2021, a $300 chip held up $50,000 cars; $210B in vehicles didn't get built
TSMC's advanced packaging is the single biggest bottleneck in AI compute
NVIDIA has locked up 60%+ of it
HBM memory is booked through 2026
Export controls change quarterly
CHIPS Act fabs in Arizona, Texas, Ohio, New York each need supply chains built from scratch
Why now: "You need to understand wafer allocation and packaging constraints at a deep level to build this, which is exactly why it's a startup opportunity and not a feature inside SAP."
15. 🏭 The AI Operating System for Companies
Partner: Diana Hu | Domain: Enterprise AI × Operations
The thesis: The best AI-native companies have made their entire company queryable — every meeting recorded, every ticket tracked, every customer interaction captured, all legible to an intelligence layer that learns from it. This turns a company from an open loop (make a decision, check results weeks later) into a closed loop (system monitors, compares to targets, adjusts automatically).
The gap: Building this today requires brutal integration work — stitching together Slack, Linear, GitHub, Notion, call recordings, and a dozen other tools with custom glue code. There's no product that connects all this context into a single intelligence layer.
Why now: The tools exist (transcription, LLMs, embeddings), but nobody has built the connective layer that makes a company legible to AI by default.

AI vision, cheap sensors, precision robotics, and biological alternatives (microbes, peptides, RNA-based solutions) have converged to break this cycle. The company that cuts pesticide use by 90% and increases yields isn't just a good business — it's a generational company.
Key signals Garry highlights:
AI can now identify individual weeds and pests in real time
Cameras and sensors are cheap enough to blanket fields
Robots can treat one plant instead of spraying entire fields
Biology is catching up: engineered plants, microbial solutions, RNA interference
Agriculture is one of the biggest markets in the world
Why now: The convergence of computer vision, cheap hardware, and biological engineering makes precision agriculture viable at scale for the first time.
2. 💼 AI-Native Service Companies
Partner: Gustaf Alströmer | Domain: Enterprise Services × AI
The thesis: The evolution went: Services → SaaS → AI Copilots → AI does the work . Most 2023–2025 startups built copilots (tools that help people). YC now wants companies that skip the tool and just deliver the outcome . Total spend on services dwarfs software spend, and outsourced services are the easiest to replace.
Target verticals YC calls out:
Insurance brokerage
Accounting, tax, and audit
Compliance
Healthcare administration
Why now: AI capabilities crossed the threshold where "replace the service entirely" is more practical than "make the human 20% faster."
3. 🧬 AI Personalized Medicine
Partner: Ankit Gupta | Domain: Healthcare × Genomics × AI Agents
The thesis: Three revolutions are colliding: (1) AI agents can now analyze personalized health data with clinical-grade accuracy, (2) the cost of diagnostics (genome sequencing, biomarkers) is plummeting faster than Moore's Law, and (3) the cost of n-of-1 therapies (mRNA, gene therapies) is dropping while the FDA is becoming more open to letting patients try them.
The result: Truly personalized care delivery — not "here's what works on average" but "here's what works for you , based on your genome, your wearables data, and your medical history."
Why now: Agent harnesses like Claude Code can now analyze multi-modal health data. Genome sequencing costs have dropped below $200. FDA is signaling openness to personalized therapy pathways.
4. 🧠 Company Brain
Partner: Tom Blomfield | Domain: Enterprise Knowledge × AI Agents
The thesis: The biggest blocker to AI automation isn't the models — they're good enough. It's the domain knowledge . Every company's critical know-how is scattered across people's heads, Slack threads, old emails, support tickets, and databases. Humans vaguely remember where it is. AI agents can't operate like that.
What YC wants: A "company brain" — a living map of how a company works (how refunds are handled, how pricing exceptions are decided, how engineers respond to incidents) that AI agents can use as an "executable skills file."
This is NOT: A company-wide search tool or a chatbot over documents.
This IS: The missing layer between raw company data and reliable AI automation.
Why now: AI agents are ready to do real work, but they need structured knowledge to do it safely and consistently. Tom says every company in the world will need this.
5. 🛡️ Counter-Swarm Defense
Partner: Tyler Bosmeny | Domain: Defense × Hardware × AI × Real-time Systems
The thesis: "Last month, a swarm of cheap Iranian drones took out an AWS data center. Nobody stopped them." A Patriot missile costs $3M. An FPV drone costs $500. The cost advantage lies entirely with attackers, and current counter-drone defenses (radars, cameras, jammers, people with binoculars) won't work against coordinated swarms of hundreds.
What YC wants — the "counter-swarm stack":
High-capacity interceptors (one platform neutralizes 50 drones, not one)
Sensor fusion software (single real-time picture from all sensors)
Non-kinetic defenses (aerosols that foul rotors, streamers that entangle swarms)
Attacks on the autonomy stack itself (beyond radio jamming)
The killer quote: "Drone defense is looking less like operating a weapon and more like running a real-time distributed system. The winning companies will look more like Cloudflare than Raytheon."
Why now: Drone swarms have gone from theoretical to battlefield-proven. The Ukraine conflict, Houthi attacks, and the AWS incident have made this existential.
6. 💻 Dynamic Software Interfaces
Partner: Ankit Gupta | Domain: Developer Tools × AI × UX Design
The thesis: Before AI, all users got the same interface with minor customizations. But the way you use email is fundamentally different from how a college student uses email — yet every email client looks the same. Coding agents are now good enough that users can become their own "forward-deployed engineers," radically customizing the software they consume.
What YC envisions:
Software companies ship shared primitives, not finished UIs
Users' coding agents customize the final interface
Your email might look like a task list; a student's looks like an events calendar
The entire software delivery stack gets rethought: source code vs. binaries, frontend vs. middleware modification
Why now: AI coding agents (Cursor, Claude Code, etc.) have made it possible for non-developers to meaningfully modify software interfaces.
7. 🚀 Electronics in Space
Partner: Philip Johnston | Domain: Aerospace × Semiconductor Design
The thesis: Reusable rockets (SpaceX, Stoke Space) are about to massively increase humanity's capacity to put things in space. That means enormous demand for space-grade compute — specifically inference chips optimized for mass, thermal management, and radiation hardness.
Target founder profile: "If you are working at SpaceX or NVIDIA and you've been spending time doing chip design, then Y Combinator would love to hear from you."
Why now: Launch costs are dropping exponentially. The bottleneck shifts from "getting to space" to "running workloads in space."
8. ⚙️ Hardware Supply Chain
Partner: Nicolas Dessaigne | Domain: Manufacturing × Logistics × Hardware
The thesis: Building hardware in the US is still far too slow compared to China. In Shenzhen, a team goes from design to new physical part in a day . In the US, that same loop takes weeks . That gap compounds with every iteration.
What YC wants:
Startups that produce parts dramatically faster
Tools enabling rapid hardware iteration
Tight integration of design, manufacturing, and logistics
YC companies already in this space: Hlabs (W26, building actuators for robots), Prototyping.io (P26, mechanical parts in days).
Why now: YC is funding more hardware companies than ever (robots, medical devices, space). Those companies all need a faster physical supply chain.
9. 🌙 Industrial Capabilities in Space
Partner: Adi Oltean | Domain: Aerospace × Manufacturing × Materials Science
The thesis: Develop industrial capabilities on the moon and in space — extracting raw materials (silicon, aluminum, iron, titanium) through electrolysis and 3D printing complex structures from molten regolith. Lunar manufacturing has structural advantages: no atmosphere means no support structures needed, lower gravity reduces energy costs.
Why now: Artemis program, commercial lunar landers (Intuitive Machines, Astrobotic), and reusable rockets are making lunar operations increasingly feasible.
10. 🔧 Inference Chips for Agent Workflows
Partner: Diana Hu | Domain: Semiconductor Design × AI Infrastructure
The thesis: Current AI chips are designed for "prompt in, response out." But agents don't work that way — they loop, call tools, branch, backtrack, and hold context across dozens of steps. Current GPUs hit only 30–40% utilization on agentic workloads because the work is bursty.
What's needed:
Fast context switching between models
Native speculative decoding
Memory built for KV caches that persist across entire execution graphs
Compilers that understand the agent loop (Groq's real insight was the compiler, not the chip)
Context: NVIDIA acquired Groq for $20B. Google built TPU v7 for inference. But nobody's designing for the agent loop itself .
Why now: Agentic AI has moved from research demo to production workload. The hardware isn't keeping up.
11. ⚔️ SaaS Challengers
Partner: Jared Friedman | Domain: Enterprise Software × AI
The thesis: "Everyone's talking about how AI coding means the end of SaaS. Investors have wiped trillions off software market caps. Well, that might be bad news for incumbents, but it's good news for startups ."
AI has collapsed the cost of producing software by 10–100×. The moat that protected legacy SaaS (millions of lines of code built over decades) is gone.
Attack vectors Jared suggests:
Clone an existing product, sell it for 1/10th the price
Build AI-native from the ground up (not a chatbot bolted onto a 2010 UI)
Bundle 10 point solutions into one suite
Open-source a $50K/seat product, monetize through services
His challenge: "Don't go after project management tools. Go after the products that seem invulnerable: chip design software, ERPs, industrial control systems, supply chain management. The giant, 10-million-line codebases that have been untouchable for decades."
Why now: AI coding has made the "million lines of code" moat obsolete. The incumbents are vulnerable for the first time in decades.
12. 🤖 Software for Agents
Partner: Aaron Epstein | Domain: Developer Tools × AI Infrastructure
The thesis: "The next trillion users on the internet won't be people — they'll be AI agents." Right now, agents interact with software designed for humans (clicking buttons in browsers), which is slow, inconsistent, and brittle.
What agents actually need:
Machine-readable interfaces (APIs, MCPs, CLIs) instead of visual ones (forms, buttons)
Thorough documentation for programmatic discovery
The ability to sign up for and use tools without human-in-the-loop
The framing: "While everyone else is building agents, the biggest opportunity might be building the software those agents depend on."
Why now: Agent usage has crossed the threshold where human-designed UIs are the bottleneck, not the models.
13. 🏢 Startups Selling to Huge Companies
Partners: Harshita Arora & Brad Flora | Domain: Enterprise Sales × AI
The thesis: PG's classic advice was "sell to other startups." But AI has unlocked a new playbook: sell directly to Fortune 100 companies from Day 1.
Three things changed:
Buyers are awake. F100 leaders are actively looking for AI teams. YC companies are landing multi-million dollar deals during the batch .
Small teams ship fast. A 2–3 person team can now ship something a Fortune 10 finds useful before the ink is dry on incorporation docs.
Leaders understand urgency. They know what to build internally, what to outsource, and what happens if they don't adapt.
Why now: Enterprise buyers are proactively seeking startups for the first time. The "stealth for 3 years until feature parity" meme is dead.
14. 🔗 Supply Chain 2.0 for Semiconductors
Partner: Diana Hu | Domain: Semiconductors × Supply Chain × Software
The thesis: A single advanced AI chip crosses ~1,400 process steps, a dozen countries, and takes five months to build. This supply chain is managed with spreadsheets, SAP, and phone calls.
The pain:
In 2021, a $300 chip held up $50,000 cars; $210B in vehicles didn't get built
TSMC's advanced packaging is the single biggest bottleneck in AI compute
NVIDIA has locked up 60%+ of it
HBM memory is booked through 2026
Export controls change quarterly
CHIPS Act fabs in Arizona, Texas, Ohio, New York each need supply chains built from scratch
Why now: "You need to understand wafer allocation and packaging constraints at a deep level to build this, which is exactly why it's a startup opportunity and not a feature inside SAP."
15. 🏭 The AI Operating System for Companies
Partner: Diana Hu | Domain: Enterprise AI × Operations
The thesis: The best AI-native companies have made their entire company queryable — every meeting recorded, every ticket tracked, every customer interaction captured, all legible to an intelligence layer that learns from it. This turns a company from an open loop (make a decision, check results weeks later) into a closed loop (system monitors, compares to targets, adjusts automatically).
The gap: Building this today requires brutal integration work — stitching together Slack, Linear, GitHub, Notion, call recordings, and a dozen other tools with custom glue code. There's no product that connects all this context into a single intelligence layer.
Why now: The tools exist (transcription, LLMs, embeddings), but nobody has built the connective layer that makes a company legible to AI by default.

Bonus: Ideas YC Didn't List But Should Have
Based on gaps in the S26 RFS, dropped themes that still matter, and emerging trends:
1. ⚡ AI for Energy / Grid Infrastructure
The most glaring omission. Energy was on the RFS from Day 1 (2014) through S24, then vanished. Meanwhile AI data centers are creating the biggest energy demand surge in a generation. Grid management, energy storage, nuclear software, and permitting automation are all massive opportunities that YC inexplicably stopped asking for.
2. 🌍 Emerging Markets / Global South
Five billion underserved people disappeared from the RFS in 2024. AI makes it economically viable to serve low-ARPU markets. Stablecoin-based banking for the unbanked, AI healthcare for low-resource settings, mobile-first tools for Africa and Southeast Asia — all still enormous opportunities.
3. 🎓 Education
After returning briefly in Summer 2025 ("AI Personal Tutor for Everyone"), education is missing again from S26. Bloom's 2-sigma problem (1:1 tutoring is 2 standard deviations better than classroom instruction) is still unsolved at scale. AI tutors are the obvious fix.
4. 🧬 Longevity & Anti-Aging
Present from 2018–2022, now absent. Sam Altman personally invested $180M in Retro Biosciences. Altos Labs raised $3B. The science is accelerating. YC dropping this from the RFS seems like an oversight, not a signal.
5. 🧑‍⚖️ AI for Legal Systems
Courts are backed up by years. AI can automate case management, legal research, plea bargaining analytics, and access-to-justice tools. A natural blend of the "AI-Native Services" and "SaaS Challengers" categories.
6. 🎮 AI-Generated Interactive Entertainment
Interactive films, personalized stories, AI game masters, procedurally generated worlds. Entertainment hasn't been meaningfully disrupted by AI yet. Huge consumer market.
7. 🔐 Post-Quantum Cryptography
Quantum computers will break current encryption. Every government, bank, and tech company needs to migrate. The window is closing and the market is mandatory — not optional.
8. 🌊 Ocean Technology
95% of the ocean floor is unmapped. Autonomous underwater vehicles, aquaculture tech, deep-sea mining, ocean health monitoring. Combines the "space-like" ambition YC loves with near-term commercial applications.
9. 🔄 AI for Circular Economy
Waste tracking, recycling optimization, product lifecycle management. The $4.5T circular economy is almost entirely un-digitized. Natural SaaS Challenger territory.
10. 🏘️ Construction & Housing
Was trending in earlier analyses, but absent from S26. The housing crisis hasn't gotten better. AI for construction project management, permitting automation, prefab housing — all still underserved.
Final Thoughts: How to Actually Use This List
✅ Do This
Read the named partner's conviction, not just the category title. Tyler Bosmeny didn't just say "defense" — he said the winning companies look like Cloudflare, not Raytheon. Diana Hu didn't just say "chips" — she said the compiler is more important than the silicon. These specifics are the signal.
Pick the intersection. The most defensible startups combine 2–3 S26 categories (see Interdisciplinary Sweet Spots). Counter-Swarm × Inference Chips × Agent Software is harder for anyone to replicate than any single category.
Start with what's dropped but still matters. Energy, education, emerging markets, and longevity fell off the RFS but the problems didn't go away. Lower competition + real demand = opportunity.
Use the difficulty ranking to match your situation. First-time founder with $0? Start with Company Brain or SaaS Challengers. SpaceX chip designer with $50M in VC conversations? Go build space inference silicon.
Notice the "Services → SaaS → Copilots → AI does the work" arc. Gustaf Alströmer is telling you: don't build another copilot. Replace the service entirely. Price it as a fraction of the human cost.
❌ Don't Do This
Don't build another ChatGPT wrapper. The S26 RFS has zero categories that can be satisfied by wrapping an LLM with a prompt.
Don't ignore the hardware shift. ~50% of S26 categories involve atoms (chips, space, agriculture, defense hardware, supply chains). This is the biggest physical-world emphasis in RFS history.
Don't be a tourist. YC can tell when you picked "counter-swarm defense" because it sounded cool vs. because you spent 10 years in the military thinking about this exact problem.
Don't start with an easy SaaS Challenger target. Jared Friedman explicitly says: "Don't go after project management tools. Go after chip design software, ERPs, industrial control systems." The easy targets are crowded.
Don't confuse "hard to build" with "bad opportunity." Moon manufacturing (literally) is the hardest category here. It's also where SpaceX-level outcomes hide.
📊 The Meta-Pattern
The S26 RFS tells a story in three sentences:
AI is no longer a feature. It's the foundation.
The next frontier is physical: silicon, space, agriculture, defense.
Don't build tools. Build the thing. Do the work. Replace the service.
The companies building "AI copilots" were the 2023–2025 vintage. The 2026 vintage either replaces the human entirely (AI-Native Services, Company Brain, AI OS) or builds something physical that couldn't exist before (Inference Chips, Space Electronics, Counter-Swarm, AgTech).
Choose accordingly.
